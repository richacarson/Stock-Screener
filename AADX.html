Company Profile
Industrials ·Aerospace & Defense ·NYSE ·United States
Applied Aerospace & Defense, Inc. designs, engineers, and manufactures integrated aerospace and defense subsystems for space and defense applications. Products include propellant tanks, engine nozzles, satellite assemblies, wings and control surfaces, radar and communications antennas, missile bodies, directed energy systems, and Spinnaker de-orbit systems. Headquartered in Huntsville, Alabama, the company IPO'd in June 2026 at $20/share, raising $650 million.

Investment Thesis
Applied Aerospace & Defense is a pure-play defense subsystems manufacturer riding a generational demand cycle in space proliferation, hypersonics, and directed energy weapons. Revenue grew 51% in FY2025 to $604M, Q1 2026 grew 21% YoY to $134.4M, and contract backlog surged 22% to $1.06B in a single quarter [1]. CEO Ferguson's 'iPhone model' thesis — that defense technology is shifting from multi-decade programs to rapid refresh cycles — creates structural demand growth for AADX's mission-critical subsystems (propellant tanks, satellite assemblies, missile bodies) [2]. The $650M IPO raised capital for deleveraging, and the company generates $99M in adjusted operating income, suggesting underlying profitability beneath GAAP losses. Physical manufacturing of flight-rated hardware with defense security clearances is maximally immune to AI disruption, while AI-driven satellite and autonomous combat demand creates an incremental tailwind.
The bear case is dominated by extreme leverage, GAAP losses, and IPO-related uncertainty. D/E of 445% is five times above the scoring floor, reflecting aggressive acquisition debt that will require years of cash generation or asset sales to reduce [4]. The company reported a $17M GAAP net loss in FY2025 and $15.1M loss in Q1 2026 despite $604M in revenue — at -4.8% profit margin, the business is not yet generating positive bottom-line earnings [1]. The stock has declined from its $20 IPO price to $18.82, suggesting early investor skepticism. Q1 2026 adjusted EBITDA margin fell to 19.8% from 22.8%, indicating margin pressure even on an adjusted basis [1]. The PE-backed rollup strategy carries integration risk across multiple acquired businesses, and private equity sponsors will eventually seek exits that could create selling pressure. Defense spending is politically subject to budget debates, and any shift in priorities away from space/hypersonics could compress the backlog pipeline.
