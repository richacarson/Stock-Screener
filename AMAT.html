Company Profile
Technology ·Semiconductor Equipment & Materials ·NASDAQ ·United States
Applied Materials, Inc. is the world's largest semiconductor equipment company, providing manufacturing equipment, services, and software to the global semiconductor and display industries. Core products include chemical vapor deposition, physical vapor deposition, etch, chemical mechanical planarization, metrology, and inspection systems. The company also provides advanced packaging solutions for high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and AI chip interconnects. Founded in 1967, headquartered in Santa Clara, California.

Investment Thesis
Applied Materials is the world's largest semiconductor equipment company and a direct beneficiary of the AI infrastructure buildout, with its deposition, etch, metrology, and advanced packaging tools required at every major fabrication node [1][2]. Q2 FY2026 delivered record revenue of $7.91B (up 11% YoY) with GAAP EPS surging 33% to $3.51, and Q3 guidance of $8.95B implies accelerating momentum as AI-driven demand for leading-edge logic and HBM capacity translates into equipment orders [1]. Advanced packaging revenue grew 90% YoY with 80% of backlog tied to HBM, and management expects semiconductor equipment business to grow >30% and packaging >50% in calendar 2026 [1]. The company's 50% non-GAAP gross margin, 32% operating margin, and 39.7% ROE demonstrate exceptional profitability for a capital equipment manufacturer, supported by a clean balance sheet with $8.24B cash against $7.27B debt [1][5]. AMAT's position as foundational AI infrastructure — every AI chip passes through its equipment — creates a structural demand tailwind that compounds as AI workloads proliferate across industries.
The primary concern is valuation. At 53.5x forward earnings and a PEG of 2.13, AMAT is priced for sustained >30% earnings growth that embeds optimistic assumptions about the duration and magnitude of the AI capex cycle [5]. The stock has appreciated significantly on AI enthusiasm, and any deceleration in hyperscaler spending or semiconductor inventory correction could trigger meaningful multiple compression. Geopolitical risk is material: 27% of revenue comes from China, and the $252.5M export control settlement in February 2026 exposed compliance failures that could result in further restrictions or reputational damage [1][4]. The $15B backlog declined 5.5% YoY with book-to-bill at approximately 1.0, suggesting order momentum has not yet matched the bullish revenue guidance — the gap between backlog trends and growth projections warrants monitoring [2]. Additionally, the semiconductor equipment industry is cyclical, and AMAT's 5-year revenue CAGR of 7.94% reminds investors that structural AI tailwinds have not fully eliminated cyclical risk [5].
Key Catalysts
  1. Q3 FY2026 guidance of $8.95B revenue (up ~23% YoY) with semi equipment business expected to grow >30% in calendar 2026, driven by leading-edge logic node transitions and AI accelerator capacity expansion at TSMC, Samsung, and Intel Foundry [1]
  2. Advanced packaging revenue growing >50% in 2026 with 80% of backlog tied to HBM for AI — every next-generation GPU and AI accelerator requires AMAT deposition and etch tools for HBM stacking, creating a structural demand multiplier [1][3]
  3. SEMVision H20 AI-powered defect detection driving yield improvements at leading-edge nodes, with potential to expand AMAT's metrology and inspection TAM as AI enables new inspection capabilities that replace manual workflows [3]
Key Risks
  1. Forward P/E of 53.5x and PEG of 2.13 leave minimal margin of safety — any deceleration in AI capex from hyperscalers or a semiconductor equipment spending downcycle could trigger significant multiple compression from the current $508B market cap [5]
  2. China revenue exposure of 27% combined with the $252.5M export control settlement creates ongoing geopolitical risk — further US restrictions on semiconductor equipment exports to China could materially reduce AMAT's addressable market and growth trajectory [1][4]
  3. Backlog of $15.0B declined 5.5% YoY with TTM book-to-bill at approximately 1.0, indicating order flow has not yet accelerated to match the bullish >30% revenue growth guidance — if orders do not inflect, current growth could prove unsustainable beyond the near-term backlog conversion [2]
