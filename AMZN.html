Finite vs Infinite Game — Sinek
Mindset: MIXED Overall: 7 /10
Amazon exhibits strong infinite game characteristics in its strategic thinking — the willingness to invest $200B in 2026 capex despite short-term earnings pressure, the 30-year track record of sacrificing near-term profits for long-term market position, and the relentless expansion into new verticals (cloud, streaming, healthcare, satellites) all reflect an organization playing a longer game than most. However, the finite game is increasingly visible under Jassy: 30,000 corporate layoffs framed as 'culture' rather than strategic necessity, a 5-day RTO mandate that prioritizes control over trust, performance reviews requiring quantifiable accomplishments that reward visible wins over collaborative work, and Glassdoor's declining scores suggest the internal culture is more finite than the external strategy.

Investment Thesis
Amazon enters 2026 as the most strategically positioned company in the AI era. The convergence of three self-reinforcing profit engines — AWS ($107B run rate, 24% growth, 35% margins), advertising ($60B+ ARR, growing faster than Google and Meta), and e-commerce (40%+ US share, 200M+ Prime members) — creates a compounding machine that few companies in history have matched. The $200B capex plan is not speculative overbuilding: AWS backlog reached $244B (+40% YoY), Bedrock customer spend is growing 60% QoQ, and Trainium chips are already a multi-billion dollar revenue stream growing triple digits. Amazon is building custom AI infrastructure at a pace and scale that only Microsoft can rival, while simultaneously reducing dependency on Nvidia through proprietary silicon — a structural cost advantage that will compound over time.
The bear case centers on three risks: (1) the $200B capex plan pressures free cash flow and could prove excessive if AI demand plateaus, (2) the FTC monopoly trial in October 2026 could result in structural remedies that weaken the e-commerce moat, and (3) internal culture is deteriorating under Jassy's aggressive efficiency drive — 30,000 layoffs, 5-day RTO, and declining Glassdoor scores risk talent attrition at precisely the moment Amazon needs its best people to execute the AI transition. The stock's pullback to $205 (below the 50-day SMA of $221) on capex fears creates a compelling entry point at 22x forward earnings for a company growing revenue 14% with accelerating high-margin AI revenue. For IOWN's mandate, Amazon scores exceptionally on innovation, infrastructure, and existential flexibility, but the Trusting Teams deficit and absence of dividend income are notable gaps.
