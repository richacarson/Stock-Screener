Investment Thesis
Arcos Dorados offers compelling value as the world's largest independent McDonald's franchisee, trading at just 9.1x trailing earnings with a PEG of 0.54 and a 3.0% dividend yield [4]. Q4 2025 revenue grew 10.7% YoY to $1.27B, and the company plans to open 105-115 new restaurants in 2026 with $275M-$325M in capex, demonstrating commitment to growth across Latin America's 650M+ consumer market [1]. Digital transformation driving 60% of revenue, loyalty program expansion to 90% of stores, and the McDonald's brand moat provide structural competitive advantages [2]. The thin 4.5% profit margin is characteristic of the franchise QSR model but leaves room for operational leverage as digital efficiencies scale across 2,400+ locations.
The bear case centers on leverage, FX risk, and earnings volatility. D/E of 290.8% with $2.25B in total debt creates meaningful refinancing risk as LatAm interest rates remain elevated [4]. Earnings declined 56.9% YoY in Q4 2025 despite solid revenue growth, exposing the company's vulnerability to currency devaluation (particularly the Argentine peso and Brazilian real) and cost inflation [1]. As a franchisee, Arcos Dorados lacks control over menu pricing, brand strategy, and royalty rates — McDonald's Corp could unilaterally change franchise terms. The $1.94B market cap and $9.23 share price reflect micro-cap status with limited institutional coverage and potential liquidity constraints during market stress. LatAm political instability across multiple markets adds sovereign risk that management cannot hedge away.
Key Catalysts

  2. FX exposure across 20 Latin American currencies — 56.9% earnings decline in Q4 2025 despite 10.7% revenue growth demonstrates translation risk severity [1]
  3. Franchise dependency on McDonald's Corp for brand strategy, menu innovation, and royalty terms limits strategic autonomy [1]
