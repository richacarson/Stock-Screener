Company Profile
Financial Services ·Capital Markets ·NYSE American ·United States
AtlasClear Holdings is a technology-enabled financial services firm operating a platform for trading, clearing, settlement, and banking through its Wilson-Davis & Co. subsidiary. The company targets small and mid-sized financial institutions with fintech products including AtlasFX, SURFACExchange, and BondQuantum. AtlasClear emerged from a SPAC combination in February 2024 and is led by Executive Chairman John Schaible and President Craig Ridenhour.

Investment Thesis
AtlasClear reported Q3 FY2026 revenue of $4.2M (up 65% YoY) with net income of $4.4M, and FY-to-date revenue of $13.5M (up 67%), demonstrating genuine operational momentum [1]. The company restored positive stockholders' equity of $22.3M from a $(6.8M) deficit, eliminated SPAC-era legacy liabilities, and has a $31.3M market cap. Forward P/E of 16.9x and profit margin of 16.8% suggest the underlying clearing business is becoming profitable at scale. The planned Commercial Bancorp acquisition would add banking capabilities, and management expects maximum operating leverage in coming quarters. At this micro-cap valuation, any meaningful scaling of the clearing platform could generate outsized returns from a tiny base.
The bear case is severe for this micro-cap SPAC remnant. The stock trades at $0.21 with $352K daily dollar volume — failing the IOWN liquidity floor and rendering it uninvestable for any institutional portfolio [4]. The $31.3M market cap and sub-$0.25 stock price create NYSE American delisting risk, as the exchange has proposed raising its minimum price threshold [3]. While revenue growth is strong at 65%, the absolute quarterly revenue of $4.2M remains immaterial by institutional standards. No analyst coverage exists for price targets or earnings estimates. The SPAC origin, micro-cap status, and governance complexity (leadership assembled from predecessor entities AtlasBanc, Atlas FinTech, Quantum FinTech) create layers of risk that compound the fundamental illiquidity problem [2]. A single large sell order could move the stock 20%+ given the thin trading volume.
