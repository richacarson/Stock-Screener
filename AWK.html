Company Profile
Utilities ·Utilities - Regulated Water ·NYSE ·United States
American Water Works is the largest regulated water and wastewater utility in the United States, serving approximately 3.6 million customer connections across 14 states. The company operates 80 surface water treatment plants, 520 groundwater treatment plants, 170 wastewater treatment plants, and 55,000 miles of transmission and distribution infrastructure. Founded in 1886.

Investment Thesis
American Water Works is the gold standard of regulated water utilities — the largest in the US with 3.6M customer connections, a 140-year operating history, and the most defensible business model in the IOWN universe [1]. FY2026 EPS guidance of $6.02-$6.12 reflects 8% growth with long-term 7-9% EPS and DPS growth targets anchored by $19-20B in 2026-2030 capital investment and regulated rate recovery mechanisms [1]. The pending Essential Utilities merger ($63B combined EV, Q1 2027 close, 99% shareholder approval) will create a 4.7M connection utility across 17 states, accretive in year one [1]. The 2.51% dividend yield with 18 consecutive years of growth at 6.5% CAGR provides reliable income. At 20.1x forward P/E, the valuation is fair for a utility with above-average growth and an impenetrable competitive moat.
The bear case for AWK is primarily about valuation and regulatory risk. D/E of 147% and $16B in total debt reflect the capital-intensive nature of water infrastructure — while this leverage is standard for regulated utilities, rising interest rates increase the cost of capital and could compress returns [5]. ROE of 10.5% is below the 15% target, constrained by the regulatory earnings cap inherent to the utility model. Q1 2026 results missed consensus on both EPS ($1.24 vs. $1.28) and revenue ($1.27B vs. $1.29B), suggesting growth is not automatic even with rate increases [1]. The Essential Utilities merger faces regulatory approval risk across multiple state commissions — any conditions or delays could impact the timeline and accretion assumptions. Negative free cash flow (-$2.34B) reflects heavy capital investment that must be funded through debt and equity issuance, creating dilution risk. Water utility valuations have compressed from pandemic-era premiums, and AWK trades 12% below its 52-week high of $150.51 [5].
