Finite vs Infinite Game — Sinek (25%)
Mindset: FINITE Overall: 4 /10
Boeing's recent history is a cautionary tale of finite game thinking — prioritizing quarterly earnings, stock buybacks, and cost-cutting over safety, quality, and engineering excellence. The 737 MAX crashes, whistleblower retaliation, and governance failures represent a company that lost its Just Cause. New CEO Ortberg offers hope for a reset, but the cultural damage is deep and the turnaround is in its earliest stages.
Just Cause
4
Boeing's original cause — connecting and inspiring the world through aerospace innovation — was once genuinely transformative, validated by decades of pioneering aircraft from the 707 to the 747 to the 787 [2]. However, the McDonnell Douglas merger in 1997 shifted the company's DNA from engineering excellence to financial optimization. The 737 MAX crashes (346 deaths), whistleblower retaliation, and failed FAA audit (33 of 89 tests failed) demonstrate a company that lost its cause [3]. Ortberg's turnaround plan acknowledges this but hasn't yet restored it. Score: 1/3 points.
Trusting Teams
2
Boeing's trust deficit is among the worst in corporate America. Whistleblower John Barnett died by suicide after raising safety concerns; engineer Sam Salehpour testified to Congress about retaliation; quality inspector Santiago Paredes quit after being labeled a 'showstopper' for flagging defects [3]. Former CEO Calhoun admitted Boeing retaliated against whistleblowers. The 2024 machinists' strike and 17,000 layoffs further eroded trust. CEO Ortberg warned the needed culture shift would be 'brutal to leadership,' implicitly acknowledging the broken trust environment [2]. Score: 0/2 points.

Investment Thesis
Boeing's bull case rests on the magnitude of the recovery opportunity. Revenue surged 57% to $89.5B in FY2025 as deliveries doubled to 160 aircraft in Q4 alone, with a record $567B backlog providing decades of revenue visibility [1]. The 737 MAX production ramp from 42 to potentially 53/month by end of 2026, plus MAX 7 and MAX 10 certification, would unlock significant incremental revenue and margin expansion. S&P revised Boeing's outlook to Stable, and CFO Malave expects inventory-driven deliveries to end by 2026, normalizing cash flow [1]. CEO Ortberg brings 35 years of aerospace credibility and is implementing a four-part turnaround (culture, stability, execution, future) that analysts like Aboulafia call the best hope for Boeing in decades [2]. At ~$190, the stock is pricing in significant execution risk, creating upside if the turnaround delivers.
The bear case is that Boeing's problems are structural, not cyclical. The company has not reported a full-year profit since 2018, carries $56.4B in debt with negative shareholder equity, and its forward P/E of 41.3x demands flawless execution to justify [4]. The 777X delay (another $4.9B charge) and inability to hit production targets (20-25 deliveries/month vs. 38 target represents $8.5B in annual missing revenue) show persistent execution failures [1]. The safety crisis remains unresolved — the FAA audit failure (33 of 89 tests), whistleblower deaths, and DOJ fraud case create ongoing legal, regulatory, and reputational risk [3]. The 2024 strike and 17,000 layoffs have depleted institutional knowledge. Supply chain constraints continue to bottleneck production. Boeing faces the impossible task of simultaneously fixing culture, ramping production, certifying new variants, and delevering the balance sheet — any one of which could derail the recovery.
Key Catalysts
  1. 737 MAX 7 and MAX 10 certification expected in 2026, unlocking new revenue streams from the 4,000+ MAX backlog orders [1]
  2. Production rate increase to 47 MAX/month and eventual 53/month target would drive significant revenue and cash flow acceleration [1]
  3. CEO Ortberg's safety and quality turnaround gaining traction — if the FAA lifts remaining restrictions and Boeing demonstrates sustained quality improvement, the stock could rerate significantly [2]
Key Risks
  1. Production execution failure — Boeing's actual delivery rate of 20-25/month vs. 38 target represents $8.5B in annual missing revenue, and further delays could extend the cash burn timeline [1]
  2. DOJ fraud case and regulatory risk — the rejected plea deal remains unresolved, and additional FAA enforcement actions could further restrict production or certification timelines [3]
  3. Balance sheet fragility — $56.4B debt, negative equity, and D/E of 1032% leave no margin for error; another crisis could force a dilutive capital raise [4]
