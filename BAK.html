Company Profile
Basic Materials ·Chemicals ·NYSE ·Brazil
Braskem S.A. is the largest petrochemical company in the Americas and the world's largest producer of biopolymers (green polyethylene from sugarcane). The company manufactures and sells polyethylene, polypropylene, PVC, caustic soda, and other chemicals and petrochemicals primarily in Brazil, with operations also in the United States and Mexico through Braskem Idesa. The company faces significant environmental liabilities from the Alagoas sinkhole disaster.

Investment Thesis
Braskem is the largest petrochemical company in the Americas and the world's largest biopolymer producer, trading at a deeply distressed valuation — $1.5B market cap against ~$14B annual revenue (0.1x revenue) [4]. The petrochemical cycle is at or near trough with Q3 2025 recurring EBITDA of US$150M up 104% sequentially, suggesting early cyclical recovery [5]. New CEO Roberto Prisco Paraiso Ramos (December 2024) is restructuring leadership and optimizing capital allocation with $465M planned investment in 2026 [1][2]. Green PE from sugarcane provides unique differentiation as ESG-driven demand for sustainable plastics grows. The company maintains US$2.3B in available liquidity to weather the downcycle [5]. Any petrochemical upcycle — driven by demand recovery, supply rationalization, or energy cost normalization — would dramatically improve profitability and the equity value from current depressed levels.
The bear case is overwhelming and multi-dimensional. Braskem's financial position is precarious: negative equity of R$-3.2B, gross debt of US$8.4B (14.76x leverage), declining cash from R$15.0B to R$6.7B, and a -14% profit margin with EPS of -$4.96 [5]. The Alagoas sinkhole disaster has already generated R$14B in provisions with potential for additional claims — a Brazilian Senate report was 'highly damning' and seven executives face environmental crime charges [3]. The $957M FCPA settlement for corruption adds governance risk. Revenue has declined -9.9% annually over three years with continued 15.9% YoY declines as the petrochemical downcycle persists [4][5]. Forward P/E is negative (-4.6x), reflecting expected continued losses. The Braskem Idesa joint venture in Mexico adds R$13.5B in additional borrowings to the consolidated balance sheet. With negative equity and 14.76x leverage, any extension of the petrochemical downcycle could threaten the company's ability to service its debt. This is a deeply distressed situation with existential risk to equity holders.
