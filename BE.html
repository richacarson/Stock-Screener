Company Profile

Bloom Energy manufactures and deploys solid oxide fuel cell systems (Bloom Energy Servers) that provide reliable, on-site power generation for enterprises, data centers, and utilities. Founded by former NASA scientist KR Sridhar, the company has installed approximately 1.4 GW across 1,000+ locations in nine countries and is rapidly scaling to meet AI data center power demand.

Investment Thesis
Bloom Energy is the premier enabler of the AI data center power buildout, with a $20B order backlog, $5B Brookfield partnership, $2.65B AEP deal, and contracts with Oracle, Equinix, and CoreWeave [1][2]. Revenue grew 37.3% to $2.02B in 2025, with Q1 2026 beating estimates by 20% at $778M, and 2026 guidance of $3.1-3.3B implies 54-63% growth acceleration. Manufacturing capacity is doubling to 2 GW by end of 2026. CEO Sridhar's 596% stock appreciation over 12 months reflects the market's recognition that Bloom's rapid-deployment fuel cells solve the critical bottleneck — power — constraining AI infrastructure scaling. The fuel-agnostic platform positions Bloom for the eventual hydrogen economy while generating revenue from natural gas today.
The bear case centers on valuation and environmental concerns: at 45.4x forward P/E with negative profitability (-4.4% profit margin) and 377.8% D/E, the stock is priced for flawless execution of an unprecedented manufacturing ramp [1]. Hindenburg Research's greenwashing allegations — that Bloom fuel cells produce more CO2 than grid power in most states where it operates — Amazon's Oregon contract cancellation, and subsidy curtailments in California, New Jersey, and Delaware highlight the tension between Bloom's 'clean energy' branding and its current natural gas fuel source [3]. If natural gas prices spike or regulators tighten emissions standards before hydrogen becomes commercially viable, the value proposition deteriorates. The $20B backlog requires manufacturing capacity that doesn't yet exist, and execution failure on the 2 GW expansion would devastate credibility and revenue guidance.
Key Catalysts
  1. AEP 1 GW fuel cell facility installation beginning in late 2026, representing the single largest fuel cell deployment in history and a $2.65B recognized revenue opportunity [1]
  2. $5B Brookfield partnership deployments ramping across Brookfield's $1T infrastructure portfolio, potentially expanding into a much larger multi-phase relationship [2]
  3. Achieving 2 GW annual manufacturing capacity by end of 2026, removing the production bottleneck and enabling acceleration of the $20B order backlog into recognized revenue [1]
Key Risks
  1. Manufacturing scaling failure — doubling capacity to 2 GW requires supply chain execution, workforce expansion, and quality maintenance that could be disrupted by component shortages or technical issues [1]
  2. Environmental and regulatory backlash — Hindenburg's greenwashing allegations, Amazon's Oregon cancellation, and subsidy curtailments in CA/NJ/DE could accelerate if regulators determine natural gas fuel cells don't qualify for clean energy incentives [3]
  3. Valuation compression — at 45x forward earnings with negative current profitability and 378% D/E, any guidance miss or backlog cancellation could trigger a severe correction from the 596% rally [1]
