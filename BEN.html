Company Profile
Financials ·Asset Management ·NYSE ·United States
Franklin Resources, Inc., operating as Franklin Templeton, is a global investment management firm with $1.68 trillion in assets under management. The company offers mutual funds, ETFs, separately managed accounts, and alternative investments across public and private markets. Founded in 1947 by Rupert Johnson Sr. (named after Benjamin Franklin), headquartered in San Mateo, California. The company has raised its dividend every year since 1981, earning Dividend Aristocrat status.

Investment Thesis
Franklin Templeton is a 79-year-old, family-controlled asset management institution with $1.68T AUM, a Dividend Aristocrat track record (45 consecutive years of increases), and accelerating momentum — Q2 2026 delivered EPS of $0.71 vs. $0.56 expected, revenue of $2.29B beating estimates by 35%, and adjusted operating margins expanding to 27.1% from 23.4% YoY [1]. Long-term net inflows of $16.9B with record gross sales across all asset classes signal broad client demand. At 11.66x forward earnings and 3.97% dividend yield, BEN offers value and income in a market where asset managers trade at a discount to historical multiples. The Johnson family's 42% voting control ensures long-term alignment and protection against activist disruption.
The structural bear case centers on the secular decline of traditional active management — passive funds and ETFs continue capturing market share from active managers, compressing fees industry-wide and threatening Franklin Templeton's revenue model [1]. ROE of 4.46% is anemic for a financial services firm, suggesting the Legg Mason and Putnam integrations have not yet delivered the expected synergies. The payout ratio near 100% means the dividend is consuming virtually all earnings, leaving minimal reinvestment capacity for organic growth or further acquisitions. Revenue growth of 0.9% over 5 years reflects a business that has barely grown organically. The removal of weapons exclusions from ESG funds and shifting CSR priorities may create reputational risks [3]. CEO Johnson's own warning that AI could disrupt the investment management industry acknowledges a risk that Franklin Templeton has not yet fully addressed in its business model.
Key Catalysts
  1. Operating margin expansion toward the projected high-29% range in fiscal Q4 2026, validating the Legg Mason and Putnam integration thesis
  2. Private markets and alternatives AUM growth providing higher-fee revenue diversification away from fee-compressed traditional active management
  3. AI-driven productivity improvements across the firm generating measurable cost savings and enabling competitive fee positioning
Key Risks
  1. Secular shift from active to passive investing continuing to compress fees and drive outflows from traditional mutual fund products
  2. Payout ratio near 100% constraining reinvestment capacity — the dividend may become unsustainable if earnings contract in a market downturn
  3. ROE of 4.46% and 5-year revenue CAGR of 0.9% reflect a mature business that has failed to generate organic growth despite $1.68T in AUM
