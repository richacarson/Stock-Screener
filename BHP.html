Company Profile
Basic Materials ·Other Industrial Metals & Mining ·NYSE ·Australia
BHP Group is the world's largest diversified mining company, operating across Copper, Iron Ore, and Coal segments with operations in Australia, the Americas, and globally. The company mines copper, iron ore, metallurgical and energy coal, nickel, uranium, gold, zinc, and potash, and is strategically pivoting toward future-facing commodities — particularly copper — to capture AI-driven electricity and electrification demand.

Investment Thesis
BHP is the world's largest diversified miner executing a generational pivot toward copper — now 51% of operating earnings — at a time when AI data center construction and global electrification are creating structural copper demand that supply cannot match (150,000-tonne deficit projected for 2026) [1]. H1 FY2026 underlying profit rose 22% to $6.2B, copper EBITDA surged 59% to $8B on 32% higher realized prices, and interim dividend of 73 cents beat consensus by 16% [1]. At 14.2x forward P/E with 3.66% dividend yield, 24.7% ROE, and $19.7B operating cash flow, BHP offers compelling value for the AI infrastructure theme at a fraction of the valuation of semiconductor or cloud plays. The Jansen potash project ($1B annual EBITDA per stage, first production mid-CY2027) provides a diversification catalyst beyond metals [1].
The bear case centers on commodity cyclicality and legal liability. Iron ore prices face secular pressure as global supply grows, and BHP's $19.41/t unit cost rose 7% in H1 — cost inflation could compress margins if prices decline [1]. The Fundão dam disaster liability remains open, with UK claimants seeking £36B ($47B) in damages — the November 2025 liability ruling means only the quantum of damages remains to be determined, and an adverse outcome could dwarf the $32B Brazilian settlement [3]. China's economic trajectory directly impacts iron ore demand, and any slowdown in Chinese steel production would pressure BHP's largest revenue source. Revenue 5yr CAGR of 3.6% reflects commodity price volatility rather than structural growth, and the dividend is not immune to cuts (BHP cut dividends in 2016 during the commodity downturn). Copper price sustainability above $4/lb is not guaranteed if global recession materializes [1][4].
Key Catalysts
  1. Copper production growth to 2.5Mt by mid-2030s (3-4% CAGR) as AI-driven electricity demand creates structural supply deficit of 150,000+ tonnes in 2026 [1]

Key Risks
  1. UK Fundão dam liability — claimants seeking £36B ($47B) after November 2025 liability ruling, damages phase could result in material additional cost beyond $32B Brazilian settlement [3]
  2. Iron ore price risk from growing global supply and China economic deceleration — 7% unit cost increase in H1 FY2026 compresses margins if prices fall [1]
  3. Commodity cyclicality — BHP cut dividends in 2016, and a global recession could repeat this pattern despite current 3.66% yield and 60% payout [1][4]
