Investment Thesis
Brookdale is the largest U.S. senior living operator entering the strongest demographic tailwind in its history — first boomers turning 80 in 2026 with NIC projecting a 100,000-unit demand-supply shortfall by 2027 growing to 400,000 by 2035 [1]. Same-community occupancy of 83.5% (84.5% spot) is climbing toward the mid-80s with 2026 RevPAR growth guided at 8-9%, driving Adjusted EBITDA guidance of $502-$516M (up from $458M in 2025) [1]. New CEO Nick Stengle from Gentiva brings healthcare operational expertise, and the portfolio is entering its 'final meaningful streamlining' at ~517 communities, allowing management to shift focus from asset sales to operational execution. BofA projects 15% annualized EBITDA growth for several years, and the Inspire score of 31 (ALIGNED) reflects positive elder care social impact [1].
The bear case centers on financial fragility and execution risk. Brookdale remains unprofitable on a GAAP basis with an expected net loss of ~$263M in 2025, negative equity, and forward P/E of 547x [1]. Leverage targets of below 6x by 2028 imply current leverage is substantially higher, and 2026-27 debt refinancing creates near-term risk in a higher-rate environment. The stock surged 113% in 2025 — significant recovery may already be priced in at $13.68 [1]. Quality-of-care controversies persist: $29.3M in cumulative regulatory penalties, the California AG's $3.25M settlement for care quality misrepresentation, and the Washington Post investigation into understaffing algorithms damage brand reputation [2][3]. Labor challenges are acute — senior living faces chronic caregiver shortages with high turnover, and the staffing algorithm controversy suggests cost-cutting pressures conflict with care quality requirements. The company has cycled through three CEOs in two years (Baier, Warren interim, Stengle), creating strategic continuity risk.
