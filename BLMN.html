Investment Thesis
Bloomin' Brands trades at a distressed valuation of 6.3x forward earnings, implying significant upside if the turnaround under new CEO Mike Spanos gains traction. The company guides FY2026 EPS of $0.75-$0.90 with U.S. comparable restaurant sales growth of 0.5-2.5%, which if achieved would represent a meaningful improvement from the essentially flat recent performance [1]. The 9.9% dividend yield (now at $0.60 annually after the 2025 cut) provides income while waiting for recovery, and the $276M in operating cash flow demonstrates the business still generates meaningful cash despite thin margins. Outback Steakhouse remains a recognizable national brand with 64,000 employees and approximately 1,460 locations, providing a substantial physical infrastructure base.
The bear case centers on structural decline in casual dining and a dangerously leveraged balance sheet. Debt-to-equity of 599% with $2.0B in debt against a $518M market cap means equity holders are deeply subordinated to creditors [3]. Profit margins of just 0.2% leave zero room for execution error — any downturn in traffic could push the company into losses. Revenue has declined from $4.17B in 2023 to $3.96B in 2025, reflecting the secular headwinds facing sit-down casual dining as consumers shift to fast-casual alternatives and delivery. The restaurant count has been flat for two years, indicating management sees no path to growth through expansion. The dividend cut from $0.96 to $0.60 annually signals financial strain, and even the reduced dividend consumes most of free cash flow ($20M FCF vs. ~$51M in annual dividends at the new rate). A second CEO transition in two years adds execution risk during a critical turnaround period.
Key Catalysts
  1. Successful execution of CEO Mike Spanos' turnaround strategy with FY2026 comparable restaurant sales growth of 0.5-2.5% demonstrating stabilization of the core business [1]
  2. Margin recovery from cost optimization and menu engineering — even a modest improvement from 0.2% to 3-4% profit margins would multiply EPS and support the stock at current valuations
  3. Debt reduction from operating cash flow and proceeds from the Brazil divestiture, reducing the 599% D/E ratio and lowering financial risk
Key Risks
  1. Secular decline in casual dining traffic as consumers shift to fast-casual (Chipotle, Sweetgreen) and delivery-first models that bypass the sit-down restaurant experience entirely [1]
  2. Unsustainable capital structure with $2.0B in debt against $59M in cash — rising interest rates or a recession could trigger covenant violations or force a dilutive equity raise [3]
  3. CEO transition risk — Mike Spanos is only 18 months into the role following Dave Deno's departure, and any further leadership instability could derail turnaround execution during a fragile period

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
7
AI Risk Protection (6/9): Bloomin' Brands operates physical restaurants — customers visit for the dining experience, food quality, and social atmosphere, none of which can be replaced by AI. The core business involves physical-world operations (food preparation, hospitality, real estate management) that are structurally resistant to AI disruption [2]. However, AI-powered automation in kitchen operations, ordering kiosks, and delivery optimization from competitors could pressure margins and labor cost advantages. Casual dining also faces indirect AI disruption through AI-enhanced delivery platforms (DoorDash, Uber Eats) that shift dining occasions away from sit-down restaurants. AI Opportunity (1/1): Bloomin' Brands can deploy AI for demand forecasting, dynamic pricing, personalized marketing through its loyalty program, and kitchen workflow optimization — generating modest operational efficiencies typical of restaurant operators adopting AI tools.
