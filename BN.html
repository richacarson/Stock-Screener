Company Profile
Financial Services ·Asset Management ·NYSE ·Canada
Brookfield Corporation is a global alternative asset manager with approximately $1 trillion in assets under management across infrastructure, renewable power and transition, private equity, real estate, and credit. The company operates and invests in long-life, high-quality assets and businesses in more than 30 countries, focusing on real assets and essential services. Founded in 1997 and headquartered in Toronto, Canada, Brookfield has positioned itself as a leading enabler of the AI-driven infrastructure buildout through data centers, renewable energy platforms, and the Radiant compute services platform. Led by CEO Bruce Flatt (23+ year tenure), the company targets doubling AUM to $1.2 trillion by 2030.

Brookfield under Bruce Flatt's 23-year tenure exemplifies infinite game thinking. The company has consistently invested in long-duration, essential assets — infrastructure, renewables, real estate — that generate value over decades, not quarters. The willingness to transform from a traditional holding company into a $1 trillion alternative asset manager, and then pivot aggressively into AI infrastructure at a $100 billion scale, demonstrates both existential flexibility and courage to lead. Flatt's philosophy of patient capital deployment and operational improvement — 'buy cash flows and proven technologies, but evolve' — aligns fundamentally with Sinek's infinite game principles of playing to keep playing rather than playing to win this quarter.

Investment Thesis
Brookfield Corporation is uniquely positioned at the intersection of two structural mega-trends: the multi-trillion-dollar AI infrastructure buildout and the global energy transition. With $1 trillion in AUM, $188 billion in deployable capital, 2 GW+ of data center capacity, and a $100 billion AI infrastructure investment program backed by NVIDIA, KIA, and QIA, Brookfield is one of the few firms globally that can integrate land, power, connectivity, and compute at hyperscale [1][2][3]. Forward P/E of 7.30 and PEG of 1.27 suggest the market is significantly undervaluing Brookfield's earnings trajectory — distributable earnings of $2.54/share LTM are growing toward forward EPS of $5.87, representing 130%+ earnings growth as AI infrastructure and fund management fee income scale [1][7]. Q1 2026 distributable earnings reached $1.6 billion ($0.66/share) with total distributable earnings of $6.0 billion ($2.54/share) for the last twelve months. The company repurchased over $1 billion of shares year-to-date in 2026, signaling management's conviction in a substantial intrinsic value gap.
The primary risk is Brookfield's complex corporate structure, which obscures true economic earnings behind GAAP reporting. GAAP ROE of 2.5% and profit margin of 1.69% are structurally misleading for an asset manager that earns primarily through management fees, carried interest, and realized gains — but they make the stock screen poorly on quantitative metrics and deter institutional buyers reliant on screens [7]. Debt-to-equity of 161.5% with $267 billion in total debt against $15 billion cash creates leverage risk, though much of this debt sits at the portfolio company level with limited recourse to the parent [7]. Revenue declined from $92.8B (2022) to $75.1B (2025), producing a negative 3-year CAGR of approximately -6.8% — partly due to asset sales, mark-to-market effects, and portfolio recycling inherent to the business model, but nonetheless reflecting GAAP revenue contraction that complicates the growth narrative. Net insider selling ($12.4M by Cockwell, $5M by Lawson) versus minimal insider buying ($125K by Pollock) sends mixed signals at the individual level despite the corporate buyback program [6]. Execution risk on the $100 billion AI infrastructure program is material — deploying capital of that scale requires sustained demand for AI compute over a multi-decade horizon, and any cyclical slowdown in hyperscaler capital expenditure could delay returns.
Key Catalysts
  1. Deployment of the $100 billion AI infrastructure investment program generating fee-related earnings and carried interest as data center and compute assets become operational through 2026-2028, with the BAIIF fund targeting $10 billion in initial equity commitments [2]
  2. Distributable earnings per share growing from $2.54 LTM toward $5.87+ forward estimates, driven by AI infrastructure realizations, organic AUM growth toward $1.2 trillion by 2030, and Wealth Solutions targeting $200 billion in insurance assets [1][5]
  3. Corporate simplification through insurance entity merger, continued $1B+ annual share buybacks, and the narrowing gap between GAAP and distributable earnings as the business model shifts toward higher-margin fee-related and carried interest revenue streams [5]
Key Risks
  1. Complex corporate structure with GAAP metrics (2.5% ROE, 1.69% profit margin, 84x trailing P/E) that severely understate economic earnings, creating persistent valuation confusion and deterring quantitative investors despite 7.3x forward P/E [7]
  2. Total debt of $267 billion across the enterprise creates leverage risk — a sustained interest rate environment above 5% or credit market dislocation could pressure portfolio company refinancing, distribution capacity, and the ability to recycle capital at attractive valuations [7]
  3. AI infrastructure buildout execution risk: deploying $100 billion requires sustained demand for AI compute over a multi-decade horizon — any cyclical slowdown in hyperscaler capital expenditure, regulatory constraints on data center energy consumption, or technology shifts could delay returns and compress fee revenue [2]

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
9
AI Risk Protection (8/9): Brookfield is structurally immune to AI disruption because it IS AI infrastructure. The company's core business involves physical-world operations — building and operating data centers, renewable power plants, transmission networks, toll roads, ports, and real estate — that AI structurally cannot displace [1][2]. AI dramatically increases demand for Brookfield's core assets: every AI model trained requires power, cooling, connectivity, and physical data center space, all of which Brookfield supplies at scale. The $100 billion AI infrastructure investment program with NVIDIA validates that Brookfield is not just resilient to AI but is a primary beneficiary of the AI buildout cycle [2]. CEO Flatt has stated Brookfield focuses on 'real assets where we don't take any technology risk' and that exposure to software companies affected by AI disruption is 'very minimal.' The slight deduction from a perfect 9 reflects that some traditional real estate and private equity portfolio companies face margin pressure as AI automates property management, deal sourcing, and back-office functions. AI Opportunity (1/1): Brookfield generates meaningful and growing AI revenue through multiple channels: 2 GW+ of operational data center capacity, the Radiant compute platform (NVIDIA Cloud Partner) selling GPU infrastructure as a service, the $5B Bloom Energy partnership for data center power, the $20B QIA partnership, and fee-related earnings from the BAIIF fund targeting $10B in initial equity commitments [2][3][4].
