Company Profile
Energy ·Oil & Gas Drilling ·NYSE ·Bermuda
Borr Drilling Limited is an international offshore drilling contractor that owns and operates a fleet of modern jack-up rigs, one of the youngest fleets in the industry. Founded in 2016 and headquartered in Hamilton, Bermuda, the company provides drilling services to oil and gas exploration and production companies worldwide in water depths up to approximately 400 feet, with operations concentrated in the Middle East, Southeast Asia, West Africa, and Mexico. Fleet expanded to approximately 34 rigs following the $360M Noble Corporation acquisition in January 2026.

Investment Thesis
Borr Drilling operates one of the youngest and highest-specification jack-up fleets in the industry (~34 rigs post-Noble acquisition) in a structurally undersupplied market — limited new rig orders since 2015 mean existing modern fleets command premium dayrates [1]. Q1 2026 revenue of $247M and full-year guidance of $1.06B demonstrate steady operational momentum, with Adjusted EBITDA margins near 35% and H1 2026 contract coverage at 80% [1]. The analyst consensus price target of $5.71 implies 35% upside from $4.24, and the five-year revenue trajectory from $245M (2021) to $1.06B (2026E) reflects successful fleet deployment. Saudi Arabia and Mexico show signs of jack-up demand inflection into 2027 [2].
The bear case is dominated by balance sheet risk and earnings deterioration. With $2.31B in debt against $246M cash, D/E of 193%, and an Altman Z-Score of 0.37, Borr faces material financial distress risk in a prolonged dayrate downturn [5]. FY2026 consensus EPS of -$0.11 suggests the company may not cover interest costs, and profit margin of 3.13% means even modest dayrate softness pushes into sustained losses. The stock has declined 30% since March 2026, reflecting market skepticism — 9.57% short interest underscores the bearish thesis [5]. H2 2026 contract coverage of only 48% leaves significant revenue uncertainty. The energy transition poses a secular headwind as ESG-conscious investors limit valuation multiples and tightening regulations could restrict offshore drilling activity over time.
Key Catalysts
  1. Backfilling H2 2026 open capacity at stable or improving dayrates would significantly de-risk revenue guidance and could drive EPS above consensus -$0.11 expectation
  2. Saudi Arabia and Mexico jack-up demand inflection supporting utilization and dayrate increases through 2027, driven by multi-year underinvestment in global offshore production capacity
  3. Deleveraging through free cash flow generation as fleet utilization normalizes, reducing the $2.31B debt burden and improving net margins from the current 3.13% level
Key Risks
  1. Altman Z-Score of 0.37 and $2.31B net debt on a $1.3B market cap create material financial distress risk if dayrates decline or fleet utilization drops below breakeven levels
  2. H2 2026 contract coverage at only 48% leaves approximately half of fleet capacity unbooked, exposing revenue to dayrate and demand volatility in a potentially softening market
  3. Oil price decline below $60/bbl or recession-driven demand destruction could compress jack-up dayrates 20-30%, devastating 3.13% profit margins and ability to service debt obligations
