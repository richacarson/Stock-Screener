Company Profile

Constellation Energy is the largest producer of carbon-free energy in the United States, operating approximately 60 GW of generating capacity (post-Calpine acquisition) consisting of nuclear, wind, solar, natural gas, and hydroelectric assets. The company serves 2.5 million customers including distribution utilities, municipalities, cooperatives, and commercial/industrial clients. Constellation completed its $26.6B acquisition of Calpine in January 2026, creating the nation's largest clean energy portfolio.

Investment Thesis
Constellation Energy is the defining AI-era energy play — the nation's largest carbon-free power producer with ~60 GW of capacity serving the insatiable electricity demand of hyperscaler data centers [1][2]. The $26.6B Calpine acquisition (completed January 2026) is immediately accretive to EPS by 20%+, and 2026 adjusted operating earnings guidance of $11.00–$12.00/share implies 49-63% growth [1]. The 20-year Microsoft PPA for the Crane Clean Energy Center (Three Mile Island restart) establishes Constellation as the preferred nuclear partner for Big Tech. At 19.8x forward P/E with a 20%+ base earnings growth trajectory through 2030, the valuation is reasonable for a utility with growth-stock characteristics. D/E of 63.9% is conservative, operating cash flow of $4.24B funds $3.9B in growth capex, and the dividend has grown 14.7% annually [1][4].
The bear case centers on execution risk and valuation premium. The Calpine integration is enormous — absorbing a $26.6B acquisition while simultaneously managing the $1.6B Three Mile Island restart creates operational complexity [1]. Nuclear regulatory risk is ever-present; NRC delays on the Crane restart (targeted 2028) could push back the earnings contribution. The stock has traded as high as $412.70 and has pulled back 33% to $275, but still trades at 37x trailing earnings — a premium that requires flawless execution to sustain. Profit margin of 9.1% is thin for a company with this valuation premium, and the Calpine natural gas fleet introduces carbon-intensity that may create ESG headwinds. Interest rate sensitivity is high given the capital-intensive nature of the business and $9.5B in debt. Policy risk from changes to IRA nuclear credits or data center permitting could materially impact long-term growth assumptions [4].
