Company Profile
Consumer Defensive ·Education & Training Services ·NYSE ·United States
Chegg, Inc. operates a learning platform providing subscription-based homework help, writing tools, math solvers, and skills-based learning for students and businesses. The company is pivoting from its legacy B2C student homework help business toward B2B enterprise skilling in AI, coding, data analytics, and professional development. Founded in 2005 and headquartered in Santa Clara, California.

Finite vs Infinite Game — Sinek (25%)
Mindset: FINITE Overall: 2 /10
Chegg exhibits almost entirely finite-game characteristics. The company optimized for quarterly subscriber growth during the pandemic boom, failed to invest in defensive moats against AI disruption, and has responded with reactive layoffs and CEO shuffles rather than principled strategic pivots. The desperate pivot to B2B skilling is driven by survival necessity rather than an enduring cause.

The pivot to enterprise skilling is a survival strategy, not a cause. No evidence that purpose has guided strategic decisions over time.

This level of organizational upheaval — cutting nearly two-thirds of the workforce in under a year — is the antithesis of psychological safety and trust. Investment Thesis
Chegg's only investment case rests on the speculative pivot to B2B enterprise skilling — a $70M run-rate business targeting the $40B workforce development market, growing 11% YoY with partnerships from DHL, GI Group, and Wolf University [1]. The aggressive cost restructuring to under $250M in expenses by 2026 could produce a smaller but potentially viable company if the skilling business scales. At $1.09 per share and a $122M market cap, the stock prices in near-complete destruction, offering asymmetric upside if the pivot succeeds.
The bear case is overwhelming. Chegg has lost $14 billion in market capitalization as ChatGPT destroyed its core homework help business — revenue has collapsed 51% from $767M to $377M in three years, and the decline continues with Q2 2026 revenue down 6% YoY even against already depressed comparisons [1][2]. Two rounds of layoffs eliminated nearly two-thirds of the workforce, and returning CEO Dan Rosensweig presided over the initial phase of AI disruption without building meaningful defenses [3][5]. The $18M quarterly Chegg Skilling revenue cannot replace the hundreds of millions lost from homework help, and the B2B skilling market is fiercely competitive with established players like Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, and Pluralsight. The convertible notes on the balance sheet (D/E 61%) create refinancing risk at current share prices, and the stock's marginal liquidity ($1.47M daily trading value) limits institutional participation. This is a company fighting for survival, not growth.
Key Catalysts
  1. Chegg Skilling revenue accelerating beyond 11% YoY growth through enterprise partnerships could prove the viability of the B2B pivot and shift the narrative from decline to transformation
  2. Cost restructuring to under $250M in expenses by 2026 could produce adjusted EBITDA profitability on the smaller revenue base, demonstrating a path to sustainable operations
  3. Legacy homework help subscriber declines stabilizing as the easy AI substitution cycle matures and remaining subscribers prove sticky for Chegg's structured learning content
Key Risks
  1. Continued revenue declines in the legacy homework help business could outpace skilling revenue growth, creating a shrinking total revenue trajectory that exhausts cash reserves
  2. Convertible note refinancing risk at a $1.09 stock price and $122M market cap could force highly dilutive equity raises or debt restructuring
  3. Competition in B2B enterprise skilling from Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, Pluralsight, and Udemy — all better-capitalized and more established — could limit Chegg Skilling's growth potential
