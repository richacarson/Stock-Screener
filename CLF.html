Company Profile
Basic Materials ·Steel ·NYSE ·United States
Cleveland-Cliffs Inc. is the largest flat-rolled steel producer in North America, operating integrated steel mills, iron ore mines, and downstream finishing facilities. Products include hot-rolled, cold-rolled, coated, stainless, and electrical steel products serving automotive, infrastructure, manufacturing, and distributor end markets. Originally an iron ore mining company (Cliffs Natural Resources), it transformed into a vertically integrated steelmaker through acquisitions of AK Steel (2020) and ArcelorMittal USA (2020). Founded in 1847, headquartered in Cleveland, Ohio.

Investment Thesis
Cleveland-Cliffs is a tariff-driven turnaround play trading at 23.6x forward earnings with significant earnings recovery potential. Q1 2026 showed improving momentum — revenue of $4.92B (up 6.3% YoY), narrowed net loss, and HRC prices averaging $980/ton (up 24% YoY) driven by 50% steel tariffs [1]. Management expects sequential quarterly improvement through 2026 with shipment guidance of 16.5-17M net tons. The Palantir AI partnership for steelmaking optimization demonstrates forward-looking operational investment [2]. As the largest flat-rolled steel producer in North America with a vertically integrated iron ore-to-steel model, Cleveland-Cliffs is uniquely positioned to benefit from sustained trade protection and manufacturing reshoring. The PEG ratio of 0.43 reflects the market's expectation of dramatic earnings recovery.
The bear case is that Cleveland-Cliffs is a deeply cyclical, capital-intensive business with 129% D/E that has posted cumulative net losses of $2.2B over FY2024-2025 [5]. Steel tariffs are politically contingent — any policy reversal or trade negotiation could collapse HRC pricing and the earnings recovery thesis. Automotive demand (Cleveland-Cliffs' largest end market) is vulnerable to economic slowdown, and the 600-worker Dearborn layoff in 2025 illustrates this sensitivity [3]. No dividend has been paid since 2020, eliminating income appeal. CEO Goncalves's combative style creates governance risk — the company is highly dependent on one leader whose confrontational approach to analysts, competitors, and regulators may alienate stakeholders. The negative 5-year revenue CAGR of -6.9% and negative profit margin demonstrate that even the largest US steelmaker cannot consistently generate returns through commodity price cycles without tariff support.
Key Catalysts
  1. 50% steel tariffs sustaining HRC prices above $1,000/ton through 2026, driving sequential quarterly earnings improvement and potential return to profitability by H2 2026
  2. Palantir AI partnership delivering quantifiable operational efficiency gains that differentiate Cleveland-Cliffs' cost structure versus domestic and international competitors
  3. Automotive production recovery in H2 2026 increasing shipment volumes toward the upper end of 16.5-17M net ton guidance range
Key Risks
  1. Steel tariff reversal or reduction through trade negotiations would collapse HRC pricing from $980/ton toward pre-tariff levels of ~$700/ton, eliminating the earnings recovery thesis
  2. Debt-to-equity of 129% with cumulative losses of $2.2B over two years creates balance sheet fragility — any extended downturn could trigger credit rating downgrades or covenant issues
  3. CEO concentration risk: Goncalves's combative leadership style and personal domination of strategy creates succession risk and potential for value-destructive confrontations with regulators or partners

AI Risk Protection (8/9): Steel production is a physical-world operation that AI structurally cannot displace — blast furnaces, hot strip mills, iron ore mining, and logistics require massive capital assets and physical processes [1]. Steel demand is tied to automotive production, infrastructure construction, and manufacturing output, none of which AI can substitute. The 50% tariff wall provides additional structural protection for domestic producers. AI actually increases demand for steel indirectly through data center construction and manufacturing reshoring. The Palantir partnership demonstrates that AI is an operational efficiency enabler, not a threat [2]. AI Opportunity (0/1): The Palantir AI partnership was announced in April 2026 and is in early deployment. No AI-related revenue or quantified cost savings have been reported. AI remains a cost optimization tool in early stages, not a revenue contributor.
