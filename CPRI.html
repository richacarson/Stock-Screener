Company Profile
Consumer Cyclical ·Luxury Goods ·NYSE ·United Kingdom
Capri Holdings is a global fashion luxury group that designs, markets, distributes, and retails branded apparel, footwear, and accessories. Following the sale of Versace to Prada for $1.4B in 2025, the company operates two segments: Michael Kors (accessible luxury handbags, apparel, footwear) and Jimmy Choo (luxury shoes, accessories). Products are sold through boutiques, department stores, and e-commerce globally.

Investment Thesis
Capri Holdings has undergone a dramatic restructuring — selling Versace for $1.4B and reducing net debt from $1.6B to $80M [2]. The streamlined two-brand portfolio (Michael Kors and Jimmy Choo) is guided to $3.45-$3.475B in FY2026 revenue with $1.30-$1.40 diluted EPS, and management expects a return to growth in FY2027 [2]. At 10.6x forward P/E, the stock prices in significant pessimism — any improvement in Michael Kors brand momentum or Jimmy Choo margin expansion could drive meaningful upside. Gross margins of 60.8% confirm underlying pricing power, and the clean balance sheet provides optionality for capital returns or strategic investments [2].
The bear case is substantial. Revenue has declined from $5.65B to a guided $3.45B over four fiscal years, representing a fundamental erosion of the Michael Kors brand franchise [1]. The D/E ratio of 1213.9% (driven by negative equity from accumulated impairments and losses) reflects years of value destruction — buying Versace for $2.1B and selling for $1.4B, the failed $8.5B Tapestry merger that consumed management attention for over a year, and a $1.18B net loss in FY2025 [1][2]. Michael Kors faces secular challenges from AI-powered fast fashion competitors (Shein, Temu) compressing the accessible luxury segment, declining foot traffic in department stores, and promotional dependency that has diluted brand perception. Jimmy Choo's 1.8% operating margin suggests the brand remains subscale [2]. CEO Idol's 22-year tenure raises succession risk, and only 44% of Glassdoor reviewers have a positive business outlook [3]. The 'return to growth' thesis requires Michael Kors to reverse multi-year share losses against better-positioned competitors.
Key Catalysts
  1. FY2027 return to revenue growth as Michael Kors stabilizes through improved full-price sell-through rates and reduced promotional dependency [2]
  2. Balance sheet optionality — net debt reduced to $80M enables potential share buybacks, dividend initiation, or strategic acquisitions [2]
  3. Jimmy Choo margin expansion from 1.8% operating margin toward mid-single digits through operational efficiency and brand repositioning [2]
Key Risks
  1. Michael Kors brand erosion — accessible luxury segment under secular pressure from AI-powered fast fashion (Shein, Temu) and shifting consumer preferences away from logo-driven products [2]
  2. Value destruction track record — Versace acquired for $2.1B, sold for $1.4B; failed Tapestry merger consumed 12+ months of management attention; FY2025 net loss of $1.18B [1][2]
  3. CEO succession risk — John Idol has led for 22 years through a period of declining performance, and only 44% of employees have a positive business outlook [3]
