Investment Thesis
Copart is a rare combination of quality and durability — 50% U.S. salvage market share, 33.8% profit margins, near-zero debt, and $5.2B in cash create an almost unassailable competitive position [1][2]. The founder-led, owner-operator culture has compounded shareholder value for decades through disciplined land acquisition and technology investment. Fiscal 2025 delivered 9.7% revenue growth and 13.9% net income growth, and the Q2 FY2026 softness appears volume-related rather than structural [1]. At a forward P/E of 19.7x, Copart trades at a discount to its quality metrics, and the global vehicle auction TAM continues to expand with rising vehicle complexity and insurance write-offs [2].
The primary risk is valuation compression — at 20x trailing earnings for a business that grew revenue -3.6% in Q2 FY2026, investors may question whether the premium multiple is justified during a soft patch [1]. The stock has fallen 48% from its 52-week high of $63.85, suggesting the market is repricing growth expectations. International expansion and dealer services are growth vectors but unproven at scale, and severe weather pattern variability creates inherent revenue volatility. The $1.25B revolving credit facility, while undrawn, signals potential appetite for M&A that could dilute returns [1].
Key Catalysts
  1. Recovery in vehicle volume and revenue growth in H2 fiscal 2026 would confirm the Q2 softness was weather-related and restore growth narrative [1]
  2. International expansion and BlueCar dealer services scaling above 14% growth could provide new revenue streams beyond insurance salvage [2]
  3. Rising EV adoption increases vehicle complexity and total loss rates, expanding the addressable market for Copart's salvage and remarketing services [2]
Key Risks
  1. Revenue decline of 3.6% in Q2 FY2026 may signal more structural headwinds in insurance salvage volumes than management acknowledges [1]
  2. 48% decline from 52-week high reflects market concern that Copart's premium multiple may not be sustainable during slower growth periods [1]
  3. Concentration in insurance industry relationships creates dependency on carrier total loss decision-making and claim processing timelines [2]
