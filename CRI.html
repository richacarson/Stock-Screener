Investment Thesis
Carter's is the #1 branded children's apparel company in North America with 150+ years of brand heritage, generating $2.90B in FY2025 revenue (+7.6% YoY) and trading at an attractive 10.2x forward P/E with 2.74% dividend yield [1][4]. Q4 2025 was particularly strong, with revenue of $925M beating consensus by $13M and EPS of $1.90 topping estimates by $0.20 [1]. The company's omni-channel model spanning 800+ retail stores, ecommerce, and wholesale creates multiple distribution advantages, while the children's apparel category benefits from non-discretionary replacement demand driven by child growth cycles. New CEO Doug Palladini (ex-VF Corp/Vans) brings fresh brand-building expertise that could reinvigorate growth and margin expansion [2].
The bear case is severe: FY2025 adjusted operating margin collapsed from 10.1% to 6.1%, EBITDA declined 32% to $232M, and the company was forced to cut its quarterly dividend 69% from $0.80 to $0.25 to preserve capital [1]. 2026 guidance is concerning — Q1 EPS of $0.02-$0.08 implies near-zero profitability in the first half (down from $0.66 in Q1 2025), with full-year EPS expected to decline low-double-digits to mid-teens due to ~$0.30 higher interest expense and a 22% tax rate [1]. Revenue has been essentially flat over five years (~$3.0B to $2.9B), suggesting the brand is mature and struggling to find organic growth drivers. Tariff headwinds continue to pressure product costs, and the CEO transition from the 30-year veteran Casey to an outsider creates execution risk during a period of financial stress. Employee culture issues (Glassdoor bottom 45%, 29% recommendation rate outside HQ) threaten operational execution and retention [2]. At $1.3B market cap, Carter's must demonstrate that the margin compression is temporary and the dividend cut enables a genuine reinvestment cycle rather than being a precursor to further deterioration.
Key Catalysts
  1. New CEO Doug Palladini's brand-building expertise from VF Corp/Vans could reinvigorate the Carter's and OshKosh brands, driving organic revenue growth above the flat 5-year trajectory [2]
  2. Dividend cut from $0.80 to $0.25/quarter frees ~$80M annually for debt reduction, store investment, and margin-accretive initiatives that could restore operating margins toward historical 10%+ levels [1]
  3. Q4 2025 earnings beat ($1.90 vs. $1.70 est.) and 7.6% revenue growth suggest underlying demand is stronger than headline margin compression indicates [1]
Key Risks
  1. Operating margin collapse from 10.1% to 6.1% and 69% dividend cut signal structural profitability challenges — tariff headwinds of $30-40M, higher interest expense, and cost inflation may keep margins depressed through 2026 [1]
  2. Five-year revenue CAGR of -0.8% reflects a mature, potentially declining brand in a competitive children's apparel market facing share losses to private-label and fast-fashion alternatives [4]
  3. CEO transition risk — new outside hire Doug Palladini must rebuild culture (Glassdoor bottom 45%) and establish strategic direction while simultaneously managing a financial turnaround [2]
