Investment Thesis
Camping World is the dominant U.S. RV retailer at a deeply distressed valuation — $7.16 per share with a $455M market cap against $6.37B in revenue and record 13%+ market share [1]. The company returned to EBITDA growth of 35%+ in 2025 under new CEO Matthew Wagner, with 2026 guidance targeting $275-325M in Adjusted EBITDA [1]. The 4.1 million customers who purchased RVs during the 2020-2022 pandemic boom represent a substantial trade-in demand wave expected over the next several years, creating a natural tailwind for CWH's used vehicle business. At 6.85x forward P/E and 5.5% dividend yield, the stock is priced for terminal decline — any stabilization in profitability represents significant upside to a levered equity stub [5].
The bear case is severe and structural. D/E of 1,097% ($4.08B debt) against a $455M equity market cap creates existential financial risk — the debt burden is 9x the equity value, leaving virtually no margin of safety for equity holders if the RV downcycle deepens [5]. Profit margin of -1.4% and negative FCF of -$318M mean the company is consuming cash while servicing massive debt obligations. The dividend payout ratio of 273% is mathematically unsustainable — CWH is paying dividends it hasn't earned, relying on borrowed capital or asset sales [5]. Revenue has declined from $6.97B (2022 peak) to $6.37B, and the RV industry faces structural headwinds from rising interest rates (RV financing is rate-sensitive), tariff impacts on imported components, and shifting consumer preferences away from big-ticket discretionary purchases. Marcus Lemonis' departure after building the company through debt-fueled acquisitions leaves Matthew Wagner to manage the consequences [1][2].
Key Catalysts
  1. Trade-in demand wave from 4.1 million RVs sold during 2020-2022 boom expected to drive used vehicle volume and F&I revenue over next 2-3 years [1]
  2. New CEO Matthew Wagner's focus on SG&A efficiency and Good Sam acceleration could restore profitability — 2026 Adjusted EBITDA guidance of $275-325M implies meaningful margin recovery [1]
  3. Potential interest rate cuts in 2026-2027 would directly benefit RV financing demand, the largest driver of new unit sales for CWH's core business [1]
Key Risks
  1. Debt-to-equity of 1,097% creates existential risk — a prolonged RV downcycle or rising interest rates could make the debt load unserviceable, threatening equity holders with severe dilution or restructuring [5]
  2. Dividend sustainability in question with 273% payout ratio and negative $318M FCF — further cuts or elimination would remove the primary reason income investors hold CWH [5]
  3. RV industry tariff exposure from imported components and chassis could compress margins further, while consumer discretionary spending weakness extends the post-pandemic normalization beyond 2026 [1]
