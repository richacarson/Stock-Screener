Company Profile

CXApp Inc. provides enterprise workplace experience solutions powered by artificial intelligence. The CXAI Platform delivers smart workplace tools including indoor mapping, desk and meeting room reservations, analytics, occupancy management, and AI-based workflow automation for Fortune 500 and Fortune 1000 clients. The company's CXAI-VU generative AI analytics platform provides actionable insights into workplace utilization and employee engagement. Formerly known as KINS Technology Group Inc., renamed in March 2023. Headquartered in Palo Alto, California.

Investment Thesis
CXApp offers a speculative option on the agentic AI workplace market at a $28M market cap, with Gartner Representative Vendor recognition, 87% gross margins, and 98% subscription revenue mix [1]. Q1 2026 showed green shoots with approximately $5M in total contract value from three major enterprise clients, and the EngineRoom acquisition announced June 2026 is expected to triple annualized revenue run-rate from $4M to $12M+ while adding $1.6M of adjusted EBITDA [2]. The CXAI 2.0 platform with agentic AI SKY capabilities could accelerate enterprise adoption, and management expects double-digit revenue growth in 2026 driven by expanding demand for AI-powered workplace automation [1].
The risks are overwhelming. Revenue has declined from $7.14M in FY2024 to $4.58M in FY2025, with Q1 2026 at just $950K (down 22% YoY), while the company burns approximately $7.7M annually in operating cash with only 35 employees [1][3]. The stock trades at $0.25 with a $28M market cap, and Nasdaq compliance requires maintaining a minimum $1.00 bid price — the proposed reverse stock split signals the severity of delisting risk [3]. ROE is -100.6% with no path to profitability on current trajectory. Microsoft Copilot, Google Workspace AI, and ServiceNow can replicate workplace AI features with minimal incremental effort, making CXApp's competitive position precarious at this scale [2]. Multiple years of sub-$5M revenue despite Fortune 500 client deployments demonstrates a fundamental product-market fit challenge — enterprises may value the concept but not enough to pay meaningful subscription fees. The EngineRoom acquisition, while strategically sensible, adds integration risk to a company already stretched thin.
