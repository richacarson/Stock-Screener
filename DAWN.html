Company Profile
Healthcare ·Biotechnology ·NASDAQ ·United States
Day One Biopharmaceuticals develops and commercializes targeted therapies for children and adults with cancer. Lead product OJEMDA (tovorafenib) is the first FDA-approved therapy for pediatric low-grade glioma. The company was acquired by Servier Pharmaceuticals in April 2026 for $21.50 per share (~$2.5B) and subsequently delisted from NASDAQ. Day One also acquired Mersana Therapeutics in January 2026 to add Emi-Le, a B7-H4-directed antibody-drug conjugate, to its pipeline.

Investment Thesis
Day One delivered explosive commercial execution in 2025 with OJEMDA generating $155.4M in net product revenue (up 172% YoY), beating Q4 consensus by 15% at $52.8M, and prescriptions surging 181% to 4,635 for the full year [1][2]. Management guided 2026 U.S. net product revenue of $225-$250M, implying 45-61% growth as OJEMDA penetration deepens in the pediatric low-grade glioma market where it remains the only FDA-approved targeted therapy. The company's $441.1M cash position provides substantial runway, and the Mersana Therapeutics acquisition adds a promising ADC pipeline to diversify beyond a single product [4]. The Servier acquisition at $21.50 per share (~$2.5B enterprise value) validates the platform and provides Day One's therapeutic portfolio with Servier's global commercialization infrastructure, particularly in European markets where OJEMDA has yet to launch [5].
The primary concern is that Day One is no longer a publicly tradable equity — the Servier acquisition closed and the stock delisted from NASDAQ in April 2026, making this a historical screen rather than an actionable investment opportunity [5]. Even prior to the acquisition, the company carried significant execution risk: profit margin of -67.85% and negative ROE reflect a cost structure that was not yet self-sustaining, with profitability dependent on continued OJEMDA revenue ramp outpacing R&D and commercial expenses [1]. The Mersana-acquired Emi-Le ADC is only in Phase 1 with no clear timeline to registration, and Day One's entire revenue base depends on a single product in a niche indication. Pipeline concentration risk was acute — any safety signal, competitive entry, or reimbursement challenge for OJEMDA would have had outsized impact on the business. The acquisition by Servier, while validating the platform, also means investors can no longer participate in the long-term upside of the pediatric oncology franchise.
Key Catalysts
  1. OJEMDA U.S. revenue trajectory toward $225-$250M in 2026 guidance, driven by continued prescription growth in pediatric low-grade glioma and potential label expansion into adult glioma indications
  2. Servier's global commercialization infrastructure enabling European regulatory submission and launch of OJEMDA, opening a market roughly equal in size to the U.S. pediatric LGG population
  3. Mersana-acquired Emi-Le (B7-H4-directed ADC) advancing through Phase 1 in adenoid cystic carcinoma, with potential to diversify the portfolio beyond a single-product revenue base
Key Risks
  1. Single-product revenue concentration — OJEMDA represents 100% of commercial revenue, and any safety signal, competitive entry from a larger pharma company, or reimbursement restriction would disproportionately impact the business
  2. Persistent unprofitability with -67.85% profit margin and negative ROE — the cost structure requires continued aggressive revenue growth to reach breakeven, and any slowdown in prescription trends would extend the path to profitability
  3. Post-acquisition delisting eliminates public market liquidity — the Servier acquisition closed at $21.50/share in April 2026, meaning this stock is no longer tradable and the screen serves as a historical record rather than an actionable recommendation [5]
