Dingdong (Cayman) Limited is a China-based fresh grocery e-commerce company operating a direct-from-warehouse-to-door delivery model. In February 2026, Dingdong announced the sale of its China operations to a Meituan affiliate for US$717M plus up to US$280M in contingent consideration, transforming the listed entity into a cash shell with potential global expansion ambitions.

Investment Thesis
Dingdong is now essentially an event-driven cash-shell story. The pending US$717M sale of China operations to Meituan (plus up to US$280M contingent) plus existing RMB 3.14B net cash positions the listed entity with significant post-close liquidity, and management has stated intent to return a substantial majority of proceeds via share repurchases and/or dividends [1]. Prior to divestiture, DDL executed a credible operational turnaround — eight consecutive quarters of YoY revenue growth, 2024 revenue up 15.5% to RMB 23B, and return to net profitability under founder Liang Changlin's 'efficiency first, scale later' strategy [1]. At ~$3 per ADS, the stock offers potential arbitrage on cash returns if the transaction closes cleanly.
The bear case is profound post-transaction uncertainty and Chinese ADR risk. After the China operations sale, the listed entity has no operating business — any 'global expansion' ambitions are speculative without specific products, regions, or leadership in place. Founder Liang Changlin resigned as CEO effective March 2026, with Song Wang succeeding [2]. Q4 2025 GAAP net income fell 63% YoY (RMB 33.6M from RMB 91.6M) with gross margin compressing on food category pricing, illustrating pre-sale operational fragility [1]. Liquidity at the ADR level is thin (~450K average daily volume × $3 ≈ $1.35M, just above the $1M IOWN liquidity floor) [1]. Historical governance concerns — the 2021 Rosen class action alleging IPO misstatements about food safety practices — add a trust overhang [3]. Chinese VIE structure and U.S.-listed ADR risk compound the uncertainty.
