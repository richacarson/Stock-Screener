Company Profile
Consumer Cyclical ·Residential Construction ·NYSE ·United States
D.R. Horton, Inc. is the largest homebuilder in the United States by volume, operating in 126 markets across 36 states. The company constructs and sells single-family detached and attached homes under the D.R. Horton brand, targeting first-time, move-up, active adult, and luxury buyers. D.R. Horton also provides mortgage financing, title services, and develops multi-family and single-family rental properties.

Investment Thesis
D.R. Horton is America's largest homebuilder operating in 126 markets across 36 states, offering deep cyclical value at 11.7x forward P/E — a significant discount to the S&P 500 [4]. Q1 FY2026 EPS of $2.03 beat the $1.93 consensus, and full-year guidance of $33.5-$35.0B revenue with 86-88K closings provides visibility [1]. The balance sheet is fortress-grade: D/E of 22.9% with $2.5B cash. The 29-year dividend streak (17.9% 5yr CAGR) signals durability, and DHI's scale advantages in land acquisition, construction costs, and mortgage origination create structural competitive advantages that smaller builders cannot replicate [1][4].
The bear case centers on the affordability-constrained housing market. Revenue declined 9.5% YoY with Q1 FY2026 net income dropping 30% to $595M as elevated mortgage rates (6.5%+) and incentive costs compress margins [1]. Profit margin of 9.9% is well below historical 15%+ levels, and management expects incentives to 'remain elevated in fiscal 2026.' The 5-year revenue CAGR of just 0.8% reveals that DHI has achieved no real growth through the cycle. ROE of 13.5% is below the 15% target as returns moderate from the 2021-2022 housing boom. A recession would further pressure orders, closings, and land values, potentially forcing write-downs on the $5.6B debt load [4]. The stock's beta of 1.45 means it would amplify any broad market selloff.
Key Catalysts
  1. Mortgage rate relief — a decline from current 6.5%+ levels would directly improve affordability, drive order growth, and allow DHI to reduce costly sales incentives [1]
  2. Scale advantage in a consolidating industry — smaller builders facing financing constraints may cede market share to DHI's 126-market footprint and cost advantages [1]
  3. Build-to-rent and multi-family expansion diversifying revenue beyond for-sale homes into recurring rental income streams [1]
Key Risks
  1. Persistently elevated mortgage rates above 6.5% compressing demand and requiring continued incentive spending that erodes profit margins from historical 15%+ to current 9.9% [1]
  2. Housing affordability crisis limiting first-time buyer demand — DHI's core customer segment is most sensitive to high rates and elevated home prices [1]
  3. Cyclical earnings risk — 5-year revenue CAGR of just 0.8% despite being at the top of the cycle, and a recession would accelerate order cancellations and land write-downs [4]
