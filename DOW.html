Company Profile
Materials ·Specialty Chemicals ·NYSE ·United States
Dow Inc. is a materials science company providing plastics, industrial intermediates, coatings, and silicones to packaging, infrastructure, mobility, and consumer care markets globally. The company was spun off from DowDuPont in 2019. With $40B in annual revenue, Dow is the world's second-largest chemical company. The company reported a net loss of $2.63B in the trailing twelve months, driven by elevated feedstock costs, weak end-market demand, and restructuring charges. The company pays a $2.80 annual dividend ($0.70/quarter) with a yield exceeding 8%.

Investment Thesis
Dow trades at 0.4x trailing revenue and 0.93x book value — deep value metrics for a company with $40B in revenue and #1–2 market positions in polyethylene, silicones, and polyurethanes. Goldman Sachs targets $27 (near current price) but sees recovery as the chemical cycle turns. The Path2Zero investment in low-carbon ethylene cracking positions Dow ahead of EU and US carbon regulations that could create competitive advantage for low-carbon plastics [1].
The bear case is severe: Dow posted a net loss of $2.63B in the trailing twelve months with negative FCF of -$1.60B, making the $2.80 dividend ($2.01B annual cost) unsustainable from earnings and barely covered by operating cash flow of $1.03B. Goldman's revised price target of $27 reflects a bearish view. The forward P/E of ~2,050 reflects near-zero earnings expectations for 2026. BASF and LyondellBasell face the same structural headwinds (weak European industrial demand, high energy costs), suggesting industry-wide pressure rather than Dow-specific issues — but that doesn't change the investability calculus [1].
Key Catalysts
  1. Chemical cycle recovery in packaging and construction end markets — any improvement in polyethylene demand from packaging customers would directly improve margins given Dow's cost position
  2. Path2Zero program milestones — the Fort Saskatchewan, Alberta, net-zero cracker represents a $3.6B long-term investment; any partner announcements or government support would validate the capital allocation
  3. Dividend sustainability confirmation with positive FCF — returning to positive free cash flow in any single quarter would remove the dividend cut risk overhang that is suppressing the stock
Key Risks
  1. Dividend sustainability: the $2.80/share annual dividend costs approximately $2.01B; with operating cash flow of $1.03B and negative FCF, the dividend is consuming more cash than the company generates — a cut is increasingly plausible
  2. Feedstock and energy cost pressure: Dow's crackers and chemical plants are energy-intensive; any sustained elevation in natural gas or naphtha prices would further compress already-thin margins
  3. Demand structural shift: AI-driven sustainable packaging alternatives and circular economy regulations could reduce demand for virgin polyethylene, Dow's core product — this is a 3–7 year secular risk, not a near-term risk
