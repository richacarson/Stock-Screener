Company Profile
Utilities ·Utilities - Regulated Electric ·NYSE ·United States
Duke Energy is one of America's largest energy holding companies, serving approximately 8.4 million electric customers and 1.7 million natural gas customers across six states in the Southeast and Midwest. Headquartered in Charlotte, North Carolina, the company operates through its regulated electric utilities (Duke Energy Carolinas, Duke Energy Progress, Duke Energy Florida, Duke Energy Indiana, Duke Energy Ohio, and Duke Energy Kentucky) and natural gas distribution subsidiaries. Duke Energy reported FY2025 revenue of $32.2B and is executing a $103 billion five-year capital plan focused on grid modernization, clean energy transition, and data center infrastructure.

Investment Thesis
Duke Energy at 17.65x forward earnings with a 3.24% dividend yield and 21 consecutive years of dividend growth offers a compelling regulated utility position anchored by structural tailwinds from the AI revolution [1][6]. The company's Q1 2026 results — revenue of $9.18B (up 11.3% YoY) and adjusted EPS of $1.93 (beating estimates by $0.13) — demonstrate accelerating growth driven by data center demand, electrification, and grid modernization investments [1]. As the nation's largest electric utility serving 8.4 million customers, Duke's regulated monopoly positions across six states provide durable, recession-resistant cash flows with government-sanctioned returns on its $103B capital deployment program [2]. The AI resilience profile is exceptional — Duke is not merely insulated from AI disruption but is a direct infrastructure beneficiary, as hyperscale data centers require massive baseload electricity that flows directly to Duke's revenue and rate base [3]. FY2026 EPS guidance of $6.55-$6.80 implies continued earnings acceleration.
The bear case centers on Duke Energy's capital-intensive balance sheet and modest shareholder returns relative to peers. D/E of 161% reflects the structural reality of utility infrastructure investment but limits financial flexibility during rising interest rate environments — every 100 basis points of higher borrowing costs compresses margins on the $103B capital plan [2]. ROE of 9.66% is below the 15% target and reflects the capped-return nature of regulated utilities, meaning Duke must continuously deploy massive capital just to grow earnings at mid-single-digit rates. Dividend growth of just 1.99% CAGR over five years is well below inflation, eroding real income for dividend investors despite the 21-year growth streak [6]. The company's environmental track record — including the 2014 Dan River coal ash spill ($102M in federal fines) and ongoing remediation liabilities — creates regulatory and litigation tail risk [4]. Duke's retreat from ESG commitments under political pressure and lobbying against renewable policies it publicly supports raise governance concerns about management credibility and long-term strategic consistency [4][5].
