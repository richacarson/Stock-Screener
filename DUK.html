Company Profile
Utilities ·Utilities - Regulated Electric ·NYSE ·United States
Duke Energy Corporation is one of America's largest energy holding companies, serving approximately 8.4 million electric and natural gas customers across six states (North Carolina, South Carolina, Florida, Indiana, Ohio, Kentucky). The company operates through Electric Utilities and Infrastructure (EU&I) and Gas Utilities and Infrastructure (GU&I) segments, generating electricity through coal, natural gas, nuclear, hydroelectric, and renewables. Founded in 1904 and headquartered in Charlotte, North Carolina.

Investment Thesis
Duke Energy is the largest U.S. electric utility by market cap ($102B), offering regulated earnings stability with 97 consecutive years of dividend payments and a 3.25% yield [4]. The company is a primary beneficiary of AI-driven electricity demand — 4.5 GW of data center contracts are secured, and the $103B five-year capital plan targets 14 GW of incremental generation capacity to meet surging load growth [1]. EPS guidance of $6.55-6.80 for 2026 implies 5-8% growth from $6.31 in 2025, with a long-term 5-7% annual EPS growth target through 2030 [1]. At 18.3x forward earnings, DUK trades at a reasonable premium for a regulated utility with visible multi-year growth driven by the AI energy buildout. Operating cash flow of $12.3B and a beta of 0.47 provide defensive portfolio characteristics [4]. AI-powered grid optimization has already generated $74M in savings and 15-20% outage restoration improvement [2].
The bear case centers on regulatory, environmental, and balance sheet risks. Duke carries $91.1B in total debt against $245M cash, reflecting the capital-intensive regulated model, with D/E of 172% [4]. The proposed 18.7% rate increase in North Carolina could face regulatory pushback and political opposition, threatening cost recovery on the $103B capital plan [3]. Environmental liabilities persist — the Dan River coal ash disaster legacy, ongoing coal ash cleanup costs at six facilities (80M tons), and environmental justice concerns in minority communities create reputational and financial risk [3]. Duke's lobbying against renewable energy policies (PURPA, Paris Climate Agreement) while publicly supporting clean energy creates a credibility gap that may invite scrutiny [3]. Revenue growth CAGR of 3.9% and dividend growth of 2.5% are below inflation-adjusted targets, reflecting the structural growth limitations of regulated utilities. Free cash flow is negative ($-2.0B) due to the massive capital program, requiring continued debt issuance and potential equity dilution [4].
