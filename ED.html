Company Profile
Utilities ·Utilities - Regulated Electric ·NYSE ·United States
Consolidated Edison, through its subsidiaries, provides regulated electric, gas, and steam delivery services in the New York metropolitan area. The company serves approximately 3.7 million electric customers in New York City and Westchester County, 1.1 million gas customers, and 1,490 steam customers. It also supplies electricity and gas to customers in southeastern New York and northern New Jersey. Founded in 1823 and headquartered in New York.

Investment Thesis
Consolidated Edison is the quintessential defensive dividend investment — a 200-year-old regulated utility serving New York City with 51 consecutive years of dividend growth (Dividend King status), a 3.1% yield, and predictable rate-regulated earnings [1][5]. The company reported $16.92B revenue in 2025 (up 10.9% YoY) and guided $6.00-$6.20 adjusted EPS for 2026, representing 5-9% earnings growth from $5.70 in 2025 [1]. At 17.0x forward P/E, the stock is reasonably valued for a utility with AI data center demand tailwinds and a $38B capital investment plan (2026-2030) that supports rate base growth of 6-7% annually. The regulated monopoly in one of the world's most valuable service territories provides structural resilience — New York City electricity demand is essentially non-discretionary.
The bear case is the limited growth ceiling inherent in regulated utilities. EPS growth guidance of 6-7% long-term is capped by regulatory allowed returns — Con Edison cannot price aggressively or expand aggressively beyond its service territory [1]. D/E of 117% reflects $28.4B in debt required to fund infrastructure, creating interest rate sensitivity if long-term rates remain elevated. ROE of just 8.8% is below the 15% target, reflecting regulatory constraints on returns. The 2.6% 5-year dividend growth CAGR barely exceeds inflation, meaning real dividend growth is near zero. Earnings declined 8.3% YoY as rate case timing created a negative comparison. Climate change and extreme weather events (severe storms, heat waves) pose operational risks to physical infrastructure in the New York metro area. Competition for capital from higher-yielding fixed-income alternatives at elevated interest rates may limit utility stock valuations [5].
Key Catalysts
  1. AI data center electricity demand — hyperscale AI buildout in the New York metro area directly increases Con Edison's rate base, driving revenue and earnings growth above historical utility averages [1]
  2. $38B capital investment plan (2026-2030) supporting 6-7% annual rate base growth and grid modernization for New York's clean energy transition [1]
  3. Potential rate case outcomes in 2026-2027 that could increase allowed returns and reset earnings growth trajectory after the -8.3% YoY earnings decline in 2025 [1]
Key Risks
  1. Regulatory ceiling on returns — allowed ROE is capped by New York regulators, limiting earnings growth to 6-7% annually regardless of operational improvements or demand growth [1]
  2. Interest rate sensitivity — $28.4B debt load makes financing costs highly sensitive to rates, and elevated long-term rates compress utility valuations as investors substitute into risk-free alternatives [5]
  3. Climate and weather risk — extreme weather events in the New York metro area threaten physical infrastructure reliability, create emergency restoration costs, and expose the company to regulatory penalties for service interruptions [1]

AI is a net demand tailwind: data centers require enormous electricity, and New York City is a primary market for hyperscale AI data center buildout, directly increasing Con Edison's rate base and revenue. The regulated utility model with government-granted monopoly provides additional insulation from any form of disruption. AI Opportunity (1/1): While Con Edison does not generate direct AI revenue, the company benefits from AI-driven electricity demand growth — data center power consumption in the New York metro area represents a material growth driver for the rate base, and the $38B capital plan includes grid capacity expansion to serve growing demand [1].
