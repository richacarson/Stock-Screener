Investment Thesis
Edison International offers deep value at 10.7x forward P/E with a 5.03% dividend yield (recently increased 6%) and 24.1% ROE—exceptional metrics for a regulated utility. The $38-41B grid modernization plan through 2030 with high-single-digit rate base growth and no expected equity issuance provides visibility into long-term earnings growth of 5-7%. Core EPS of $6.55 in 2025 beat guidance, and the securitization program provides financial flexibility to manage wildfire costs. Total shareholder returns of 10-12% annually (dividends + growth) are attractive at current depressed levels.
The bear case is dominated by wildfire liability risk—the Eaton Fire investigation where an idle SCE transmission line is suspected of sparking the blaze could result in billions in additional claims beyond the ~$1B already recorded. Subrogation settlements at $0.55 on the dollar suggest significant total liability exposure. 2026 EPS guidance of $5.90-$6.20 represents a decline from 2025's $6.55 due to wildfire costs and regulatory headwinds. D/E of 215.7% is heavily elevated, limiting financial flexibility, and any future catastrophic wildfire could threaten the company's solvency. The stock has declined significantly from prior levels, reflecting the market's assessment of existential wildfire risk.
Key Catalysts
  1. 2027 EPS guidance of $6.25-$6.65 represents recovery from 2026 muted earnings, validating the long-term 5-7% growth trajectory off the $5.84 base
  2. $38-41B grid modernization investment with no expected equity issuance could drive high-single-digit rate base growth through 2030
  3. Resolution of Eaton Fire liability at manageable levels would remove the primary overhang and could trigger significant multiple expansion from 10.7x forward P/E
Key Risks
  1. Eaton Fire investigation could result in multi-billion dollar liabilities if SCE's idle transmission line is confirmed as the ignition source, potentially threatening solvency
  2. D/E of 215.7% and muted 2026 EPS guidance ($5.90-$6.20 vs $6.55 in 2025) create near-term financial pressure as wildfire costs flow through earnings
  3. California's increasing wildfire frequency and severity pose recurring existential risk to utility infrastructure, with future catastrophic events potentially dwarfing current liabilities
