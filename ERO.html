Company Profile
Basic Materials ·Copper ·NYSE ·Canada
Ero Copper is a mid-tier copper producer with operations in Brazil. Its flagship Caraiba Operations in Bahia State produce copper concentrates with gold and silver by-products. The company also operates the Tucuma copper mine and is developing the Furnas copper-gold project, which has a preliminary economic assessment outlining a 24-year mine life with 108,000 tonnes average annual production.

Investment Thesis
Ero Copper delivered record 2025 results — $785.8M revenue (up 67% YoY), $409.7M adjusted EBITDA, and $263.7M net income — driven by Tucuma ramp-up and favorable copper prices [1]. At 6.5x forward P/E with 34.9% ROE and 33.6% profit margins, the stock is deeply discounted relative to its financial quality [4]. 2026 production guidance of 67,500-77,500 tonnes at C1 costs of $2.15-$2.35/lb positions Ero for continued strong cash flow in a $4.50+/lb copper environment. The Furnas PEA outlines a transformative growth project ($2.04B NPV, 24-year mine life, 108,000 tonnes annual production) that could double the company's production profile [1]. The Pilar shaft deepening extends Caraiba mine life, and management's focus on deleveraging (targeting sub-1x net debt/EBITDA) strengthens the balance sheet ahead of Furnas construction.
The bear case revolves around execution risk and commodity cyclicality. The Furnas project requires $1.28B in initial capex — more than Ero's current market cap of ~$3B — creating significant financing and dilution risk if copper prices decline during the multi-year construction period [1]. Brazil operational concentration exposes Ero to country risk including regulatory changes, environmental permitting delays (Furnas Phase 1 drilling already faced permit delays), and currency volatility. Free cash flow was negative $8.4M in 2025 despite record revenue, reflecting heavy capital investment at Tucuma and Pilar shaft [4]. Copper price sensitivity is extreme — a sustained move below $3.50/lb would compress margins significantly given C1 costs of $2.15-$2.35/lb. The stock's beta of 1.61 reflects high volatility, and the 52-week range of $10.85-$39.80 shows the market treats Ero as a high-risk mining equity [4].
Key Catalysts
  1. Updated Furnas feasibility study expected H1 2026, with improved economics in a higher metal price environment potentially unlocking the $2.04B NPV project and attracting development financing [1]
  2. Full revolver paydown and sub-1x net debt/EBITDA targeted in 2026, strengthening balance sheet ahead of Furnas construction decision and enabling potential shareholder returns [1]
  3. Secular copper demand growth from AI data centers, electrification, and grid buildout expanding the addressable market and supporting copper prices above $4.50/lb through 2026-2027 [2]
Key Risks
  1. Furnas project financing risk — $1.28B initial capex against a ~$3B market cap creates dilution or leverage risk if copper prices decline during the multi-year construction period [1]
  2. Brazil country risk — environmental permitting delays, regulatory changes, and currency volatility could disrupt operations at Caraiba, Tucuma, and the Furnas development [3]
  3. Copper price cyclicality — a sustained decline below $3.50/lb would severely compress margins given C1 costs of $2.15-$2.35/lb and high fixed-cost mine infrastructure [1]

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
9
AI Risk Protection (8/9): Copper mining is a physical-world operation that AI structurally cannot displace — extraction, processing, and transportation of ore requires massive physical infrastructure, specialized geological expertise, and decades-long permitting processes [2]. AI actually increases demand for copper, as AI data centers consume significantly more power than traditional facilities, driving electricity infrastructure buildout that is copper-intensive. Analysts note that new AI, defense, and robotics applications could strain copper supply without major gains in mining and recycling [2]. Ero's Brazilian operations face zero substitution risk from AI. AI Opportunity (1/1): While Ero does not generate direct AI revenue, its copper output is a critical input for AI infrastructure — data center power systems, electric grid expansion, and semiconductor manufacturing all require copper, making Ero an indirect AI beneficiary [2].
