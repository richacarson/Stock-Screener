Company Profile

Ford Motor Company develops, delivers, and services Ford trucks, SUVs, commercial vans, cars, and Lincoln luxury vehicles worldwide. The company operates through Ford Blue (ICE/hybrid), Ford Model e (EVs), Ford Pro (commercial), and Ford Credit (financing) segments. Founded in 1903, headquartered in Dearborn, Michigan.

Investment Thesis
Ford's bull case centers on its dominant truck/SUV franchise and undervalued forward P/E of 6.65x. Q1 2026 results were strong with $43.3B revenue (up 6% YoY), $2.5B net income, and adjusted EBIT of $3.5B — more than tripling YoY [1]. The company raised full-year 2026 EBIT guidance to $8.5-10.5B, partly aided by a $1.3B tariff refund after the Supreme Court struck down certain Trump-era tariffs. Ford Pro's commercial fleet business generates industry-leading margins, the 4.87% dividend yield provides income support, and the F-150's 48+ year sales dominance represents an unassailable franchise in American trucks.
The bear case is anchored in Ford's structural challenges. Model e EV losses continue at billions annually with no clear path to profitability, creating a persistent drag on group margins [1]. The -3.2% trailing profit margin and -14.8% ROE reflect a company that lost money in full-year 2025 despite $187B in revenue. D/E of 425% (including Ford Credit) represents massive leverage in a cyclical industry vulnerable to recession. The dividend was cut in both 2008 and 2020, and the current 64% payout ratio on volatile earnings offers no safety margin. Tariff uncertainty persists despite the recent refund, and a U.S. recession would hit auto demand severely. The company faces a trillion-dollar question: can it fund the EV transition without destroying its ICE business profitability?
Key Catalysts
  1. Ford Pro commercial fleet business achieving sustained double-digit margin expansion as fleet electrification and software-defined services drive recurring revenue
  2. Model e reaching contribution margin breakeven through cost reductions and next-generation EV platform launches, proving the EV transition can be self-funding
  3. Tariff clarity and favorable trade policy reducing input cost uncertainty, with the $1.3B Supreme Court refund as a template for further relief
Key Risks
  1. Model e EV losses continuing at multi-billion-dollar annual run rate with no clear timeline to profitability, consuming cash that could fund dividends and share buybacks
  2. U.S. recession triggering a cyclical downturn in auto demand, compressing pricing power on trucks and SUVs while 425% D/E leverage amplifies downside risk
  3. Autonomous driving disruption from Waymo and Tesla FSD reducing personal vehicle ownership over the medium term, structurally shrinking Ford's addressable market
