Company Profile
Industrials ·Engineering & Construction ·NYSE ·United States
Fluor Corporation provides engineering, procurement, and construction (EPC) services through three segments: Urban Solutions (advanced technologies, life sciences, mining, infrastructure), Energy Solutions (oil & gas, LNG, nuclear, energy transition), and Mission Solutions (U.S. government, nuclear remediation, defense). The company operates globally with a $25.5B backlog and was founded in 1912, headquartered in Irving, Texas.

Investment Thesis
Fluor is a top-tier global EPC firm with a $25.5B backlog (81% reimbursable) positioned at the intersection of multiple secular growth themes: data center construction, LNG expansion, nuclear energy revival, energy transition, and defense modernization [1]. The company is establishing 2026 adjusted EBITDA guidance of $525-585M, with consensus expecting 23.3% EPS growth and a 3.4% revenue increase, supported by a book-to-burn ratio exceeding 1 [1]. The balance sheet is exceptionally strong with $3.8B cash against $1.1B debt (D/E just 32.7%), providing flexibility for share buybacks and strategic investments [4]. At 15.1x forward P/E with a trailing PEG of 1.19, the stock is attractively valued for a company emerging from its turnaround with significant earnings leverage as the backlog converts to revenue.
The bear case centers on profitability uncertainty and execution risk. Fluor reported a GAAP net loss of $51M in 2025 with negative profit margin of -0.33% and negative ROE of -1.7%, meaning the turnaround to profitability is not yet complete [4]. Revenue declined 2% YoY in 2025 despite the strong backlog, and the 3-year revenue CAGR of 4.1% is modest. The CEO transition (Jim Breuer, May 2025) introduces leadership uncertainty during a critical execution period. Sustainalytics assigns a controversy level 5 (significant) and a 'High' ESG risk score of 33, suggesting unresolved governance or operational issues [3]. EPC project execution inherently carries risk — cost overruns, schedule delays, and client disputes can rapidly erode margins, particularly on the 19% of backlog that is not reimbursable. Operating cash flow was negative at -$387M in 2025, and free cash flow was -$292M, raising questions about when cash generation will match the earnings improvement narrative [4].
Key Catalysts
  1. Backlog conversion — $25.5B backlog with roughly half expected to convert to 2026 revenue, providing a visible and growing revenue pipeline across LNG, nuclear, data centers, and defense [1]
  2. Earnings inflection from GAAP loss to consensus forward EPS of $3.25, representing the completion of Fluor's multi-year turnaround as margin-accretive reimbursable work dominates the revenue mix [1]
  3. AI-driven infrastructure demand — data center construction and power generation projects in the pipeline benefit directly from the massive capital expenditure cycle driven by hyperscaler AI buildout [1]
Key Risks
  1. Profitability turnaround execution risk — 2025 GAAP loss of $51M and negative operating cash flow of -$387M mean the transition to sustainable profitability is not yet proven [4]
  2. CEO transition risk — Jim Breuer appointed May 2025 must execute on backlog conversion and margin improvement while establishing credibility with investors during a critical inflection period [2]
  3. EPC project execution risk — 19% of $25.5B backlog is non-reimbursable, and cost overruns or schedule delays on major projects could materially impact margins and repeat the losses that plagued Fluor in prior years [1]
