Fermi Inc. (d/b/a Fermi America) is a pre-revenue advanced energy and hyperscale data center development company co-founded by former U.S. Energy Secretary Rick Perry and Quantum Energy Partners co-founder Toby Neugebauer. The company is building Project Matador, a campus targeting 11 GW of private power generation (natural gas, nuclear, solar, battery) in Amarillo, Texas to serve AI hyperscalers.

Investment Thesis
Fermi represents a high-conviction bet on the intersection of AI demand growth and US energy infrastructure. The company has secured 2.3 GW of natural gas generation assets for 2026 delivery, 11 GW of TCEQ clean air permits (with 17 GW total campus projections), the first NRC Combined Operating License Application accepted in 15+ years for 4.4 GW of AP1000 nuclear, and partnerships with Westinghouse, Hyundai E&C, and Doosan [2]. At $6.18 per share (83% below the $36.99 IPO peak), the $3.8B market cap prices in significant execution risk while analyst consensus targets $29.50 (308% upside) with a 'Strong Buy' rating from 9 analysts [1]. The $165M equipment financing and $350M pre-IPO financing (including Macquarie's $100M equity) provide near-term capital, and the 40% projected revenue CAGR from 2025-2028 reflects the structural tailwind from AI power demand [1].
The bear case is substantial. Fermi is entirely pre-revenue with a $346.8M net loss in Q3 2025, and the company faces a class action lawsuit alleging it overstated tenant demand for Project Matador [3]. U.S. nuclear construction has a catastrophic track record — the last AP1000 reactors completed (Vogtle Units 3 & 4 by Southern Company in 2023) were 7 years late and $17B over budget, and former NRC regulator Dale Klein warned about the industry's pattern of 'overpromising and underdelivering' [2]. The stock has fallen 83% from its IPO peak in 5 months, suggesting the market has lost confidence in near-term execution. The 14.6M shares sold short (2.4% of float) reflects bearish sentiment. Key risks include: nuclear construction execution (cost overruns, delays, NRC permitting); tenant demand materialization (hyperscaler commitments may not convert); capital requirements (building 11+ GW requires tens of billions in financing beyond current resources); and governance concerns raised by the class action [1][3].

  3. Capital adequacy — building an 11-17 GW campus requires tens of billions in financing; current $165M term loan and $350M pre-IPO capital are a fraction of total needs, creating dilution and financing risk [1]
