Investment Thesis
GE Aerospace is the world's preeminent jet engine company delivering accelerating results in an aerospace super-cycle — Q1 2026 revenue surged 29% to $11.6B, orders exploded 87% to $23.0B, engine unit deliveries jumped 43%, and services revenue grew 39% with shop visit revenue up 35% [1]. The $190B+ backlog provides years of revenue visibility, with commercial services backlog alone at $170B. FY2026 guidance of $7.10-7.40 EPS, $9.85-10.25B operating profit, and $8.0-8.4B FCF (>100% conversion) demonstrates sustained double-digit growth with world-class cash generation [1]. Culp's FLIGHT DECK lean operating model is driving margin expansion and operational discipline across 57,000 employees [2]. The defense segment is inflecting with DPT book-to-bill above 2.0 for two consecutive quarters and 67% order growth in Q1 [1]. At $369 per share, the premium valuation (47.6x forward PE) is underpinned by a structurally advantaged competitive position — CFM International's ~60% narrowbody engine market share, multi-decade fleet maintenance revenue streams, and unmatched aerospace IP [1].
The bear case centers on extreme valuation and cyclical risk. At 47.6x forward PE and $384B market cap, GE Aerospace is priced for flawless execution of the aerospace super-cycle — any deceleration in commercial aviation demand, supply chain disruption, or global recession would compress the multiple severely [4]. The PEG ratio of 3.15 suggests the stock is significantly overvalued relative to its growth rate. Q1 2026 operating margins compressed 200bps due to loss-making GE9X ramp costs, and continued engine unit growth (+43%) pressures near-term profitability [1]. D/E of 117% with $21.3B debt creates financial obligations, though $10.6B cash and $8B+ annual FCF provide substantial coverage. The Middle East conflict overshadowed the Q1 earnings, creating geopolitical risk for air travel demand [1]. The aerospace cycle is inherently cyclical — a 2008-style recession would defer airline capital expenditure and compress engine orders. Defense business carries reputational and geopolitical risk. The company has yet to face a full economic downturn as a standalone entity [3].

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
9
AI Risk Protection (8/9): GE Aerospace's core business — designing, manufacturing, certifying, and servicing jet engines — is a physical-world, deeply regulated operation that AI structurally cannot displace [1]. Aircraft engines require decades of metallurgical expertise, FAA/EASA certification, and safety-critical manufacturing that no AI system can replicate. The aerospace MRO business benefits from fleet aging — as global aircraft supply remains constrained and fleets age, airlines spend more on maintenance, creating a captive service revenue stream backed by $170B in services backlog [1]. Defense engine programs (T901, F414) are long-cycle government contracts immune to AI substitution. Moreover, AI is a net positive for aerospace — AI data centers increase electricity demand, driving air travel for business and supply chain logistics. The $190B+ backlog provides multi-year revenue visibility that AI disruption cannot erode [1]. AI Opportunity (1/1): GE Aerospace deploys AI for predictive engine health monitoring, manufacturing process optimization, MRO workflow improvements, and digital twin technology — CEO Culp confirmed active AI deployment across operations [2].
