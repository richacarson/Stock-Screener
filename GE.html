Investment Thesis
GE Aerospace is the world's preeminent jet engine company riding a multi-year aerospace super-cycle, with 2025 results demonstrating accelerating execution: revenue up 18% to $45.9B, operating profit up 25% to $9.1B, FCF up 24% to $7.7B, and backlog expanding to $190B [1]. The company delivered record LEAP engine output (1,800+ units, +25% YoY) and is ramping toward 2,000 annually to meet insatiable demand from Boeing and Airbus [1]. 2026 guidance of $7.10-7.40 EPS (up 12-16%), $9.85-10.25B operating profit, and $8.0-8.4B FCF shows continued double-digit growth. Culp's FLIGHT DECK lean operating model is driving margin expansion and operational discipline across 57,000 employees [2]. The 25% dividend hike and $15B share repurchase authorization reflect confidence in durable cash generation. At 32x forward P/E, the premium is justified by 15%+ earnings growth, >100% FCF conversion, and a $190B backlog providing years of revenue visibility [1].
The bear case focuses on valuation and execution risks. At 32x forward P/E and $288B market cap, GE Aerospace is priced for sustained double-digit growth — any deceleration in commercial aviation demand or supply chain disruption would compress the multiple [4]. The defense segment (11% revenue growth) carries geopolitical risk, particularly the shareholder proposal regarding defense sales to Israel scheduled for the May 2026 annual meeting [3]. D/E of 114% with $21.6B debt creates financial obligations, though $12B cash and $7.7B annual FCF provide substantial coverage. LEAP production ramp to 2,000 units requires flawless supply chain execution — any bottleneck (like the 2024 engine inspection mandate) could disrupt deliveries. The aerospace cycle is inherently cyclical; a global recession would reduce airline profitability and defer engine orders. The company's sustainability ambitions (net-zero by 2050) require substantial R&D investment in next-gen propulsion that may not generate returns for decades [3].

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
9
AI Risk Protection (8/9): GE Aerospace's core business — designing, manufacturing, and servicing jet engines — is a physical-world, deeply regulated operation that AI structurally cannot displace [1]. Aircraft engines require decades of certification, metallurgical expertise, and safety-critical manufacturing that no AI system can replicate. The aerospace MRO business benefits from fleet aging — as global aircraft supply remains constrained, airlines spend more on maintenance, creating a captive service revenue stream. Defense engine programs (T901, F414) are long-cycle government contracts immune to AI substitution. The $190B backlog provides multi-year revenue visibility that AI disruption cannot erode [1]. AI Opportunity (1/1): GE Aerospace uses AI to optimize manufacturing processes, predictive maintenance for engine health monitoring, and MRO workflow improvements — CEO Culp confirmed AI is being deployed to 'help worker performance' across operations [2].
