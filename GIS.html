Investment Thesis
General Mills is a 160-year-old packaged food icon navigating the brutal transition from finite-game dominance to infinite-game resilience. The investment case rests on three pillars: (1) fortress brands with genuine pricing power (Cheerios, Betty Crocker, Blue Buffalo), (2) unmatched dividend consistency (127 years uninterrupted), and (3) emerging digital transformation leadership that positions the company as AI-enabled rather than AI-disrupted. CEO Jeff Harmening's 'Accelerate' strategy — boldly building brands, relentlessly innovating, unleashing scale, and standing for good — shows infinite game awareness, evidenced by regenerative agriculture commitments, digital twin supply chain, and MillsChat AI deployment achieving $20M+ savings.
However, the finite game still dominates daily reality. Organic growth has stalled (flat to +1% guidance FY2026), operating margins are compressing (down 4% FY2025), and market share losses necessitate increased promotional spending that erodes profitability. The 22% stock decline reflects market recognition that legacy categories (cereal, yogurt, baking) face secular decline, and Blue Buffalo pet food — while strategic — isn't large enough to offset the drag. At current valuation (9.68 P/E, below historical average), downside is protected by dividend fortress and brand equity, but upside requires proof that Accelerate strategy can accelerate growth, not just rhetoric. The 5.47% dividend yield is attractive and likely safe given 52% payout ratio and FCF generation, making this a defensible income hold.

  2. Elevated debt-to-equity (147%) limits financial flexibility during downturn, and dividend could face pressure if FCF deteriorates from margin compression, category headwinds, and rising interest expense
  3. Cultural erosion from recurring restructuring cycles and finite-game internal competition damages innovation capacity and employee engagement, preventing the trusting teams necessary to execute infinite-game transformation at scale
