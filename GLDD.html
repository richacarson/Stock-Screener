Investment Thesis
Great Lakes Dredge & Dock delivered record 2025 results with $888.3M revenue (up 16.5%), $73.5M net income, and record $171.3M adjusted EBITDA, demonstrating strong execution in America's largest dredging franchise [1]. The company has structural tailwinds from climate-driven coastal restoration demand, U.S. port deepening requirements for larger container vessels, and offshore wind energy support. The $1.0B dredging backlog plus $215M in pending awards provided strong forward visibility [1]. At 13.4x forward P/E with 26.5% revenue growth and 15.2% ROE, the stock was attractively valued before the acquisition. Jones Act protections limit foreign competition for domestic contracts, and the regulatory moat around environmental permits creates high barriers to entry.
The investment thesis is now academic as Saltchuk Resources completed its acquisition of GLDD at $17.00/share in April 2026, taking the company private at a total transaction value of approximately $1.5B [4]. The $17.00 price represented a significant premium to the pre-announcement trading range but was criticized by some shareholders as undervaluing the company's long-term potential given record financials and favorable industry tailwinds. Key risks that existed prior to the acquisition included: cyclical dependence on federal government spending for Army Corps of Engineers projects, weather-related project delays that create quarterly earnings volatility, and the capital-intensive nature of fleet maintenance requiring ongoing investment. The 88.6% D/E ratio reflected fleet financing that limited financial flexibility. With the stock now trading at the acquisition price, there is no investable opportunity for new positions [3].

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
9
AI Risk Protection (8/9): Dredging is among the most AI-immune industries in existence. The core business involves operating massive physical vessels (hopper dredges, cutter suction dredges) to move millions of cubic yards of sediment from waterways, ports, and coastlines [1]. This requires heavy capital equipment, specialized maritime crews, federal permits, environmental compliance, and relationships with the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. AI cannot replace the physical act of dredging, and the regulatory barriers (Jones Act requiring U.S.-flagged vessels, environmental permits) provide structural protection [2]. Climate change and sea-level rise are increasing demand for coastal restoration and port deepening — trends that AI amplifies rather than threatens. AI Opportunity (1/1): The company's VP of IT & Digital Innovation brings AI and analytics background, and modern dredging operations use GPS-guided positioning systems, sonar, and automated monitoring that incorporate machine learning for efficiency optimization [2].
