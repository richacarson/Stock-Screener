Investment Thesis
GoPro trades at $0.78 per share ($128M market cap) — a deep-value price reflecting severe pessimism about a once-iconic consumer electronics brand. The GP3 chip launching Q2 2026 — a proprietary 5nm SoC with 2x pixel processing, dedicated AI NPU, and materially better power efficiency — represents GoPro's most significant hardware innovation in years and could reignite the product cycle [1]. Management guides 2026 revenue of $750-$800M (nearly 20% growth at midpoint), with operating expenses cut to $220-$230M following aggressive restructuring. The AI content-licensing program (500,000+ hours submitted) is a novel revenue stream with near-zero marginal cost. Adjusted EBITDA improved from -$72M to -$29M in 2025, and the company generated positive free cash flow of $8.1M, suggesting the path to profitability is plausible [1].
The bear case is that GoPro is a structurally declining hardware business facing existential threats. Revenue has contracted from $1.09B in 2022 to $652M in 2025, a 40% decline in three years [1]. The stock trades at $0.78 — down from $98 at its 2014 IPO peak — reflecting a decade of value destruction under Woodman's leadership. With only 636 employees and $49.7M in cash against $83.3M in debt, the balance sheet is thin [4]. Chinese competitors DJI and Insta360 offer comparable or superior action cameras at lower prices, smartphone AI cameras continue closing the quality gap, and GoPro's subscription base is guided to decline 7% in 2026 [1][2]. Memory price increases are expected to compress gross margins by 500 basis points, tariff volatility adds supply chain risk, and the company has no proven ability to execute a turnaround after a decade of failed pivots (drones, media, subscriptions). The GP3 chip is a bet-the-company product launch — if it fails to reignite demand, GoPro's viability as a standalone company is in question [1][3].
