Company Profile
Financial Services ·Asset Management ·NYSE ·United States
Goldman Sachs BDC is a business development company specializing in middle market lending, originating secured and unsecured debt investments in private companies with EBITDA of $5-75 million. Externally managed by Goldman Sachs Asset Management, the fund invests $10-75 million per deal across first lien, unitranche, second lien, and mezzanine debt.

Investment Thesis
Goldman Sachs BDC offers a 15.1% dividend yield at 0.75x book value, backed by Goldman Sachs' institutional origination platform and a $3.5 billion middle market lending portfolio [1][3]. The forward P/E of 8.0 and profit margin of 32.6% reflect the BDC model's embedded leverage. Portfolio quality has improved with median borrower EBITDA increasing 84% to $71.8 million, and two-thirds of the portfolio is in first lien secured debt [1]. If base rates stabilize and credit quality holds, the current dividend of $0.32/quarter ($1.28 annualized, ~13.5% yield) appears more sustainable than the prior $0.48 level. The Goldman Sachs brand provides differentiated access to middle market deal flow that smaller BDCs cannot replicate [2].
The bear case centers on credit deterioration and structural headwinds. The 135.9% payout ratio remains unsustainable, and further dividend cuts are probable if NII continues declining — earnings fell 34.7% YoY with NII per share dropping from $0.40 to $0.37 [1][3]. Revenue declined 17.1% as spread compression from a competitive lending environment pressured yields. The SEC's 2022 $4 million fine against Goldman Sachs Asset Management for ESG policy failures raises governance concerns about the external manager [4]. Trading at 0.75x NAV signals the market prices in further credit losses or NAV erosion. The external management fee structure creates a structural conflict — GSAM earns fees on gross assets regardless of shareholder returns, incentivizing portfolio growth over quality [3]. Rising non-accruals in the middle market could accelerate losses if the economy weakens.
Key Catalysts
  1. Federal Reserve rate stabilization supporting net investment income and reducing borrower refinancing pressure across the $3.5B portfolio [1]
  2. Portfolio quality improvement with median borrower EBITDA up 84% to $71.8M, potentially reducing non-accruals and supporting NAV [1]
  3. Discount to NAV of 25% creates potential upside if credit performance improves and the market re-rates GSBD closer to book value [3]
Key Risks
  1. Sustained payout ratio above 100% threatens further dividend cuts below the current $0.32/quarter level, eroding the income thesis [3]
  2. Middle market credit deterioration as higher-for-longer rates pressure smaller borrowers' ability to service floating-rate debt [1]
  3. External management fee structure misaligns incentives — GSAM earns base management fees on gross assets regardless of GSBD shareholder returns [3]
