Investment Thesis
Garrett Motion is a post-bankruptcy turbocharger leader delivering steady cash flow in a transitioning industry. Q4 CY2025 revenue of $891M (+5.6% YoY) beat estimates, and EPS of $0.42 exceeded the $0.35 consensus by 18.6% [1]. 2026 guidance calls for revenue of $3.6-$3.8B, EBITDA of $672M midpoint, and the board authorized a $250M share repurchase program — meaningful capital return on a ~$3.7B market cap [1]. Garrett is one of the two global turbocharger leaders (with BorgWarner), serves premium OEM platforms, and is extending the franchise into electrified boosting, fuel cell compressors, and connected vehicle software [2]. At ~12.8x forward earnings with mid-single-digit revenue growth and disciplined cost structure, GTX offers reasonable value.
The bear case is the ICE-to-EV transition. Battery electric vehicles don't need turbochargers, and while hybrid/PHEV adoption extends the turbocharger runway, the long-term volume trajectory is negative. 2026 revenue guidance of -2% to +2% constant currency confirms the franchise is not growing organically [1]. Fuel cell compressors and electrified boosting offer optionality but are early-stage and R&D intensity remains below tech-forward auto parts peers, potentially limiting the electrification pivot [2]. Post-bankruptcy balance sheet still carries meaningful leverage, which could stress coverage if auto cycle weakens. Glassdoor reviews are mixed with some citing toxic workplace culture [2]. The stock has nearly doubled off the 52-week low ($8.69 to $19.48), compressing the margin of safety. Auto supplier cyclicality and OEM concentration add volatility risk.
Key Catalysts
  1. $250M 2026 share repurchase program reducing share count and supporting EPS growth above revenue growth [1]
  2. Fuel cell compressor and electrified boosting revenue ramp as hybrid adoption accelerates [2]
  3. Continued hybrid/PHEV penetration extending the ICE turbocharger runway [1]
Key Risks
  1. Long-term ICE-to-EV transition structurally reducing turbocharger demand [1]
  2. Auto cycle cyclicality and OEM concentration risk, particularly in Europe and China [1]
  3. R&D intensity below tech-forward peers may limit electrification product competitiveness [2]
  4. Post-bankruptcy leverage creating refinancing risk if credit conditions tighten [1]
