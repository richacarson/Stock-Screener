Company Profile
Energy ·Oil & Gas Equipment & Services ·NYSE ·United States
Halliburton Company provides products and services to the energy industry worldwide through two segments: Completion and Production (C&P) and Drilling and Evaluation (D&E). C&P delivers cementing, stimulation, artificial lift, and completion tools. D&E provides drilling fluids, wireline, testing services, and the Landmark iEnergy digital platform. The company serves oil and gas operators across North America and international markets. Under CEO Jeff Miller since 2017, Halliburton has focused on capital discipline, technology differentiation, and returns-focused growth. Founded in 1919, headquartered in Houston, Texas.

Investment Thesis
Halliburton reported Q1 2026 revenue of $5.4B (flat YoY) with operating margin of 13% and EPS of $0.55 [1]. While topline growth has stalled — revenue declined from $22.9B in 2024 to $22.2B in 2025 — the company's capital discipline under CEO Miller has maintained strong returns with 14.6% ROE and a manageable 74.7% D/E ratio. At 14.9x forward P/E with a 1.12 PEG ratio, Halliburton is attractively valued for a technology-differentiated oilfield services leader. The Landmark iEnergy AI platform, electric fracturing technology, and international D&E growth (up 4% in Q1) provide differentiation against commoditized peers. The 1.6% dividend yield with 37.6% payout ratio leaves room for continued shareholder returns.
The primary risk is that the global oilfield services cycle has peaked. Revenue has been flat-to-declining for three consecutive quarters, and North America activity (Halliburton's largest market) fell 6% in 2025 as US shale operators prioritize free cash flow over production growth [1]. Profit margin of 6.9% is structurally low for the capital-intensive services model, limiting earnings leverage even if revenue recovers. The 5-year revenue CAGR of only 3.0% reflects the industry's cyclicality and raises questions about long-term growth potential. OPEC+ production decisions, oil price volatility, and the energy transition create fundamental uncertainty about long-term demand for oilfield services. Completion and Production segment revenue declined 3% with operating income down 17% in Q1 2026, suggesting margin pressure in Halliburton's largest segment [1].

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
8
AI Risk Protection (7/9): Halliburton's core business — physically providing equipment, chemicals, and skilled crews to drill, complete, and produce oil and gas wells — is deeply protected from AI disruption. Oilfield services require heavy machinery, trained field engineers, and physical presence at wellsites that AI structurally cannot replace [1]. The regulatory and safety requirements of operating in hazardous environments create additional barriers. AI is purely an enabler for this business — it optimizes drilling paths, predicts equipment failures, and improves reservoir modeling, but cannot substitute for the physical service delivery. However, AI-driven automation could eventually reduce the number of field personnel needed per operation, creating some headcount risk. AI Opportunity (1/1): The Landmark iEnergy AI platform generates meaningful revenue as a digital offering to E&P operators, providing AI-powered well planning, drilling optimization, and production analytics [3]. Halliburton's hundreds of annual patents increasingly incorporate machine learning and data analytics capabilities, and AI-enhanced services command premium pricing.
