Company Profile
Technology ·Computer Hardware ·NYSE ·United States
HP Inc. provides personal computing, printing, 3D printing, hybrid work, gaming, and other related technologies globally. It operates through Personal Systems (commercial and consumer PCs, workstations), Printing (consumer and commercial printers, supplies, solutions), and Corporate Investments segments. Founded in 1939 as Hewlett-Packard Company, reincorporated as HP Inc. following the 2015 split, and headquartered in Palo Alto, California.

Investment Thesis
HP Inc. delivered solid Q2 FY2026 results with $14.4B revenue (up 9% YoY), non-GAAP EPS of $0.86 (up 21% from $0.71), and Personal Systems driving 13% growth on AI PC demand [1]. The stock trades at a deep value multiple of 8.1x forward earnings with a 4.81% dividend yield supported by 11 consecutive years of dividend growth and a sustainable 43.5% payout ratio. HP generates $3.5B in annual free cash flow, enabling consistent capital return through dividends and buybacks. The AI PC upgrade cycle provides a near-term tailwind as enterprises refresh aging hardware with AI-capable processors, and the workforce optimization plan should improve operating leverage over time [1][2].
The bear case centers on HP's structural challenges in both core segments. Printing faces irreversible secular decline as AI-driven digital workflows reduce printing demand, compressing the historically high-margin supplies revenue stream. The PC business is commoditized with thin 4.4% profit margins, and while AI PCs provide a short-term refresh cycle boost, they do not fundamentally change the competitive dynamics against Dell and Lenovo [1]. The negative stockholders' equity (-$346M) from aggressive buybacks creates balance sheet risk if business conditions deteriorate. The CEO transition to an interim leader, combined with 4,000-6,000 planned layoffs and Glassdoor reviews citing toxic culture, raises questions about HP's ability to attract and retain the technical talent needed for its AI transformation [2][3]. The 3-year revenue CAGR of -4.2% reflects the post-pandemic normalization reality that organic growth is elusive in mature hardware markets.
Key Catalysts
  1. AI PC enterprise refresh cycle driving Personal Systems revenue growth above 13% YoY as organizations upgrade to AI-capable hardware for on-device AI workloads
  2. Permanent CEO appointment providing strategic clarity and potentially accelerating the AI-driven transformation of HP's product portfolio and operations
  3. Workforce optimization delivering $500M+ in annualized cost savings by FY2028 through AI automation of 4,000-6,000 positions, expanding operating margins
Key Risks
  1. Secular decline in printing revenue as AI-powered digital workflows accelerate the shift to paperless operations, threatening HP's highest-margin business segment
  2. Commoditized PC market with 4.4% profit margins leaves minimal buffer against macroeconomic slowdowns or competitive pricing pressure from Dell and Lenovo
  3. Cultural deterioration evidenced by Glassdoor reviews citing toxic leadership, combined with planned 10% workforce reduction and interim CEO leadership, risks talent exodus during critical AI transformation period
