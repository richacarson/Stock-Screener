Company Profile
Technology ·Computer Hardware ·NYSE ·United States
HP Inc. provides personal computing, printing, 3D printing, and hybrid work technologies globally. Operating through Personal Systems ($10.3B Q1 FY2026 revenue) and Printing ($4.2B) segments, the company sells commercial and consumer PCs, notebooks, workstations, printers, supplies, and solutions. Formerly Hewlett-Packard Company, HP was split into HP Inc. (hardware) and Hewlett Packard Enterprise (enterprise IT) in 2015. Founded in 1939, headquartered in Palo Alto, California.

Investment Thesis
HP Inc. offers compelling income value at 6.98x forward earnings with a 5.78% dividend yield backed by 61 consecutive years of payments, 14 years of consecutive growth, and a 7.6% 5-year dividend growth CAGR [1][5]. Q1 FY2026 delivered $14.4B in revenue (+6.9% YoY) with Personal Systems growing 11% on AI PC demand, and management guides FY2026 non-GAAP EPS of $2.90-$3.20 with $2.8-3.0B in free cash flow. The AI PC refresh cycle provides a multi-year demand catalyst as enterprises upgrade aging fleets, and the 44% payout ratio leaves room for continued dividend growth. At $19B market cap, HP trades at less than 7x FCF, creating a significant margin of safety for income investors.
The bear case centers on secular decline in core markets and chronic leadership instability. Printing revenue fell 2% YoY and faces accelerating structural headwinds from digital workflows, while PC margins are being compressed by 100% sequential memory cost increases that management expects to persist [1]. The February 2026 CEO departure — the 6th leadership transition in 25 years — creates strategic uncertainty under interim CEO Bruce Broussard, and Glassdoor reviews describe a 'toxic culture' that suggests organizational dysfunction beyond any single leader [2]. Revenue has contracted from $62.9B (FY2022) to $55.3B (FY2025), a -4.2% CAGR that reflects post-pandemic normalization and underlying demand weakness. The company's negative stockholders' equity (-$346M) from aggressive buybacks means the balance sheet has no equity cushion against cyclical downturns, and the $10.9B debt load requires sustained free cash flow to service.
Key Catalysts
  1. Enterprise AI PC refresh cycle driving sustained double-digit Personal Systems growth as organizations upgrade aging fleets with NPU-enabled hardware for on-device AI workloads
  2. Permanent CEO appointment providing strategic clarity and potentially restoring organizational confidence after 6 leadership transitions in 25 years
  3. Subscription services expansion (HP All-In Plan) creating recurring revenue streams that could improve the margin profile beyond commodity hardware economics
Key Risks
  1. Secular printing decline accelerating as AI-powered digital workflows reduce physical printing demand, threatening the higher-margin Printing segment that generates disproportionate profit contribution
  2. Memory cost escalation — 100% sequential increase in Q1 FY2026 with further increases expected — compresses PC margins and may force HP to choose between profitability and market share
  3. Leadership vacuum with interim CEO and toxic organizational culture (Glassdoor reviews) creating execution risk during a critical AI PC transition period, compounded by the morale impact of announced 4,000-6,000 AI-driven layoffs
