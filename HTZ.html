Hertz Global Holdings, Inc. operates as a vehicle rental company through its Hertz, Dollar, and Thrifty brands from over 11,000 locations in 160 countries. Founded in 1918, the company emerged from Chapter 11 bankruptcy in 2021 and is headquartered in Estero, Florida.

Five CEOs in seven years, a bankruptcy in 2020, a botched EV strategy, and constant strategic pivots reveal a company lurching from crisis to crisis rather than playing to keep playing. The turnaround under Gil West shows operational discipline but no evidence of a deeper purpose beyond survival and margin recovery.

Investment Thesis
Hertz represents a deep-value turnaround play trading at 14x forward earnings with a clear path to profitability. Full-year 2025 saw $2B in profitability improvement, and management guides Q1 2026 mid-single-digit revenue growth with 600-800bps margin improvement YoY [1]. Fleet rotation is complete with average vehicle age under 10 months (youngest in a decade), direct operating costs are declining toward the 'North Star' low-$30s target, and the 2027 adjusted EBITDA goal of $1B would imply massive upside from the current $1.4B market cap [1]. Additionally, Hertz's positioning as an autonomous vehicle fleet management partner (per Wedbush) provides optionality beyond the core rental business.
The bear case is substantial. Hertz lost $747M in 2025 on $8.5B revenue, has negative book value, and carries $19.3B in total debt against just $566M cash [1][4]. The company has churned through five CEOs since 2017, preventing strategic continuity, and the disastrous EV fleet experiment destroyed hundreds of millions in shareholder value. The AI vehicle scanner rollout — intended to reduce damage fraud — instead alienated customers by billing at 5x historical rates, earning formal OECD AI Incident classification [3]. Analyst consensus is 'hold' with a median price target of $4.00 (below current price), and 3 of 9 analysts rate the stock 'sell' [1]. The turnaround thesis requires flawless execution from a management team with no track record of working together.
Key Catalysts
  1. Achievement of $1B adjusted EBITDA target by 2027 through continued direct operating expense reduction toward the low-$30s per transaction day target and mid-single-digit revenue growth
  2. Expansion of off-airport and mobility business segments, which carry higher margins than traditional airport rental and represent a structural growth opportunity beyond cyclical rental demand
  3. Potential autonomous vehicle fleet management partnerships as robotaxi operators like Waymo and Cruise need physical fleet infrastructure that Hertz's 11,000-location network uniquely provides
Key Risks
  1. Negative book value and $19.3B debt load create existential balance sheet risk — if the turnaround stalls, debt refinancing at current leverage levels could be prohibitively expensive or impossible
  2. Executive instability risk as the fifth CEO in seven years leads an almost entirely new C-suite team, with no proven track record of working together through economic downturns or competitive pressure
  3. Consumer trust erosion from the AI scanner controversy and EV fleet debacle — brand reputation damage may suppress pricing power and customer retention in a competitive rental market

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
8
AI Risk Protection (7/9): Car rental is a physical-world, infrastructure-heavy business that AI structurally cannot displace — customers need physical vehicles at physical locations with physical maintenance and logistics [3]. Hertz's fleet of 500,000+ vehicles across 11,000 locations represents a moat that no AI system can replicate. The long-term risk from autonomous vehicles exists but is years away from commercial scale and may actually benefit Hertz as a fleet management partner for robotaxi operators, per Wedbush's analysis of Hertz's 'unique positioning' in the AV ecosystem [3]. AI Opportunity (1/1): Hertz generates measurable value from AI through its UVeye vehicle damage scanners (deployed at major U.S. airports), AI-powered fleet management via Palantir partnership, and cloud-based AI operations under CIO Langley-Hawthorne — though the scanner controversy shows execution risk in AI deployment [3].
