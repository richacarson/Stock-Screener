Company Profile
Consumer Cyclical ·Residential Construction ·NYSE ·United States
Installed Building Products is a leading installer of insulation and complementary building products for residential and commercial construction in the United States. The company operates over 250 locations across the U.S. and offers insulation (fiberglass, spray foam, cellulose), waterproofing, fire-stopping, fireproofing, garage doors, rain gutters, window blinds, shower doors, and closet shelving. IBP grows through a disciplined acquisition strategy, having completed over 200 acquisitions since its founding.

Investment Thesis
IBP is the industry-leading insulation installer in the United States with a proven acquisition-driven growth strategy that has compounded revenue from $1.77B to $2.97B over five years. FY2025 delivered record profitability — $265.4M net income with Q4 gross margins at a record 35.0%, and EPS grew 7% despite a flat housing market [1]. ROE of 38.4% reflects the capital-efficient nature of the installation services model, and forward P/E of 19.5x is reasonable for a company with consistent M&A-driven growth visibility ($100M+ in targeted 2026 acquisitions). The 250+ branch network creates a durable competitive moat through local builder relationships, geographic coverage, and cross-selling across multiple installation trades. The Inspire Insight score of 56 with 9 positive attributions — including a 55% reduction in CO2 emissions from spray foam since 2020 — reflects genuine ESG progress [3]. IBP is structurally immune to AI disruption as physical installation work cannot be automated.
The bear case centers on housing cycle exposure, acquisition integration risk, and balance sheet leverage. Q1 2026 same-branch sales declined 5.9%, signaling meaningful organic weakness as mortgage rates above 6.5% suppress housing starts and remodeling activity [1]. The company's D/E of 176.9% reflects cumulative acquisition debt, and a prolonged housing downturn could strain leverage ratios — particularly if acquired branches underperform revenue expectations. IBP's roll-up model inherently carries integration risk: 200+ acquisitions create a complex operational portfolio where any individual branch can underperform without corporate visibility. Profit margin of 8.6% is thin for a $5.9B market cap company, and margin expansion depends on volume leverage that requires a healthy housing market. The stock has declined 10% from its 50-day SMA of $242.51, reflecting housing sector concerns [4]. Labor availability remains a structural risk — the skilled trades workforce is aging, and wage inflation for installers could compress margins. The 1.52% dividend yield provides minimal income support while waiting for the housing cycle to recover.
Key Catalysts
  1. Record Q4 2025 gross margins of 35.0% demonstrate pricing power and operational efficiency gains that should persist even in a softer housing market — margin expansion is a more reliable earnings driver than volume growth [1]
  2. Guided $100M+ in 2026 acquisition revenue, continuing the proven roll-up strategy that has grown the company from $1.77B to $2.97B over five years, with 200+ successful integrations providing a repeatable playbook [1]
  3. Housing cycle recovery potential — when mortgage rates eventually decline, pent-up housing demand could drive significant organic revenue acceleration on top of IBP's acquisition growth, creating a powerful dual-growth engine
Key Risks
  1. Q1 2026 same-branch sales declined 5.9%, indicating genuine organic weakness as elevated mortgage rates suppress housing starts — a prolonged housing downturn could strain the D/E ratio of 176.9% and pressure cash flow needed for acquisitions [1][4]
  2. Roll-up integration risk across 200+ acquisitions and 250+ branches — any portfolio of acquired businesses carries execution risk, and a housing downturn tests whether acquired branches can maintain profitability without the volume growth that justified their purchase multiples [1]
  3. Skilled labor shortage in the installation trades — aging workforce, rising wages, and competition for tradespeople from other construction sectors could compress margins and limit the company's ability to service growing backlogs [2]

AI Risk Protection (8/9): IBP's core business — physical installation of insulation, waterproofing, fire-stopping, and complementary building products in homes and commercial buildings — is structurally immune to AI disruption [2]. The company's competitive advantages — local relationships with builders, geographic coverage across 250+ locations, cross-selling capability, and decades of acquisition expertise — are physical and relational moats that AI cannot replicate. The only AI-adjacent risk is that AI-powered construction technology could optimize scheduling and reduce waste, but this would benefit IBP rather than threaten it.
