Company Profile
Consumer Cyclical ·Packaging & Containers ·NYSE ·United States
International Paper is the world's largest packaging company by revenue following the January 2025 acquisition of DS Smith for $7.2B. The company produces renewable fiber-based packaging, linerboard, and containerboard through its Packaging Solutions North America and Packaging Solutions EMEA segments. Founded in 1898, IP operates in over 30 countries with TTM revenue of approximately $25B. CEO Andy Silvernail (ex-IDEX/KKR) is executing a $710M cost-out program targeting $5B EBITDA by 2027.

Investment Thesis
International Paper is the world's largest packaging company following the $7.2B DS Smith acquisition, offering a 4.83% dividend yield at 22.3x forward P/E with Q1 2026 revenue of $5.97B (up 13.4% YoY) marking a return to profitability with EPS of $0.11 [1]. The $710M cost-out program and expected $514M in merger synergies provide clear earnings visibility toward the $5B EBITDA target by 2027 [1]. D/E of 66% is well-managed despite the acquisition, and the PEG ratio of 0.62 suggests undervaluation relative to growth. E-commerce growth structurally increases corrugated packaging demand, and IP's renewable fiber-based products benefit from the plastic-to-paper substitution trend. The 56-year unbroken dividend streak and 62,602-person global workforce across 30+ countries demonstrate institutional durability [4].
The bear case centers on persistent unprofitability and integration execution risk. ROE of -16.04% and profit margin of -13.77% reflect a company still absorbing DS Smith integration charges, and negative trailing EPS means the $1.85 annual dividend is currently uncovered by earnings — sustainability depends entirely on the earnings recovery materializing [4]. The 2026 full-year guidance of $24.1-24.9B revenue and $3.5-3.7B adjusted EBITDA was accompanied by a cut to Q2 EBITDA guidance, reflecting ongoing cost pressures [1]. The dividend has been frozen at $1.85 since 2022, with a negative 5yr growth CAGR of -2.03%, and there is no near-term path to dividend increases until earnings stabilize. IP's historical environmental penalties ($498M+ cumulative since 2000) and commodity-cyclical exposure to containerboard pricing create additional risk. The planned separation into two independent public companies adds execution complexity and management distraction during a critical integration period.
