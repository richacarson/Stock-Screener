Investment Thesis
Jiuzi Holdings is a micro-cap Chinese company with $2.88M in annual revenue, 33 employees, and a $1.2M market cap that has pivoted from NEV battery retail to crypto asset custody and solar energy investments [1][2]. The bull case rests entirely on optionality: the $30M Xinhui Solar partnership and $60M crypto treasury strategy could transform the company if crypto markets rally and solar demand materializes in Southeast Asia. The company has secured investor commitments to expand its private placement to $1B, suggesting institutional interest in the pivot [3].
The bear case is overwhelming. Jiuzi generated just $2.88M in revenue with a 0.7% gross margin and -$10.2M net loss in FY2025 [2]. The company has no competitive moat, no intellectual property, no meaningful market share, and only 33 employees. The frequent strategic pivots — NEV retail to crypto to solar within months — suggest a capital-raising vehicle rather than an operating business. The $300M-to-$1B private placement expansion is massively dilutive to a company with a $1.2M market cap, and the crypto treasury strategy exposes shareholders to volatile asset classes with no demonstrated custody expertise [3]. Leadership instability (chairman resignation in 2025) and opaque Chinese corporate governance add additional risk layers.
Key Catalysts
  1. Successful deployment of $60M crypto treasury strategy generating custody fee revenue or asset appreciation [1]
  2. $30M Xinhui Solar partnership producing tangible revenue from Southeast Asian solar energy markets [1]
  3. Completion of expanded private placement providing capital for acquisitions in the crypto custody ecosystem [3]
Key Risks
  1. Massive shareholder dilution from $300M-to-$1B private placement against a $1.2M market cap, effectively wiping out existing shareholders [3]
  2. No proven business model — zero revenue from crypto custody or solar ventures, with the legacy NEV business generating negligible revenue at negative margins [2]
  3. Chinese corporate governance risk with leadership turnover, opaque financial reporting, and a company that has pivoted strategy three times in 12 months [3]
