Company Profile
Real Estate ·REIT - Office ·NYSE ·United States
Kilroy Realty is a leading U.S. landlord and developer with operations in San Francisco, Los Angeles, Seattle, San Diego, and Austin. The S&P MidCap 400 REIT owns approximately 16.8 million square feet of primarily office and life science space plus ~1,000 residential units. The company has seven decades of experience developing, acquiring, and managing properties for technology, media, life science, and business services tenants.

Investment Thesis
Kilroy Realty offers a 7.0% dividend yield backed by 29 consecutive years of payments, $566M operating cash flow, and $1.3B liquidity, providing meaningful income for yield-focused investors [1]. The company's West Coast portfolio of 16.8M sq ft serves tech and life science tenants that benefit from AI-driven demand, and 2.05M sq ft of new leases signed in FY2025 demonstrate ongoing tenant demand despite office sector headwinds [1]. Carbon neutral operations since 2020 and industry-leading sustainability credentials differentiate Kilroy among institutional ESG-conscious allocators [3]. Net income grew to $276M from $211M in FY2025, and the $3.7B market cap at $30.87/share represents a substantial discount to replacement cost of the portfolio.
The bear case is substantial for an office REIT in a secular decline. FY2025 revenue declined 2% to $1.11B, FFO dropped from $4.59 to $4.20/share, and 2026 FFO guidance of $3.25-3.45 implies a further 18-22% decline [1]. Occupancy of 81.6% is below pre-pandemic levels and may face further pressure as AI-enabled remote work reduces office demand. The 93.1% payout ratio on the $2.16 annual dividend leaves virtually no margin of safety — if FFO declines further, a dividend cut becomes increasingly likely [5]. D/E of 83.7% with $4.7B debt against declining earnings creates leverage risk, and the forward P/E of 60.7x reflects the market's expectation of continued earnings deterioration [5]. West Coast concentration in San Francisco and Los Angeles exposes Kilroy to region-specific risks including high construction costs, regulatory burden, and tech sector layoffs that reduce office demand [1].
Key Catalysts

Key Risks
  1. Dividend sustainability at risk — 93.1% payout ratio with 2026 FFO guidance declining 18-22% YoY leaves minimal headroom before a potential cut [1][5]
  2. Secular office demand decline driven by remote work and AI tools reducing corporate space requirements, pressuring occupancy below 81.6% [2]
  3. West Coast concentration risk — San Francisco and Los Angeles face tech layoffs, regulatory burden, and shifting corporate headquarters preferences toward lower-cost markets [1]
