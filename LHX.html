Company Profile
Industrials ·Aerospace & Defense ·NYSE ·United States
L3Harris Technologies is a global aerospace and defense technology company providing mission-critical solutions across three segments: Space & Mission Systems (satellite and missile defense), Communications & Spectrum Dominance (tactical radios, broadband, night vision), and Missile Solutions (propulsion, hypersonics). The company was formed through the 2019 merger of L3 Technologies and Harris Corporation and acquired Aerojet Rocketdyne in 2023.

Investment Thesis
L3Harris Technologies is a top-tier defense prime positioned at the intersection of traditional military hardware and next-generation AI-enabled defense systems. Full-year 2025 revenue of $21.9B grew 5% organically with 6% organic revenue growth and 10% non-GAAP EPS growth, and 2026 guidance of $23.0-23.5B (7% organic growth) with $3B free cash flow reflects sustained momentum [1]. The strategic partnership with Palantir for AI-powered production and battlefield intelligence, involvement in the Pentagon's Golden Dome missile defense program, and $2B+ in missile-tracking satellite contracts position L3Harris to capture the defense modernization wave [2]. The Aerojet Rocketdyne integration provides vertical integration in propulsion and hypersonics — critical capabilities as the US prioritizes missile defense and deterrence. Conservative D/E of 34.6% and 44+ consecutive years of dividend payments demonstrate financial discipline, and free cash flow grew 20%+ to $2.8B in 2025 [1].
The bear case centers on defense budget uncertainty, execution risk, and valuation. At 37.2x trailing P/E and 23.5x forward P/E (PEG 2.01), L3Harris is priced for sustained growth that depends on continued defense spending increases which are politically uncertain [4]. Q1 2026 revenue of $5.65B slightly missed expectations despite the EPS beat, suggesting organic growth may be lumpy [1]. ROE of 8.2% is well below the 15% target, dragged down by the $19.6B equity base inflated by acquisition goodwill — any future write-downs could materially impact returns. The Aerojet Rocketdyne integration carries ongoing execution risk, and Glassdoor reviews of unrealistic project budgets and cost-cutting suggest margin expansion may come at the expense of engineering quality [2]. The stock has declined 16% from its 52-week high of $379, reflecting tariff and budget sequestration concerns. International revenue growth, while positive, exposes the company to geopolitical risk and export control changes [1].
Key Catalysts
  1. Pentagon's Golden Dome missile defense program selection and AI integration contracts, leveraging Palantir and commercial AI partnerships [2]
  2. 2026 free cash flow target of $3B (up from $2.8B in 2025), supporting continued dividend growth and potential share repurchases [1]
  3. International defense spending increases driven by NATO commitments and Indo-Pacific security concerns, expanding the addressable market beyond US DoD [1]
Key Risks
  1. Defense budget uncertainty — political shifts toward deficit reduction or spending caps could constrain the 7% organic growth trajectory that supports the current 23.5x forward P/E [1]
  2. Aerojet Rocketdyne integration execution — the $4.7B acquisition adds complexity and goodwill impairment risk to a company with already-thin 10.4% profit margins [4]
  3. ROE compression — 8.2% ROE reflects acquisition-heavy capital allocation that may not deliver returns above cost of capital, with risk of goodwill write-downs if defense spending disappoints [4]
