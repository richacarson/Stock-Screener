Company Profile
Industrials ·Aerospace & Defense ·NYSE ·United States
Lockheed Martin Corporation is the world's largest defense contractor, operating across four business segments: Aeronautics (F-35, F-16), Missiles and Fire Control (THAAD, Javelin, HIMARS), Rotary and Mission Systems (Sikorsky, Aegis), and Space (Orion, GPS satellites). The company delivered a record 191 F-35 jets in 2025 and ended the year with a record $194B backlog. Founded in 1995 from the merger of Lockheed Corporation and Martin Marietta, headquartered in Bethesda, Maryland.

Investment Thesis
Lockheed Martin is the world's largest defense contractor with FY2025 sales of $75B (up 6%), a record $194B backlog providing 2.5x annual sales in revenue visibility, and 2026 guidance of $77.5-$80B with segment operating profit growth exceeding 25% [1][2]. The company delivered a record 191 F-35s in 2025 and secured a transformative $35B THAAD interceptor contract. The dividend profile is exceptional — 23 consecutive years of growth, 2.20% current yield, and 6.4% 5-year CAGR — supported by a forward P/E of 17.69 and anticipated free cash flow of $6.5-$6.8B in 2026 [5]. Lockheed is structurally positioned to benefit from the global defense spending super-cycle driven by geopolitical tensions in Europe, the Indo-Pacific, and the Middle East.
Execution risk has emerged as a meaningful concern. Q2 2025 included a $1.6B program loss charge that dragged full-year EPS to $21.49, and Q1 2026 disappointed with $18.0B revenue (flat YoY), $6.44 EPS (below $6.74 consensus), and negative free cash flow of -$291M attributed to ERP system implementation issues [1]. D/E ratio of 339% exceeds the scoring floor, reflecting heavy leverage from acquisitions and share buybacks — the company carries $22.77B in debt against just $4.12B in cash [4]. Revenue growth is structurally modest (2.7% 5-year CAGR, 6% YoY) given the scale of the business and defense budget constraints. Profit margin of 6.69% reflects the cost-plus contract structure common in defense but limits earnings leverage. The 65.74% payout ratio, while sustainable, leaves limited room for aggressive dividend acceleration.
Key Catalysts
  1. THAAD interceptor production quadrupling under a $35B seven-year contract, representing the largest missile defense procurement award in decades and driving Missiles & Fire Control segment growth through 2032
  2. F-35 Block 4 upgrade program and sustained production at 191+ jets annually, with international demand accelerating from European allies responding to the Russia-Ukraine conflict
  3. Segment operating profit growth exceeding 25% in 2026 as the $1.6B Q2 2025 program losses roll off, driving EPS recovery and free cash flow generation of $6.5-$6.8B supporting continued dividend growth
Key Risks
  1. Q1 2026 negative free cash flow of -$291M from ERP implementation issues (1LMX program) creates near-term cash generation uncertainty and could recur through the multi-year enterprise system migration
  2. D/E ratio of 339% and $18.65B net debt position leave limited balance sheet flexibility if defense spending growth slows or additional program charges materialize on classified programs
  3. Revenue growth of 2.7% 5-year CAGR and 6.69% profit margin reflect structural constraints of the cost-plus defense contracting model, limiting upside in earnings growth relative to the commercial technology sector

AI Resilience LOW RISK

AI Risk Protection (8/9): Lockheed Martin's core business — designing, manufacturing, and maintaining the world's most advanced weapons systems, fighter aircraft, missile defense networks, and space platforms — is structurally immune to AI displacement [1]. These programs require decades of engineering expertise, security clearances, classified manufacturing facilities, multi-billion dollar capital investments, and deep integration with the U.S. government procurement apparatus that AI cannot replicate. The defense prime contractor model is protected by ITAR restrictions, sole-source contracts (F-35), and switching costs measured in decades and billions. AI actually increases demand for Lockheed's products through autonomous systems integration and AI-enabled warfare platforms. AI Opportunity (1/1): Lockheed is actively integrating AI into defense systems — appointing Dr. Craig Martell as CTO, deploying AI for autonomous targeting, mission planning, and predictive logistics [3]. The $35B THAAD contract and F-35 Block 4 both incorporate AI-enabled capabilities. AI is increasingly embedded in the defense systems Lockheed sells.
