Company Profile
Consumer Cyclical ·Department Stores ·NYSE ·United States
Macy's is an omni-channel retail organization operating department stores, websites, and mobile applications under the Macy's, Bloomingdale's, and Bluemercury brands. The company sells apparel, accessories, cosmetics, home furnishings, and other consumer goods. Founded in 1830, Macy's is executing a 'Bold New Chapter' strategy under CEO Tony Spring, closing 150 underperforming locations and investing in reimagined stores and luxury expansion.

Investment Thesis
Macy's Q1 FY2026 results exceeded expectations across every metric — net sales of $4.7B (up 1.8%, beating $4.61B consensus), comparable sales growth of 3.0% (strongest Q1 in four years), adjusted EPS of $0.13 (vs $0.03 consensus), and five consecutive quarters of better-than-expected results [1]. The company raised full-year FY2026 guidance to adjusted EPS of $2.00-$2.20 and comp growth of 0.5-1.2% [1]. Bloomingdale's posted its highest-ever Q1 sales and Bluemercury comps accelerated to 6.4%, validating the luxury expansion strategy. At 11.4x forward P/E with 3.0% dividend yield and 30.7% payout ratio, the stock offers deep value in a turnaround context [4]. The 'Ask Macy's' AI chatbot driving 5x spend per session and personal executive coaching for 200+ leaders demonstrate genuine operational modernization [2]. The stock is up 138% from its 52-week low, yet still trades at a significant discount to historical multiples.
The bear case centers on secular decline and structural headwinds. Revenue has contracted from $25.4B (FY2022) to a guided $21.5-21.75B for FY2026, and even the raised guidance implies revenue decline from the prior year [1]. Profit margin of 2.94% leaves minimal buffer for execution missteps or economic downturn. The 2024 accounting scandal — a single employee hiding $151M in delivery expenses over three years — exposed internal control failures and led to executive bonus clawbacks, damaging institutional credibility [3]. E-commerce penetration, AI-powered personalization by Amazon, and off-price expansion by TJX and Ross continue to structurally compress department store demand. The dividend was suspended during COVID (2020) and reinstated only in 2021, giving just 5 years of consecutive payments — a shorter track record than Dividend sleeve peers. Tariff headwinds of $0.10-$0.20 EPS impact could worsen if trade tensions escalate. The fundamental question remains: can department stores survive the structural shift to digital and off-price retail?
