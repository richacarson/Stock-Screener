Company Profile
Healthcare ·Medical Instruments & Supplies ·NASDAQ ·United States
Microbot Medical is a medical device company focused on transforming endovascular procedures through advanced robotic technology. Its flagship LIBERTY Endovascular Robotic System is the first single-use, remotely operated robotic solution that allows physicians to conduct catheter-based procedures from outside the catheterization laboratory, avoiding radiation exposure and physical strain. The company also has NovaCross intraluminal revascularization technology and a collaboration with Emory University on autonomous robotic endovascular procedures.
43 Employees ·microbotmedical.com

Investment Thesis
Microbot Medical offers early-stage exposure to the endovascular robotics revolution through LIBERTY, the first single-use, remotely operated robotic system for catheter-based procedures. The full market release at SIR 2026 marks the transition from development-stage to commercial company, with analysts projecting $4.39M in 2026 revenue growing 120% to $9.64M in 2027 [1]. The addressable market is large — over 2 million endovascular procedures are performed annually in the U.S. alone, and LIBERTY's single-use design eliminates the capital equipment barrier that limits adoption of competing systems. At a $157M market cap with near-zero debt (D/E 1.1%) and a PEG ratio of 0.7x, the valuation implies significant growth potential if commercialization gains traction. The Emory University autonomous robotics collaboration adds an AI-driven product evolution pathway [2].
The bear case is dominated by execution risk and capital constraints. Microbot has generated zero revenue in its 16-year history, lost $13.1M in the trailing period, and carries negative ROE of -32.4% — the transition from development to commercial revenue is the highest-risk inflection point for any medical device company [4]. The company must compete against Intuitive Surgical ($180B+ market cap), Siemens Healthineers, and J&J's Monarch platform with only 43 employees and a $157M market cap. Medical device adoption cycles are notoriously slow — hospital procurement committees, physician training requirements, and insurance reimbursement negotiations can delay revenue ramps for years. Additional capital raises will likely be needed, creating dilution risk. Forward P/E of -7.1x confirms the market expects continued losses. If LIBERTY adoption is slower than projected or competitors launch competing single-use solutions, the equity could face significant downside from current levels.
Key Catalysts
  1. LIBERTY commercial ramp — initial hospital installations and case volumes in 2026 will provide the first real-world adoption data, with the transition from $0 to $4.39M in projected revenue representing proof of commercial viability [1]
  2. Insurance reimbursement codes — securing CPT codes and favorable reimbursement rates for LIBERTY-assisted procedures would remove a major adoption barrier and accelerate hospital procurement decisions
  3. Autonomous robotics advancement — the Emory University collaboration on AI-driven autonomous endovascular procedures could yield next-generation LIBERTY capabilities that differentiate against larger competitors' manual systems [2]
Key Risks
  1. Commercial execution failure — the transition from FDA clearance to meaningful hospital adoption is the highest-mortality stage for medical devices, and Microbot's 43-person team must compete against the sales forces of Intuitive Surgical, Siemens, and J&J [2]
  2. Capital exhaustion and dilution — with -$13.1M in net losses and no revenue base, additional equity raises will be necessary to fund commercial operations, diluting existing shareholders in a low share price environment [4]
  3. Competitive response from incumbents — Intuitive Surgical, Siemens (Corindus), and J&J (Monarch) have the resources to develop competing single-use or AI-enhanced endovascular robotic platforms that could undercut LIBERTY's first-mover advantage

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience MODERATE
7
AI Risk Protection (6/9): Microbot Medical's core business — manufacturing and selling physical robotic surgical systems — involves hardware-intensive products with FDA regulatory clearance requirements that create meaningful barriers to AI-only disruption [1]. The LIBERTY system is a physical robot that must be manufactured, sterilized, and deployed in catheterization labs — AI cannot substitute for the hardware. However, the endovascular robotics space sits directly adjacent to autonomous AI-driven surgical systems, where larger competitors like Intuitive Surgical (da Vinci) and Siemens Healthineers (Corindus) are investing heavily in AI-enhanced capabilities. If autonomous AI-guided catheter navigation advances rapidly, MBOT's human-operated remote robotics approach could face obsolescence pressure from AI-native competitors with greater resources. AI Opportunity (1/1): The collaboration with Emory University on autonomous robotics in endovascular procedures positions Microbot to integrate AI capabilities into future LIBERTY iterations [2]. While no AI revenue is quantifiable today, the autonomous robotics R&D represents direct AI product development.
