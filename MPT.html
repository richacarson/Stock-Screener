Company Profile
Real Estate ·REIT - Healthcare Facilities ·NYSE ·United States
Medical Properties Trust is a self-advised REIT that acquires and develops net-leased hospital facilities. Founded in 2003 in Birmingham, Alabama, it owns 388 facilities with approximately 39,000 licensed beds in nine countries. MPT is the second-largest non-governmental owner of hospital beds in the U.S.

Finite vs Infinite Game — Sinek (25%)
Mindset: FINITE Overall: 1 /10
Medical Properties Trust plays a deeply finite game. The company's model — buying hospital buildings at inflated prices, charging above-market rents, and lending money back to struggling tenants to pay those rents — is optimized for short-term revenue recognition, not enduring value creation. The Steward Health Care catastrophe, where MPT's largest tenant collapsed into bankruptcy with $9B in liabilities while MPT continued collecting inflated rents, is a textbook example of finite game thinking where all parties prioritize near-term extraction over long-term sustainability [3].
Just Cause
1

The Steward bankruptcy led to hospital closures, unpaid employee wages ($290M), and community healthcare disruptions [3]. OCCRP investigations revealed MPT bought Steward properties at 9x market prices and charged 'exorbitant rents' the operator could not afford. Senators Warren and Markey described the relationship as resembling 'a Ponzi scheme' [3]. Whatever cause was articulated, the evidence shows a financial extraction model, not a healthcare mission.

Investment Thesis
The bull case for MPT rests on recovery and stabilization. The company beat Q4 2025 EPS estimates ($0.03 vs. forecast), reported normalized FFO of $0.58/share for full-year 2025, and targets $1B in annualized cash rent by end of 2026 [1]. The Steward relationship has been severed with properties transitioned to replacement operators, LifePoint and ScionHealth reported double-digit revenue growth and $200M+ EBITDARM increase, and the $150M share buyback signals management confidence [1]. At $4.53 with a 7.95% dividend yield and 388 facilities globally, the stock is priced for significant distress — any normalization of tenant operations could drive material upside from current levels. The international portfolio (particularly Germany) provides geographic diversification away from U.S. tenant risks.
The bear case is severe and well-documented. MPT has posted $3.2B in cumulative net losses over three years (2023-2025), the stock has fallen 82% from its 2021 peak of $23.63, and the dividend has been cut 69% from $1.16 to $0.36 annually [4]. The Steward bankruptcy exposed fundamental flaws in MPT's business model — above-market purchase prices, circular financing, and inadequate tenant underwriting — flaws that persist with replacement tenant HSA defaulting in February 2025 [3]. Key debt maturities of €500M (October 2026) and $1.4B (October 2027) create refinancing risk in a stressed balance sheet with 212% D/E [1]. Activist short sellers (Viceroy Research, Bear Cave) continue to flag tenant liquidity risks and revenue round-tripping concerns. Massachusetts has passed legislation limiting REIT hospital ownership, creating regulatory precedent risk [3]. CEO compensation of $16.4M annually while shareholders lost $11.5B raises serious governance concerns [2].
Key Catalysts
  1. Annualized cash rent reaching $1B target by end of 2026 as recently transitioned tenants ramp to 100% contractual rent, particularly Noor beginning partial rent in June 2026 [1]
  2. LifePoint and ScionHealth reporting double-digit revenue growth with $200M+ EBITDARM improvement, demonstrating replacement operator viability [1]
  3. $150M share buyback program execution at deeply depressed valuations, with board signaling confidence in recovery trajectory [1]
Key Risks
  1. Replacement tenant default risk — HSA received default notice in February 2025 while paying only 50% of contractual rent, repeating the Steward pattern of tenant distress [3]
  2. Critical debt maturities of €500M (October 2026) and $1.4B (October 2027) require refinancing at elevated leverage of 212% D/E during a period of negative profitability [1]
  3. Ongoing short seller scrutiny (Viceroy Research 21 reports, Bear Cave March 2026 report) alleging revenue round-tripping and tenant liquidity risks, with Massachusetts legislative precedent restricting REIT hospital ownership [3]
