Company Profile
Basic Materials ·Other Precious Metals & Mining ·NYSE ·Canada
McEwen Inc. (formerly McEwen Mining) explores, develops, produces, and sells gold and silver deposits in the United States, Canada, Mexico, and Argentina. The company owns 100% of the Gold Bar gold mine in Nevada, the Fox Complex in Ontario, and the Fenix project in Mexico, along with a 49% interest in the San José mine in Argentina. It also holds a significant copper development asset (Los Azules). Founded in 1979 by Rob McEwen and headquartered in Toronto, Canada.

Investment Thesis
McEwen Inc. offers compelling gold exposure at deep value pricing — forward P/E of 4.35x with a conservative 23.5% D/E balance sheet and a founder-CEO who owns 15% of the company on a $1 salary. The 2025 financial turnaround ($34.4M net income versus losses in 2024) validates the operational improvements, and the 2026 guidance of $80M in free cash flow from owned operations plus $50M+ in San José dividends positions the company for significant cash generation at current gold prices above $2,400/oz. The plan to double production to 250,000-300,000 GEOs by 2030 through Fox Complex expansion and Tartan development provides a clear growth trajectory, while the Los Azules copper asset offers substantial optionality as copper demand grows from electrification and AI infrastructure buildout.
The bear case revolves around execution risk and commodity price dependence. McEwen's production of ~100,000 GEOs makes it a junior miner with limited scale advantages, and the ambitious plan to reach 250,000-300,000 GEOs by 2030 requires successful mine development in Canada where Fox Complex production must grow from 16,000-19,000 to 105,000-120,000 GEOs — a 6x increase that carries significant capital expenditure and timeline risk. Cash costs of $2,100-$2,300/oz are above industry averages for mid-tier producers, leaving margins vulnerable if gold prices retreat below $2,000/oz. The company's earnings history has been highly volatile ($55M profit in 2023, -$44M loss in 2024, $34M profit in 2025), reflecting the operational challenges inherent in running mines across four countries with limited corporate scale. Rob McEwen is 76 years old with no obvious succession plan, creating key-man risk for a company whose brand and strategy are inseparable from its founder.
Key Catalysts
  1. Gold price remaining above $2,400/oz in 2026 would drive outsized free cash flow given the forward P/E of 4.35x and projected $80M+ FCF from owned operations, potentially enabling dividend reinstatement or accelerated mine development
  2. Fox Complex expansion from 16,000-19,000 to 105,000-120,000 GEOs by 2030 — successful construction milestones and permitting progress would significantly de-risk the production doubling thesis
  3. Los Azules copper project advancement could unlock substantial value as copper demand grows from electrification and data center construction — McEwen's 13% personal ownership of McEwen Copper signals management conviction
Key Risks
  1. Commodity price dependence — gold price decline below $2,000/oz would compress margins given $2,100-$2,300/oz cash costs, potentially returning the company to losses as experienced in 2022 and 2024
  2. Execution risk on the production doubling plan — Fox Complex development requires a 6x production increase by 2030 with significant capital expenditure, permitting, and construction risk in Ontario, Canada
  3. Key-man risk with 76-year-old founder Rob McEwen serving as Chairman and CEO with no disclosed succession plan — the company's identity, strategy, and shareholder base are deeply tied to his personal brand and capital commitment

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
9
AI Risk Protection (8/9): Gold and silver mining is structurally immune to AI disruption — the business involves physical extraction of minerals from underground and open-pit operations, geological exploration, chemical processing, and environmental remediation that AI cannot substitute [2]. The demand for gold is driven by monetary policy, inflation hedging, central bank reserves, and jewelry/industrial consumption — none of which are affected by AI technology. If anything, AI infrastructure buildout (data centers, semiconductors) increases demand for metals and power, creating indirect tailwinds for the mining sector [1]. McEwen's operations require physical mine development, environmental permits, community engagement, and geological expertise that exist entirely outside the digital domain. AI Opportunity (1/1): Rob McEwen has publicly championed AI-driven mining innovation at PDAC 2026, and AI applications in geological modeling, autonomous drilling, and operational optimization represent meaningful revenue-enabling opportunities that could reduce McEwen's cash costs per ounce from the current $2,100-$2,300 range [3].
