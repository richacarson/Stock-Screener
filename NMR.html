Company Profile
Financial Services ·Capital Markets ·NYSE ·Japan
Nomura Holdings is Japan's largest investment bank and securities firm, providing wealth management, investment management, and wholesale services (sales & trading, investment banking, advisory) globally. Founded in 1925, the company operates across Japan, the Americas, Europe, and Asia-Pacific with a significant presence in fixed income, equities, and M&A advisory.

Investment Thesis
Nomura is Japan's premier investment bank delivering accelerating financial performance — nine-month FY2026 net revenue of ¥1,590.5B (up 10% YoY) with ROE hitting 10.3% for seven consecutive quarters above the 8-10% target range [1]. Investment banking revenue surged 31% QoQ in Q3, driven by increased cross-border M&A and financing activity as Nomura leverages its unique East-West positioning. At 14.9x forward P/E and 0.81 PEG ratio, the ADR trades at a significant discount to Western peers (Goldman Sachs ~13x, Morgan Stanley ~14x) despite comparable growth rates [5]. The ¥60B share buyback and 4.95% dividend yield provide attractive capital returns, while Japan's structural tailwinds (NISA expansion, corporate governance reform, Tokyo Stock Exchange reforms) support the domestic wealth management franchise.
The bear case centers on recurring governance failures and structural challenges. The 2024 market manipulation scandal led at least 10 major Japanese financial institutions to temporarily halt business with Nomura, directly threatening the firm's domestic franchise [4]. This follows the 2012 insider trading scandal that forced CEO resignation, suggesting systemic compliance culture issues. The 895% D/E ratio reflects structural leverage inherent to investment banking but creates vulnerability during market dislocations. Non-Japan operations — particularly the troubled Lehman Brothers integration legacy in Europe — have historically generated subpar returns. EPS declined 8.7% in the most recent quarter despite revenue growth, indicating margin pressure from compensation and technology costs [5]. Currency risk (ADR denominated in USD, earnings in JPY) adds volatility for US-based investors.
Key Catalysts
  1. Japan's structural capital markets reform — NISA expansion, corporate governance improvements, and Tokyo Stock Exchange reforms driving domestic wealth management and equity trading volumes [1]
  2. Cross-border M&A acceleration as Japanese companies pursue overseas acquisitions and foreign capital flows into Japan, leveraging Nomura's East-West positioning [1]
  3. ¥60B share buyback program (Feb-Sep 2026) plus 4.95% dividend yield providing near-term shareholder return catalyst [1]
Key Risks
  1. Recurring governance and compliance failures — 2024 bond manipulation scandal followed 2012 insider trading scandal, indicating systemic cultural issues that could result in further client defections and regulatory penalties [4]
  2. Investment banking cycle risk — IB revenue surged 31% QoQ but is highly cyclical; any global M&A slowdown or market dislocation would compress wholesale earnings significantly [1]
  3. Currency and geopolitical exposure — JPY/USD fluctuation impacts ADR returns, while Japan-China tensions and global trade disruption could affect cross-border deal flow [5]
