Company Profile
Energy ·Oil & Gas Exploration & Production ·NYSE ·United States
Occidental Petroleum is a leading U.S. oil and gas company with major operations in the Permian Basin, Gulf of Mexico, and internationally. Following the January 2026 sale of OxyChem (its chemicals subsidiary) for proceeds used to reduce debt by $5.8B, the company is now focused purely on oil and gas and carbon management through its Oxy Low Carbon Ventures subsidiary. FY2025 revenue was $22.4B with free cash flow of $4.28B. Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway owns ~27% of outstanding shares.

Investment Thesis
Occidental is a Buffett-endorsed oil major trading at significant discount to fair value — Morningstar estimates $59 fair value vs. ~$44 current price [1]. The $5.8B debt reduction from OxyChem positions the company near its $15B long-term debt target, freeing cash flow for buybacks and dividends (8%+ dividend hike already announced). With 84% of the resource base breaking even below $50/barrel and FCF improving by $1.2B+ in 2026, OXY generates significant cash even in lower oil price environments. Warren Buffett's $12.6B stake provides a credible vote of confidence.
The bear case centers on oil price: Morningstar assumes WTI of $58/barrel in 2026, and at current prices of ~$65-70, the stock looks reasonably valued [1]. EPS declined ~30% YoY as oil prices fell from 2024 levels. Revenue has been declining for two consecutive years. The DAC/STRATOS investment is a long-duration bet that may not generate meaningful commercial revenue for 5-10 years. Analysts are split 16 Hold / 6 Buy / 4 Sell, reflecting genuine valuation uncertainty.
Key Catalysts
  1. Oil price recovery above $70 WTI sustained — would significantly increase FCF and potentially trigger a dividend increase above the current $0.26/quarter
  2. STRATOS DAC facility achieving commercial carbon removal credits — validation of the carbon management thesis and potential new revenue stream
  3. Debt reduction to $10-12B range — would signal potential for accelerated buybacks and multiple re-rating toward peer multiples
Key Risks
  1. Oil price decline to $50-55 WTI would compress FCF significantly, threatening the dividend growth trajectory given the recent OxyChem divestiture reducing earnings diversification
  2. STRATOS DAC commercial viability: direct air capture economics remain dependent on government carbon credits and voluntary carbon markets that lack price stability
  3. Energy transition acceleration: faster-than-expected EV adoption and renewable energy growth would reduce long-term oil demand forecasts and compress oil company valuations
