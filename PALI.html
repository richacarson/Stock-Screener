Company Profile
Healthcare ·Biotechnology ·NASDAQ ·United States
Palisade Bio is a clinical-stage biopharmaceutical company developing oral PDE4 inhibitor prodrugs for targeted delivery to the terminal ileum and colon. Its lead candidate PALI-2108 is in Phase 1b clinical trials for ulcerative colitis and fibrostenotic Crohn's disease, with Phase 2 IND submission planned for H1 2026.

Investment Thesis
Palisade Bio offers high-risk/high-reward exposure to a differentiated IBD drug candidate with compelling early clinical data. PALI-2108 achieved a 100% clinical response rate in Phase 1b UC and a 47.5% reduction in SES-CD in fibrostenotic Crohn's disease, validating the microbiota-activated PDE4 inhibitor mechanism [1]. The Phase 2 IND submission is planned for H1 2026, with RA Capital's 9.99% stake providing institutional validation [3]. At $290M market cap with $133M cash ($0.80/share in cash against $1.75 stock price), investors are paying only $157M for the pipeline — modest compared to the multi-billion dollar IBD market where drugs like Humira generated $21B at peak and newer agents command premium pricing. The gut-targeted delivery mechanism could differentiate PALI-2108 from systemic PDE4 inhibitors (apremilast) with fewer side effects, potentially capturing meaningful share in the 3+ million U.S. IBD patient population.
The bear case centers on clinical, regulatory, and financial risk typical of a 14-person clinical-stage biotech. The Phase 1b data, while encouraging, involved small patient cohorts — Phase 2 and Phase 3 trials could fail to replicate the 100% UC response rate or 47.5% SES-CD reduction [1]. The company previously failed a Phase 2 trial with its prior lead asset, sending the stock into free fall, demonstrating the binary nature of the investment [3]. With $10.8M in annual operating cash burn against $133M cash, runway extends 10+ years, but Phase 2/3 trials will require significantly more capital, and the ATM/dilution pathway has already been used [4]. Competition in IBD is intense — AbbVie, J&J, Pfizer, and Eli Lilly all have established or advancing treatments with massive commercial infrastructure that a 14-person company cannot replicate. The failed shareholder quorum (28.34%) suggests limited retail investor engagement, creating liquidity risk. CEO Finley's dual CEO/CFO role is a governance concern for a company entering later-stage clinical development.
Key Catalysts
  1. Phase 2 IND submission for PALI-2108 in moderate-to-severe ulcerative colitis planned for H1 2026, unlocking the next stage of clinical development in a multi-billion dollar IBD market [1]
  2. Positive Phase 1b topline data in fibrostenotic Crohn's disease (47.5% SES-CD reduction) opens a second IBD indication with limited competition, potentially doubling the addressable market [1]
  3. RA Capital 9.99% stake and $133M cash position provide financial stability and institutional credibility as the company advances toward Phase 2 trials [3][4]
Key Risks
  1. Binary clinical risk — Phase 1b small-cohort results (100% UC response, 47.5% SES-CD) may not replicate in larger Phase 2/3 trials, and the company's prior lead asset already failed Phase 2 [1][3]
  2. Intense IBD competition from AbbVie (Rinvoq), J&J (Tremfya), Pfizer (Xeljanz), and Eli Lilly with established commercial infrastructure that a 14-person company cannot match [2]
  3. Governance and organizational fragility — dual CEO/CFO role, 14 employees after 20% staff cut, failed shareholder quorum, and terminated executive equity plans signal precarious corporate health [3]
