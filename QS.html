Company Profile
Consumer Cyclical ·Auto Parts ·NYSE ·United States
QuantumScape Corporation develops solid-state lithium-metal battery technology for electric vehicles and other applications. Founded in 2010 and headquartered in San Jose, California, the company is pre-revenue and focused on commercializing its proprietary solid-state separator technology through its Cobra manufacturing process and Eagle pilot production line. Key partner Volkswagen Group (via PowerCo) is the primary development customer.

Investment Thesis
QuantumScape is the furthest-advanced pure-play solid-state battery company, with its Cobra separator process validated by Volkswagen Group through actual QSE-5 cell shipments and $19.5M in customer billings in 2025 [1]. The Eagle automated pilot line represents the critical bridge from lab to factory, and $970.8M in liquidity provides runway through 2029 without dilutive financing. The total addressable market for solid-state EV batteries is estimated at $100B+ by 2035, and QuantumScape's technology leadership and VW partnership position it as the front-runner to capture meaningful share if commercialization succeeds.
The bear case is severe: QuantumScape has generated zero commercial revenue in 16 years of existence, with a $421.4M net loss in 2025 and guided $250-275M EBITDA loss in 2026 [1]. The path from pilot line to mass production at automotive scale remains unproven — yield rates, cycle times, and cost per kWh at volume are all unknown. The $4.5B market cap implies significant future revenue that may never materialize if Toyota, CATL, or Samsung SDI commercialize competing solid-state technologies first. At $7.30/share, the stock has fallen ~95% from its 2021 SPAC-era highs, reflecting the market's diminishing confidence in the timeline. Free cash flow of -$148.6M annually plus operating cash outflows of -$241.2M mean the 2029 runway could shorten if manufacturing scale-up costs exceed projections [4].
Key Catalysts
  1. Successful Eagle Line scale-up demonstrating commercially viable yield rates and cycle times for QSE-5 cells, validating the Cobra process at production scale
  2. Expansion of customer billings beyond Volkswagen/PowerCo to additional OEM partnerships, diversifying revenue risk and validating technology across platforms
  3. Achievement of specific energy density and cost-per-kWh targets that prove economic viability against conventional lithium-ion batteries at automotive scale
Key Risks
  1. Manufacturing scale-up failure — the gap between lab-quality cells and high-volume automotive production remains the central risk, with yield rates and cost economics at scale entirely unproven
  2. Competitive obsolescence from Toyota (planning solid-state production by 2027-2028), Samsung SDI, and CATL, any of which could commercialize faster with deeper manufacturing expertise
  3. Cash depletion accelerating beyond projections if Eagle Line costs overrun, potentially forcing dilutive equity raises that further erode the stock from its current $7.30 level
