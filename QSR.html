Restaurant Brands International is a quick-service restaurant company that owns and franchises four iconic brands: Tim Hortons, Burger King, Popeyes Louisiana Kitchen, and Firehouse Subs. The company operates over 30,000 restaurants in more than 100 countries, primarily through a franchise model. RBI is majority-owned by 3G Capital.

Finite vs Infinite Game — Sinek (25%)
Mindset: MIXED Overall: 6 /10
RBI shows mixed infinite game characteristics. The company owns four iconic brands that have endured for decades (Burger King since 1954, Tim Hortons since 1964), suggesting infinite game DNA. However, the 3G Capital-influenced culture of aggressive cost-cutting and acquisition-driven growth reflects finite game optimization. CEO Josh Kobza represents a shift toward operational excellence and brand investment, but the high leverage and near-100% payout ratio leave little margin for long-term flexibility.

Investment Thesis
Restaurant Brands International owns four iconic QSR brands with 30,000+ restaurants in 100+ countries, generating $9.4B in annual revenue with 24% ROE and a 3.4% dividend yield [1][5]. The franchise-light model produces strong free cash flow, and CEO Josh Kobza is reaffirming 8%+ organic adjusted operating income growth and 5%+ net restaurant growth through 2028 with $1.6B in 2026 capital returns [1]. Q4 2025 delivered 7.4% revenue growth and 18.7% EPS growth (ex-items), with international same-store sales up 6.1% demonstrating global brand strength. At 17.5× forward P/E, the stock trades at a reasonable valuation for a global franchise platform with improving fundamentals.
The bear case centers on leverage and domestic weakness. D/E of 304% is at the scoring floor, reflecting the acquisition-funded franchise model's inherent leverage risk [5]. The 94.3% payout ratio leaves minimal cushion for dividend safety if earnings decline. Burger King U.S. same-store sales remain a challenge despite the 'Reclaim the Flame' investment, and domestic market share losses to Chick-fil-A and fast-casual concepts continue [1]. Profit margin of just 8.2% is thin for a franchise model and reflects the company's mix of owned restaurants and G&A costs. The 3G Capital overhang creates governance concerns — aggressive cost-cutting culture may conflict with the brand investment needed for long-term health. Deforestation-linked supply chain controversies (beef, soy, palm oil) have attracted shareholder activism, and weak ESG disclosure creates reputational risk [4].
Key Catalysts
  1. Burger King U.S. turnaround through 'Reclaim the Flame' modernization program delivering sustained same-store sales improvement and market share recovery [1]
  2. International expansion driving 5%+ net restaurant growth, particularly in underpenetrated markets where Burger King and Tim Hortons have strong brand recognition [1]
  3. $1.6B capital return in 2026 through dividends and buybacks, supporting yield and EPS growth for shareholders [1]
Key Risks
  1. Excessive leverage — D/E of 304% at the scoring floor with 94.3% payout ratio leaving minimal cushion for dividend maintenance if earnings decline [5]
  2. Burger King U.S. domestic market share erosion to Chick-fil-A and fast-casual competitors, despite $400M+ 'Reclaim the Flame' investment [1]
  3. Supply chain ESG risk — shareholder resolutions demanding deforestation reporting, weak sustainability disclosure, and reputational risk from beef/soy/palm oil sourcing [4]

The franchise model creates structural protection as franchisees own the physical assets and local customer relationships. Food preparation, real estate, and supply chain logistics are AI-resistant activities. Drive-thru ordering and kitchen operations may see AI augmentation but not displacement.
