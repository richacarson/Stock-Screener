Company Profile
Technology ·Computer Hardware ·NASDAQ ·United States
Quantum Computing Inc. is an integrated photonics company developing thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN) chips for quantum and classical optical applications. Products include entropy quantum computers (EQC) for optimization, reservoir computing machines (Emucore/Neurawave), LiDAR systems, and quantum photonic vibrometers. The company operates a TFLN foundry and completed acquisitions of Lumina Semiconductor and Nucreb in 2026. Founded in 2001, based in Hoboken, New Jersey. Market cap ~$2.1B.
72 Employees ·www.quantumcomputinginc.com

Investment Thesis
Quantum Computing Inc. offers speculative exposure to the quantum photonics revolution with a uniquely clean balance sheet — $986M cash against $5.4M debt and D/E of just 0.3% — providing multi-year runway to execute its TFLN foundry strategy [1][3]. Q1 2026 revenue of $3.7M (vs. $39K prior year) shows initial traction from the Lumina and Nucreb acquisitions, and the NASA R&D subcontract validates the technology's relevance. The $2.1B market cap prices in significant future optionality across quantum computing, AI photonic interconnects, LiDAR, and defense applications, with the company positioned at the intersection of quantum and AI hardware infrastructure.
The bear case is substantial: QUBT remains fundamentally pre-revenue with only $204K in organic Q1 2026 revenue (ex-acquisitions), a $20.6M operating loss, and negative gross margins — meaning every dollar of revenue costs more than it generates [1]. The 64% increase in shares outstanding represents massive dilution, and the $2.1B market cap is built almost entirely on speculative promise rather than commercial proof [3]. Multiple CEO changes in two years signal organizational instability, and the shareholder rights investigation by Purcell & Lefkowitz introduces governance risk [3]. The quantum computing industry remains pre-commercial with no clear timeline for when quantum advantage will translate to meaningful enterprise revenue. Competing quantum hardware architectures (superconducting from IBM/Google, trapped ion from IonQ/Quantinuum) and photonic competitors (Lightmatter, PsiQuantum) could marginalize QUBT's TFLN approach.
