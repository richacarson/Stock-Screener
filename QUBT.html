Company Profile

Quantum Computing Inc. is an integrated photonics company developing thin-film lithium niobate chips, the Entropy Quantum Computer (EQC) for optimization problems, and quantum sensing products including LiDAR and photonic vibrometers for commercial and government markets. The company recently acquired Luminar Semiconductor and NuCrypt, growing Q1 2026 revenue to $3.7M and holds $1.4B in cash.

Investment Thesis
Quantum Computing Inc. represents a high-risk speculative play on the photonic quantum computing thesis, now better capitalized with $1.4B in cash and demonstrating revenue acceleration to $3.7M in Q1 2026 (beating estimates of $2.8M). The acquisitions of Luminar Semiconductor and NuCrypt provide manufacturing capability and near-term revenue, while the EQC and reservoir computing products target the growing quantum-AI hybrid market.
The bear case remains severe: organic revenue was only $24K in Q1 2026 — the 9,370% headline growth is entirely acquisition-driven[3]. The $2.4B market cap on ~$15M annualized revenue (mostly acquired) implies extreme valuation. Operating losses of $19.8M per quarter are unsustainable without continued dilution. The Purcell & Lefkowitz shareholder investigation and insider activity concerns add governance risk. The WATCH rating reflects improved financial positioning offset by speculative valuation and unproven organic growth.
Key Catalysts
  1. Fab 1 reaching volume production of thin-film lithium niobate chips with commercial orders from defense or telecom customers
  2. EQC achieving demonstrated quantum advantage on a commercial optimization problem, validating the core technology thesis

Key Risks
  1. Organic revenue of only $24K suggests core quantum products have no commercial traction despite $2.4B market capitalization
  2. Shareholder investigation by Purcell & Lefkowitz into potential fiduciary duty breaches and questions about corporate claims regarding NASA relationships
  3. Competition from IBM, Google, IonQ with significantly larger R&D budgets could render photonic approach obsolete before commercialization
