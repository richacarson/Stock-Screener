Company Profile

QXO is the largest publicly traded distributor of roofing, waterproofing, and complementary building products in the United States, formed through Brad Jacobs' acquisition of Beacon Roofing Supply for $11 billion in 2025. Investment Thesis
QXO is Brad Jacobs' fifth company-building venture, applying his proven M&A playbook to the $800B building products distribution market. The $11B Beacon acquisition creates the largest US publicly traded roofing distributor with $6.84B revenue, 23% gross margins, and 9.5% adjusted EBITDA margins [1]. The pending $2.25B Kodiak acquisition adds $2.4B revenue and pushes EBITDA run rate above $1B. Jacobs' track record of doubling profits within five years at United Rentals and XPO provides a credible template for margin expansion through AI-enabled pricing, procurement optimization, and operational efficiency [2]. With $6B available for additional acquisitions post-Kodiak, QXO has significant firepower to consolidate a fragmented industry where the top 10 distributors control less than 30% of the market.
The bear case is execution risk at unprecedented scale. QXO is attempting to transform a historically low-tech industry while simultaneously integrating two major acquisitions ($11B Beacon + $2.25B Kodiak), managing significant acquisition debt (D/E 40.3%), and building an entirely new technology platform [1]. GAAP losses of $(0.63) per share and -4.1% profit margin reflect the massive integration burden. The building products market faces headwinds with residential construction expected flat to down 5% in 2026 [1]. The hostile Beacon takeover creates cultural integration risk — imposed acquisitions often suffer from employee disengagement and talent flight. Jacobs' AI transformation vision depends on attracting top AI talent to a distribution company, which the industry itself acknowledges as a major challenge. The $50B revenue target requires $40B+ in additional acquisitions, creating serial dilution and execution risk [2].
Key Catalysts
  1. Kodiak Building Partners acquisition closing Q2 2026, adding $2.4B revenue and pushing EBITDA run rate above $1B — expected to be highly accretive to 2026 earnings [1]
  2. AI-enabled margin expansion from dynamic pricing, demand forecasting, and warehouse optimization, targeting material improvement over Beacon's 9.5% EBITDA margin baseline [2]
  3. Additional acquisitions from $6B remaining capacity in a fragmented $800B market, with potential for accelerating the consolidation timeline toward the $50B revenue target [1]
Key Risks
  1. Integration execution risk — simultaneously absorbing $13B+ in acquisitions (Beacon + Kodiak) while building new technology infrastructure, with GAAP losses of $(0.63) per share reflecting the financial strain [1]
  2. Residential construction slowdown — William Blair expects flat to -5% residential activity in 2026, which would pressure organic revenue and complicate the integration of newly acquired businesses [1]
  3. Acquisition dependency — the $50B revenue target requires $40B+ in additional deals, creating serial dilution risk and dependence on accessible capital markets and willing sellers [2]
