Company Profile
Energy ·Oil & Gas Drilling ·NYSE ·Switzerland
Transocean Ltd. is the world's largest offshore contract drilling company, operating a fleet of 27 mobile offshore drilling units consisting of 20 ultra-deepwater floaters and 7 harsh environment semisubmersibles. The company provides drilling services to major integrated energy companies, government-owned energy entities, and independent operators worldwide.

Investment Thesis
Transocean is the premier way to play the deepwater drilling renaissance, with a $7.1B backlog at an implied $450,000+ average dayrate, 86% contract coverage for 2026, and average daily revenue of $476,000 — the highest in over a decade [1]. Q1 2026 revenue of $1.08B (up 19.3% YoY) and adjusted EBITDA of $440M (~41% margin) demonstrate the operating leverage inherent in a tight ultra-deepwater rig market approaching 100% utilization by 2027. The proposed $5.8B Valaris merger would create an industry champion with unrivaled scale and pricing power [2]. At $6.40 per share and a forward P/E of 31x (PEG 1.17), the stock offers meaningful upside if deepwater spending cycles extend through 2028-2030 as analysts project, with active debt reduction improving the equity value waterfall.
The bear case is anchored in cyclicality, leverage, and GAAP distortions: GAAP profit margin of -66.8% and ROE of -30.0% reflect massive impairment charges that, while non-cash, highlight the asset-heavy nature of the business and prior capital misallocation [6]. Total debt of $5.27B against $330M cash leaves limited margin for error if day rates decline. The proposed Valaris merger introduces significant integration risk and potential antitrust scrutiny in an already concentrated market. Transocean's business is fundamentally tied to oil prices and deepwater E&P spending decisions by a small number of national oil companies (Petrobras, Saudi Aramco) and supermajors — a capital allocation change by one or two key customers could materially impact utilization. The energy transition and ESG pressures on fossil fuel investment remain long-term structural headwinds for offshore drilling demand [5].
Key Catalysts
  1. Deepwater rig utilization approaching 100% by 2027, with $1.6B in new Q1 2026 backlog additions at ~$410,000 weighted average dayrate supporting continued revenue growth and potential dayrate inflection above $500,000
  2. Proposed $5.8B Valaris merger creating a 'disciplined duopoly' with enhanced pricing power, fleet rationalization synergies, and improved counterparty diversification
  3. Active debt reduction — $358M Deepwater Titan notes retired in Q1 2026 — derisking the balance sheet and increasing equity value per share as the offshore upcycle generates $136M+ quarterly free cash flow
Key Risks
  1. Oil price decline below $60/bbl would trigger deepwater project deferrals by national oil companies and supermajors, collapsing day rates and utilization from the current near-record levels
  2. $5.27B total debt against $330M cash creates significant financial fragility — a 2015-style downturn would threaten solvency given the capital-intensive nature of maintaining a 27-rig fleet
  3. Valaris merger execution risk including antitrust review, fleet integration, and cultural alignment, with any material delays potentially destroying the strategic rationale for consolidation
