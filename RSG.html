Investment Thesis
Republic Services is a high-quality defensive industrial with predictable cash flows, pricing power, and a physical moat from local route density in waste collection [1]. The company guided 2026 revenue of $17.05-17.15B with adjusted EBITDA of $5.475-5.525B and free cash flow of $2.52-2.56B, demonstrating the steady compounding characteristic of essential services businesses. At 26.1x forward P/E, RSG is priced as a premium compounder — justified by recession-resistant revenue, pricing exceeding cost inflation, and 22 consecutive years of dividend growth. AI adoption through RISE, MPower fleet optimization, and AI-driven recycling sorting enhances margins without disruption risk, as waste collection is inherently physical and cannot be digitized [2]. The Polymer Centers represent a genuine innovation in plastics recycling that could create new revenue streams as circular economy regulations tighten [3].
The bear case centers on valuation and growth limitations. At 30.6x trailing P/E and 3.02 PEG ratio, RSG is priced for perfection in a business that grows revenue only 2-4% organically [1]. The 1.19% dividend yield is below the 2% threshold, making it less attractive as an income investment despite the long growth streak. D/E of 115% reflects heavy capital intensity, with $13.8B in debt required to maintain fleet, landfills, and processing facilities. Revenue growth of 2.2% YoY is essentially inflation-pass-through with volume declines of approximately 1%, meaning the business is not growing in real terms [1]. The waste management duopoly (RSG and WM) faces regulatory risk from environmental standards changes and potential disruption from new recycling technologies. The premium valuation leaves little margin of safety if economic conditions weaken and waste volumes decline further.

  3. Regulatory changes to landfill emissions accounting standards or new environmental requirements could increase compliance costs and limit pricing flexibility [3]
