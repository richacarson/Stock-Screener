Company Profile
Industrials ·Aerospace & Defense ·NYSE ·United States
RTX Corporation is a global aerospace and defense company formed from the 2020 merger of Raytheon Company and United Technologies Corporation. Operating through three segments — Collins Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney, and Raytheon — RTX designs, manufactures, and services advanced systems for commercial aerospace and defense customers worldwide. Market cap of ~$251B with a record $271B backlog as of Q1 2026.

Investment Thesis
RTX Corporation delivered a strong Q1 2026 with $22.1B revenue (9% growth, 10% organic) and adjusted EPS of $1.78 (up 21% YoY), raising full-year guidance [1]. The investment case is anchored by a record $271B backlog (25% YoY growth) with a TTM book-to-bill above 1.4, providing multi-year revenue visibility unprecedented in the defense industry [3]. Raytheon segment margins expanded 150bps to 12.2% on favorable defense program mix and 40%+ munitions output growth. Global defense spending is accelerating — NATO allies increasing budgets, US supplemental appropriations for Ukraine/Taiwan, and Middle East demand for air defense systems all benefit RTX's product portfolio (Patriot, NASAMS, Tomahawk, Stinger). Pratt & Whitney's sole-source F-35 engine position and growing commercial aftermarket provide counter-cyclical stability. At 27x forward earnings on $6.60-$6.80 guided EPS, RTX is fairly valued for a company compounding revenue at 8-10% with expanding margins.
The bear case centers on execution risk, valuation, and margins. Forward P/E of 27x is elevated for a defense company historically trading at 15-20x, pricing in sustained double-digit earnings growth that requires continued backlog conversion and margin expansion [4]. Profit margin of 7.98% is structurally low for the defense industry and far below the 20% IOWN target, reflecting the capital-intensive manufacturing nature of aerospace-defense [4]. The Pratt & Whitney powder metal remediation continues to create earnings drag and engine grounding costs that could extend beyond current estimates. D/E of 59% is manageable, but $39.5B in total debt creates interest expense burden. Cultural integration challenges from the 2020 merger persist 6 years later, with reports of micromanagement, turnover, and organizational friction across 180,000 employees [2]. Government shutdown risk or defense spending cuts in future budget negotiations could slow backlog conversion. ESG concerns around weapons manufacturing and the Inspire MISALIGNED score (-49) may limit the investor base.

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK
9
AI Risk Protection (8/9): RTX's core business is structurally immune to AI disruption across all three segments [2]. Defense systems (Patriot missiles, NASAMS, Tomahawk) require physical manufacturing of complex weapons systems in classified facilities with multi-year government contracts — AI cannot substitute for warhead production, propulsion systems, or radar arrays. Pratt & Whitney jet engines involve metallurgy, thermodynamics, and precision manufacturing that are fundamentally physical. Collins Aerospace avionics and cabin systems are FAA/DoD-certified hardware installed on aircraft. The defense market has 30+ year procurement cycles, classified technology barriers, and sole-source positions (F-35 engine) that create impenetrable moats. AI actually increases demand for RTX products — autonomous systems, AI-guided munitions, and sensor fusion require more of RTX's hardware. The only exposure is that AI could eventually optimize procurement processes, but this affects the customer's decision-making speed, not RTX's business model. AI Opportunity (1/1): RTX actively deploys AI across defense applications — missile guidance, sensor fusion, predictive engine maintenance, autonomous systems — generating meaningful defense revenue from AI-enabled products [2]. The $271B backlog includes significant AI-enhanced weapons and sensor systems.
