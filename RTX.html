RTX Corporation is an aerospace and defense company operating through three segments: Collins Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney, and Raytheon. The company provides systems and services for commercial, military, and government customers worldwide, including aircraft engines, avionics, defense electronics, missiles, and integrated defense systems.

Just Cause
5
RTX articulates its purpose as advancing aviation and building smarter defense systems, which connects to national security and human mobility — meaningful causes [3]. However, the mission was essentially rewritten during the 2023 rebrand from Raytheon Technologies, and it hasn't been tested through decades of consistent execution under this identity. Defense contractors inherently serve a cause bigger than profit (national security), but RTX's commercial half dilutes the clarity of purpose.

Investment Thesis
RTX is a top-3 global aerospace and defense company delivering accelerating growth — FY2025 revenue of $88.6B (up 10% organically) with record backlog of $268B (up 23% YoY) providing multi-year revenue visibility [1]. Free cash flow surged 75% to $7.9B, and 2026 guidance calls for $92-93B in sales with $8.25-8.75B FCF [1]. The three-segment structure (Collins Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney, Raytheon) provides diversification across commercial aerospace recovery, defense spending growth, and high-margin aftermarket services. Commercial aftermarket grew 17% in Q4 while defense orders accelerated to 10% organic growth, supported by global security spending increases [1]. At 25.2x forward P/E, the stock is priced for continued execution but the $268B backlog and 1.56x book-to-bill ratio de-risk the growth trajectory significantly.
The bear case centers on valuation stretch, margin headwinds, and governance concerns. Trailing P/E of 38.2x is elevated even for an aerospace compounder, reflecting optimism already priced in [5]. Profit margin of 7.6% lags peers due to Pratt & Whitney's GTF powder metal compensation ($700M cash outflow in 2026) and defense contract mix [1]. The $124M FCPA settlement over Qatari bribery raises governance questions about internal controls [6]. Political risk emerged with threats to cut government contracts over perceived slow delivery and stock buyback prioritization. Supply chain constraints and input-cost inflation (metals, electronics) could pressure the margin expansion narrative. The 1.43% dividend yield falls below inflation, and the payout ratio of 53.8% limits flexibility for dividend growth acceleration.
Key Catalysts
  1. Record $268B backlog converting to revenue at 5-6% organic growth in 2026, with commercial aftermarket expected to grow high-single-digits at accretive margins [1]
  2. Free cash flow expansion to $8.25-8.75B in 2026 enabling accelerated shareholder returns and debt reduction while funding $10.5B in R&D investment [1]
  3. Global defense spending acceleration driven by NATO rearmament, Indo-Pacific security concerns, and Middle East instability benefiting Raytheon's missile defense and sensor portfolios [3]
Key Risks
  1. Pratt & Whitney GTF powder metal defect remediation — $700M cash outflow in 2026 with multi-year engine inspection and compensation obligations that pressure margins and free cash flow [1]
  2. Political and regulatory risk — government contract threats, FCPA settlement history, and potential defense spending reprioritization could impact the $107B defense backlog [6]
  3. Valuation compression risk — at 38.2x trailing P/E and 25.2x forward P/E, any execution miss or guidance reduction could trigger significant multiple contraction [5]
