StandardAero is a leading independent aerospace engine aftermarket services provider offering MRO, on-wing support, asset management, and component repair for commercial, business, military, and helicopter aircraft. Investment Thesis
StandardAero is the world's largest independent aerospace engine MRO provider delivering strong financial results — 2025 revenue of $6.06B (up 16% YoY) with adjusted EBITDA up 17% and a dramatic free cash flow turnaround to $209M from -$45M in 2024 [1]. The 2026 guide is compelling: $6.3-6.4B revenue, $870-905M adjusted EBITDA, $1.35-1.45 EPS (18% growth at midpoint), and $270-300M free cash flow [1]. At 15.5x forward P/E, the stock is attractively valued for a business with long-term tailwinds — the aging global aircraft fleet drives structurally rising MRO demand, and StandardAero's exclusive positions on CFM56 and LEAP engines provide multi-decade revenue visibility. The LEAP program ramp alone could drive sustained double-digit growth as the installed base requires its first overhaul cycle.
The bear case centers on margin compression and execution risk. Profit margin of 4.6% is thin even for MRO businesses, and the LEAP program ramp is margin-dilutive in 2026 before achieving profitability [1]. D/E of 92% and legacy Carlyle-era debt burden the balance sheet; net leverage of 2.4x EBITDA limits financial flexibility. Supply chain part delays continue to constrain throughput and tie up working capital, with full normalization potentially 12+ months away [1]. A December fire at the Phoenix CRS facility disrupted component repair operations. US government shutdown disrupted military maintenance revenue in Q4 2025 with spillover into Q1 2026 [1]. StandardAero's culture shows signs of resistance to innovation, with Glassdoor reviews noting quarterly-focused executives and limited R&D investment [2]. The stock's post-IPO decline from ~$30 to $26 suggests the market is pricing in execution risk on the LEAP ramp.
Key Catalysts
  1. LEAP engine program ramp reaching profitability in H2 2026, with the growing installed base driving decades of recurring MRO demand as first overhaul cycles begin [1]
  2. Free cash flow accelerating to $270-300M in 2026 (36% growth), enabling debt reduction from 2.4x leverage and potential capital returns [1]
  3. Commercial aerospace aftermarket structural tailwind — aging global fleet with deferred maintenance from COVID-era and supply chain constraints driving sustained MRO demand growth [1]
Key Risks
  1. Supply chain part delays constraining throughput and tying up working capital across the global network, with normalization potentially 12+ months away [1]
  2. LEAP program margin dilution in H1 2026 — if the ramp to profitability is delayed, it could compress overall margins below guidance [1]
  3. Thin 4.6% profit margin with 92% D/E ratio leaves limited buffer if aerospace aftermarket demand softens or a recession reduces flight hours [1][4]
