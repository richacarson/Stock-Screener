Companhia de Saneamento Basico do Estado de Sao Paulo - SABESP provides water supply and sewage collection and treatment services across 375 municipalities in Sao Paulo State, Brazil. Privatized in 2024 with Equatorial Energia as the strategic investor, the company serves approximately 9.5 million water connections and 8.2 million sewage connections. Founded in 1954 and headquartered in Sao Paulo, SABESP is the largest water and wastewater utility in the Americas.

Investment Thesis
SABESP is the largest water utility in the Americas, serving 28 million people across 375 municipalities in Sao Paulo State. The 2024 privatization under CEO Carlos Piani has unleashed a growth transformation — CapEx more than doubled to a record BRL 15.2B in 2025 with BRL 20B planned for 2026, driving 43.9% revenue growth and 87.2% earnings growth [1]. At 11.5x forward P/E and 0.47 PEG, the stock is remarkably cheap for a utility delivering 21.3% ROE, 22.2% profit margin, and a clear path to universal sanitation by 2029 [4]. Adjusted EBITDA of BRL 13.2B grew 17% YoY, the company completed a $784M IFC-backed sustainability loan, and M&A ambitions (Copasa) signal a platform for inorganic expansion beyond Sao Paulo State [2][3]. The universalization mandate provides regulatory-backed growth visibility through the end of the decade.
The bear case centers on Brazil country risk, governance concerns, and execution challenges. D/E of 94.7% will rise further as the company funds BRL 20B+ annual CapEx programs, potentially straining the balance sheet if Brazilian interest rates remain elevated or the real depreciates [4]. The 2025-2026 Sao Paulo drought exposed tensions between private management and public service obligations — rotating water cuts disproportionately affected low-income communities, drawing regulatory and political scrutiny that could result in tariff restrictions or concession modifications [3]. SABESP's ~20% water loss rate from aging infrastructure requires massive remediation spending that competes with expansion CapEx. Currency risk is significant — the stock is priced in USD on the NYSE but revenues and costs are denominated in Brazilian reais, exposing investors to BRL/USD volatility. The privatization is only two years old, and whether the post-privatization management team can sustain operational excellence through Brazil's volatile political and economic cycles remains unproven [2].

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience LOW RISK

AI Risk Protection (8/9): SABESP's core business — operating water treatment plants, maintaining thousands of kilometers of pipes, and managing sewage collection and treatment facilities across 375 municipalities — is structurally immune to AI disruption [1]. Water and wastewater services involve massive physical infrastructure, complex chemical and biological treatment processes, and regulatory compliance that AI cannot substitute. The company's natural monopoly in Sao Paulo State, backed by long-term concession agreements, provides absolute protection from digital disintermediation. AI may actually increase demand for water services as data centers and AI infrastructure require significant cooling water resources. AI Opportunity (1/1): While SABESP has not disclosed specific AI revenue, the company can deploy AI for predictive maintenance of pipe networks (reducing the current ~20% water loss rate), optimizing treatment plant operations, and demand forecasting across its 9.5 million connections — all of which would generate measurable operational savings that translate to margin improvement [3].
