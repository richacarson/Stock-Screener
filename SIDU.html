Company Profile
Industrials ·Aerospace & Defense ·NASDAQ ·United States
Sidus Space, Inc. designs, manufactures, launches, and operates commercial satellites for data collection worldwide. The company offers the LizzieSat modular satellite platform, the Fortis VPX edge computing module, and the Orlaith AI Ecosystem integrating edge processing with AI software for space-based sensor analytics. Founded in 2012 by CEO Carol Craig and headquartered in Merritt Island, Florida, the company serves commercial space, government, intelligence, and defense customers.
99 Employees ·www.sidusspace.com

Investment Thesis
Sidus Space offers speculative exposure to the growing space economy through its vertically integrated satellite manufacturing, AI-enabled edge computing (Fortis VPX), and space-based data analytics (Orlaith AI Ecosystem) platform [2]. The company entered 2026 with $43.2M in cash and no debt, providing 12-18 months of runway to commercialize its growing defense and commercial pipeline [1]. CEO Carol Craig's appointment to the Canaveral Port Authority Board reflects growing industry credibility, and the space data analytics market is expanding rapidly as AI drives demand for satellite-based intelligence. If the company converts pipeline to contracted programs, the micro-cap valuation ($276M market cap) offers significant upside.
The bear case is substantial: revenue has declined for three consecutive years from $7.3M (2022) to $3.4M (2025), cost of revenue increased 48% to $9.1M while revenue fell 28%, and the net loss widened to $29.5M in 2025 from $17.5M in 2024 [1]. The company burns roughly $8M per quarter and has provided no numeric 2026 guidance, leaving the path to commercial viability entirely uncertain. At 99 employees and a $276M market cap, Sidus is a micro-cap competing against well-funded defense primes (Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman) and venture-backed space startups (Planet Labs, Spire Global). The $43.2M cash position was raised through dilutive equity offerings, and additional raises may be needed if commercialization is delayed beyond 18 months.
Key Catalysts
  1. Conversion of defense and commercial pipeline into contracted programs could drive revenue inflection — management cited accelerating commercialization as the 2026 priority, with Fortis VPX customer deployments underway
  2. LizzieSat-4 and LizzieSat-5 production milestones and successful deployment could validate the modular satellite platform and attract additional government and commercial customers
  3. Growing defense and intelligence spending on space-based ISR (intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance) capabilities could benefit Sidus as a domestic, ITAR-compliant small satellite manufacturer
Key Risks
  1. Three consecutive years of declining revenue with no numeric 2026 guidance creates fundamental uncertainty about commercial viability — the company may be unable to achieve product-market fit before cash reserves are depleted
  2. Cash burn of approximately $8M per quarter against $43.2M cash provides only 12-18 months of runway, creating near-term dilution risk through additional equity raises if commercialization is delayed
  3. Micro-cap status ($276M market cap) with 99 employees competing against defense primes and well-funded space startups creates significant competitive disadvantage in winning large government contracts and attracting talent
