Company Profile
Consumer Discretionary ·Auto Parts ·NASDAQ ·United States
Solid Power, Inc. is a developer of solid-state battery technology focused on producing sulfide-based solid electrolyte material for electric vehicles and other applications. The company's proprietary solid electrolyte replaces the liquid or gel electrolyte in traditional lithium-ion batteries, potentially offering higher energy density, improved safety, and lower cost. Founded in 2011 and headquartered in Louisville, Colorado, Solid Power has partnerships with BMW and Ford for joint development of all-solid-state batteries and a partnership with SK On for automotive-scale production.

Investment Thesis
Solid Power holds a differentiated position in the solid-state battery race with proprietary sulfide-based solid electrolyte technology backed by partnerships with BMW, Ford, and SK On, who are jointly funding development toward automotive-scale production [1][3]. The company reported a narrower-than-expected Q1 2026 loss of $0.06 per share versus the estimated $0.13 loss, and maintains approximately $435M in total liquidity providing a multi-year cash runway to reach commercial milestones [1]. If solid-state batteries achieve commercial viability, the total addressable market exceeds $100B by 2035 as EV adoption accelerates, and Solid Power's electrolyte-first strategy positions it as a key supplier rather than competing directly with cell manufacturers [4].
Revenue declined 49% YoY in Q1 2026 to $3.07M as milestone-dependent revenue proves lumpy and unpredictable, with TTM revenue of just $15.81M against $91M in annual losses — a burn rate that will consume the cash runway within 5-6 years without commercialization [1][2]. Solid-state battery technology faces formidable competition from QuantumScape, Toyota's sulfide batteries targeted for 2027-2028 production, and Samsung SDI, any of whom could achieve commercial scale first and commoditize the electrolyte market [4]. The 52-week price decline from $8.86 to $2.60 reflects deep market skepticism about the timeline and commercial viability of solid-state technology, and additional dilutive equity raises are likely needed if the commercialization timeline extends beyond the current cash runway [1][2].
