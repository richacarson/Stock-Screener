Investment Thesis
SS&C Technologies offers a compelling value proposition at a forward P/E of just 9.9x, trading at a significant discount to application software peers due to an overstated AI disruption narrative [1][8]. The company delivered record Q4 2025 revenue of $1.65B (up 8.1%) and guided for 5.1% organic growth in 2026 with ~12% adjusted EPS growth to $6.70-$7.02 [1]. Operating cash flow surged 25.7% to $1.74B, and the company targets a 40% EBITDA margin by Q4 2026 [1]. Bill Stone's 40-year leadership tenure, a 12-year dividend track record with 10.2% 5-year CAGR, and aggressive AI deployment through the Link platform position SS&C for sustained mid-single-digit organic growth with expanding margins.
The bear case centers on SS&C's $7.5B gross debt load (2.8x net leverage) which limits financial flexibility in a rising-rate environment [1]. GAAP EPS declined 22.1% YoY, and the significant gap between GAAP ($3.15) and adjusted ($6.76) earnings raises quality-of-earnings concerns. Morgan Stanley's downward price target revision to $86 with an Equal Weight rating reflects persistent AI disruption fears for back-office processing [8]. The Calastone acquisition adds integration risk, and organic revenue growth of 4.8-5.3% is modest for a technology company. At 28,800 employees, margin expansion may require difficult workforce restructuring decisions.
Key Catalysts
  1. Agentic AI platform rollout across fund administration clients, with 29% adoption rate and 44% planning implementation within 12 months [4]
  2. EBITDA margin expansion toward 40% target in Q4 2026, driving adjusted EPS growth of ~12% to $6.70-$7.02 [1]
  3. Calastone acquisition integration expanding SS&C's fund distribution capabilities into new markets [2]
Key Risks
  1. AI disruption narrative continues to weigh on valuation despite fundamental resilience, with Morgan Stanley maintaining Equal Weight at $86 PT [8]
  2. $7.5B gross debt burden at 2.8x net leverage constrains capital allocation flexibility and increases interest rate sensitivity [1]
  3. GAAP vs. adjusted earnings divergence ($3.15 vs. $6.76 EPS) reflects heavy amortization from acquisition-heavy strategy that may obscure true profitability trends [1]
