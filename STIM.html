Company Profile
Healthcare ·Medical Devices ·NASDAQ ·United States
Neuronetics designs, develops, and markets the NeuroStar Advanced Therapy System for transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) treatment of major depressive disorder and obsessive-compulsive disorder. Following the December 2024 acquisition of Greenbrook TMS, the company operates both a device/consumable business and a network of TMS treatment clinics across the United States.

Investment Thesis
Neuronetics is the market leader in TMS therapy for treatment-resistant depression, with over 7 million treatments delivered and the largest US TMS clinic network following the December 2024 Greenbrook acquisition. Full year 2025 revenue doubled to $149.2M, with pro forma growth of 15% and Q4 treatment utilization up 11% [1]. Q1 2026 guidance of $33-35M implies continued growth, and management targets annualized operating expenses below $100M by Q4 2026 with significant operating cash flow improvement. Insider Jorey Chernett (14.1% holder) has purchased over $1.36M in shares at $1.23-1.41 in late March 2026, signaling deep-value conviction at the current $99M market cap [4]. The mental health treatment market is growing secularly, and NeuroStar's FDA-cleared, non-drug approach addresses treatment-resistant patients underserved by pharmaceuticals.
The bear case is severe. D/E of 344% and net losses of $39M on $149M revenue show a company drowning in acquisition debt with no near-term path to GAAP profitability [5]. Forward EPS of -$0.32 means losses continue into 2026. The largest independent shareholder has publicly called the Greenbrook synergies 'illusory' and demanded a strategic review including potential sale of the TMS business — this is not an endorsement of the status quo despite his buying [3]. CEO Sullivan retired in March 2026 and the CFO departed, creating a leadership vacuum during the critical integration period. Glassdoor ratings of 2.8/5 suggest cultural dysfunction that new CEO Reuvers must address. The stock has fallen 74% from its 52-week high of $5.55 to $1.42, and at $99M market cap with $344% D/E, dilutive financing risk is material. Wall Street Zen downgraded to Sell in March 2026 [3].
Key Catalysts
  1. Greenbrook integration cost synergies — management targets annualized opex below $100M by Q4 2026 and significant operating cash flow improvement, which could demonstrate the acquisition thesis [1]
  2. New CEO Dan Reuvers brings 30+ years of medtech experience with a track record in revenue growth, market expansion, and acquisition integration — fresh leadership could reset execution [2]
  3. Strategic alternatives review demanded by 14.1% shareholder Chernett could unlock value through a sale of the TMS device business or the entire company to a larger medtech acquirer [3]
Key Risks
  1. Balance sheet distress — D/E of 344%, net loss of $39M in 2025, and negative forward EPS create dilutive financing risk if cash flow improvement targets are not met [5]
  2. Boardroom conflict — largest shareholder publicly calling Greenbrook synergies 'illusory' and demanding strategic alternatives review creates governance uncertainty and management distraction [3]
  3. Leadership vacuum — CEO transition (Sullivan to Reuvers), CFO departure, and Glassdoor 2.8/5 culture ratings create execution risk during the critical Greenbrook integration period [2][3]
