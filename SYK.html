Company Profile
Healthcare ·Medical Devices ·NYSE ·United States
Stryker Corporation is a global medical technology leader operating in two segments: MedSurg and Neurotechnology, and Orthopaedics and Spine. Products include surgical instruments, endoscopy systems, neurovascular devices, Mako robotic-arm assisted surgery systems, hip and knee joint replacements, trauma and extremities devices, and spine products. Revenue exceeded $25 billion in 2025, making Stryker one of the largest pure-play medical device companies globally. Founded in 1941, headquartered in Portage, Michigan.

Investment Thesis
Stryker is a best-in-class medical device compounder delivering 11.2% revenue growth to $25.1B in FY2025 with 17 consecutive years of dividend increases and 8-9.5% organic growth guidance for FY2026 [1][2]. The Mako robotic surgery platform (2,500+ installations) is the #1 orthopedic robotics system with multi-year growth runway as hospital adoption accelerates. SmartHospital ecosystem extends the competitive moat into connected surgery data analytics. CEO Lobo's 'category leadership' strategy ensures Stryker holds #1 or #2 positions in every market it serves, driving consistent above-market growth. Q1 2026 was temporarily impacted by a March cybersecurity incident, but management reaffirmed full-year guidance and expects accelerated growth in H2 as production normalizes [1].
The primary concern is valuation — at 22.4x forward earnings and PEG of 2.08, Stryker is priced for perfection in a medical device sector facing margin pressure [4]. Profit margin of 12.92% is below the IOWN target and reflects manufacturing-intensive operations that limit earnings leverage. The March 2026 cybersecurity incident by the 'Handala' hacker group disrupted global logistics and order fulfillment, pressuring Q1 gross margin to 63.3% and raising questions about IT infrastructure resilience [1][3]. The D/E ratio of 73% and aggressive M&A strategy (Wright Medical, recent bolt-ons) carry integration execution risk. Dividend yield of 1.19% is below typical dividend stock thresholds, though the 17-year growth streak and 39.6% payout ratio provide safety and room for continued increases. Healthcare reimbursement reform or hospital capital spending slowdowns remain sector-level headwinds.
