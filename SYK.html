Investment Thesis
Stryker is one of the highest-quality compounders in medtech, delivering four consecutive years of double-digit revenue growth to $25.1B in FY2025 with adjusted EPS of $13.63 (up 11.8%) [1]. The Mako surgical robot platform with 3,000+ installations and 1M+ procedures performed creates a recurring ecosystem of implant pull-through and service revenue that deepens over time [1][2]. The $4.9B Inari Medical acquisition drove 50.6% growth in the vascular segment, opening a new multi-billion-dollar addressable market [1]. 2026 guidance of 8-9.5% organic growth and $14.90-$15.10 adjusted EPS reflects sustained momentum. With 34 years of consecutive dividends, 30 years of growth, and a near-perfect infrastructure score of 10, Stryker combines elite growth characteristics with dividend reliability. The Mako RPS handheld robotic system completing first total knee cases represents the next wave of surgical robotics innovation [1].
The bear case centers on valuation — at a forward P/E of 20x, Stryker is priced for perfection with limited margin of safety if growth disappoints. CEO Lobo's estimated $400M tariff impact in 2026 could pressure margins, particularly as the company absorbs the Inari Medical integration [1]. MSCI rates Stryker below average on ESG, citing environmental misalignment with climate objectives and past ethical concerns including bribery allegations [3]. Profit margin of 12.9% is below medtech peers, reflecting the capital intensity of manufacturing. The D/E of 73% has crept above the target level due to the Inari acquisition debt. If Mako adoption slows or competitive robotic platforms gain traction, the premium valuation would compress rapidly. The cyberattack-related global network disruption highlights operational risk in an increasingly connected medical device company [3].
