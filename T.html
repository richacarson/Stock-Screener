Investment Thesis
AT&T offers a compelling total return story with a 3.9% yield well-covered at a 27% payout ratio, $18B+ FCF guided for 2026, and $45B+ in planned shareholder returns through 2028 [1]. The fiber expansion to 40M+ locations by year-end 2026 drives 17% consumer fiber revenue growth, while convergence with wireless (42% of fiber households bundling) reduces churn and increases wallet share. At 9.3x trailing P/E, the stock is priced for no growth but is delivering mid-single-digit EBITDA growth accelerating to 5%+ by 2028.
The bear case includes persistent broadband subscriber losses (587,000 YoY decline) amid 5G fixed wireless competition from T-Mobile, and the $124% D/E ratio limits strategic flexibility [1]. Revenue growth of just 3.6% is modest, and adjusted EPS guidance of $2.25-2.35 implies negative YoY EPS growth despite operational improvements, reflecting the weight of debt service and capital intensity. The Lumen fiber integration adds complexity, and the company's dividend was cut 47% in 2022, meaning the 'income stock' thesis has already been broken once.
Key Catalysts
  1. Fiber expansion to 40M+ locations by YE2026 with 5M additions annually through 2030 drives sustainable mid-teens broadband revenue growth
  2. Conversion of free wireless lines to paid relationships in H2 2026 creates a material revenue tailwind for the wireless segment
  3. EBITDA growth acceleration from 3-4% in 2026 to 5%+ by 2028, combined with $45B+ shareholder returns, supports a total return thesis of 10%+ annually
Key Risks
  1. T-Mobile 5G fixed wireless continues to take broadband share — 587,000 net broadband subscriber losses in 2025 could accelerate if fiber rollout delays occur
  2. D/E of 124% limits financial flexibility; rising rates could increase refinancing costs on AT&T's ~$130B debt load
  3. Simplified all-inclusive broadband pricing warned to compress ARPU near-term, with improvement not expected until H2 2026
