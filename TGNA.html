Company Profile
Communication Services ·Broadcasting ·NYSE ·United States
TEGNA is a broadcast and digital media company that owns or operates 68 television stations in 54 U.S. markets. Originally part of Gannett Co. (founded 1906), TEGNA was spun off in 2015. The company operates local news, digital advertising platform Premion, multicast networks True Crime Network and Quest, and streaming services. As of March 19, 2026, TEGNA operates as a subsidiary of Nexstar Media Group following a $6.2 billion acquisition, though a federal court has temporarily halted integration activities.

Investment Thesis
TEGNA offers a near-term merger arbitrage opportunity with Nexstar's $22/share acquisition providing a price ceiling and floor dynamic. The stock trades at $20.03, implying ~10% upside to the deal price if the court-ordered integration halt is resolved in Nexstar's favor [1][3]. The underlying business generates meaningful free cash flow ($231M annually) with a 2.5% dividend yield and 37% payout ratio, providing income while the merger situation resolves. Premion's digital advertising platform provides exposure to CTV/streaming advertising growth. At 11.6x forward P/E, the stock is cheap even as a standalone broadcaster if the deal collapses [4].
The bear case is dominated by legal and structural risks. A federal judge has blocked Nexstar's integration of TEGNA with a preliminary injunction issued April 17, 2026, and attorneys general argue the merger consolidates too much control of local TV markets [3]. If the deal unwinds, TEGNA reverts to a standalone broadcaster with declining revenue (-18.9% YoY), aggressive cost-cutting that has gutted newsroom capabilities, and a CEO whose primary strategic vision was to sell the company. Revenue decline of 18.9% reflects both the post-political-cycle ad hangover and structural secular decline in linear TV advertising. AI disruption poses existential threat to local TV news — AI-generated content, automated weather, and chatbot-driven news aggregation threaten to disintermediate TEGNA's audience relationship. The firing of investigative journalists and fact-checkers under CEO Steib raises reputational and regulatory risk, particularly as FCC scrutiny of the Nexstar deal intensifies [2][3].
Key Catalysts
  1. Resolution of the federal court injunction halting Nexstar integration — if the appeal succeeds, shareholders receive the remaining ~10% spread to the $22/share deal price [3]
  2. Political advertising revenue cycle returning in 2026 midterms could boost revenue from the depressed 2025 off-cycle base [1]
  3. Premion digital advertising platform growth in CTV and streaming ad markets, providing secular growth offset to linear TV decline [1]
Key Risks
  1. Federal court injunction could unwind the Nexstar deal entirely if antitrust litigation succeeds, removing the $22/share price floor and leaving TEGNA as a weakened standalone broadcaster [3]
  2. Structural decline in linear TV broadcasting as cord-cutting accelerates and AI-driven news aggregation disintermediates local TV's audience relationship and advertising revenue [2]
  3. Reputational and regulatory risk from aggressive journalist layoffs and elimination of fact-checking team, potentially inviting FCC scrutiny and advertiser backlash during the sensitive merger review period [2][3]
