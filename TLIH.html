Company Profile
Industrials ·Industrial Distribution ·NASDAQ ·Singapore
Ten-League International Holdings is a Singapore-based provider of turnkey project solutions, selling and renting new and used heavy equipment and parts for the port, construction, civil engineering, and underground foundation industries. The company also offers engineering consultancy services including equipment retrofitting, upgrading, and modernization. Founded in 1998, the company IPO'd on Nasdaq in July 2025 and is currently facing delisting proceedings for non-compliance with the minimum bid price rule.

Investment Thesis
Ten-League International Holdings is a 27-year-old Singapore heavy equipment company benefiting from a structural infrastructure investment cycle. H1 2025 revenue grew 21.6% to S$37.7M with net income surging 269% to S$2.4M, driven by Changi Airport Terminal 5 construction demand [1]. FY2025 guidance of S$75-76M revenue (28-30% growth) and S$5.0-5.5M net income (165-192% growth) shows accelerating momentum [1]. At a P/E of 2.5x and market cap of just $7.5M, the stock appears extremely cheap on a pure earnings basis. ROE of 53% demonstrates efficient capital deployment in a capital-light equipment resale and rental model. The physical-world business is immune to AI disruption and positioned for multi-year infrastructure tailwinds in Singapore.
The bear case is severe and dominates. The stock is facing Nasdaq delisting for non-compliance with the $1.00 minimum bid price rule — it has traded below $1 since shortly after its July 2025 IPO, and the company is appealing the determination [4]. Daily liquidity averages just $543K, making institutional investment impossible and creating substantial execution risk for any position. D/E of 381% signals a heavily leveraged balance sheet with potential solvency concerns. Market cap of $7.5M makes this a nano-cap with zero analyst coverage and extreme informational asymmetry. The controlled company structure (80% insider ownership) means minority shareholders have limited governance rights [4]. Revenue concentration in Singapore infrastructure creates geographic risk — any slowdown in Changi T5 or government infrastructure spending would directly impact results. Profit margin of just 5.6% leaves minimal cushion for operational disruption [5].
