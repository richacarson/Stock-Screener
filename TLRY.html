Investment Thesis
Tilray is the largest cannabis company globally by revenue, having built a diversified CPG platform spanning cannabis ($64.8M Q3 revenue, +19% YoY), craft beer, spirits, and wellness. Record Q3 FY2026 revenue of $207M with 11% organic growth and 73% international cannabis growth demonstrate the platform's scalability [1]. German cannabis legalization is a proven revenue catalyst, and the $265M cash position with low D/E of 21% provides financial flexibility to capitalize on further European and potential US regulatory changes. Management reconfirmed FY2026 adjusted EBITDA guidance of $62-72M, representing 13-31% growth YoY [1].
Despite revenue growth, Tilray has never generated sustained profitability—$1.34B in TTM net losses on $858M revenue reflects massive goodwill impairments from systematically overpaying for acquisitions [2]. Share count increased 26% YoY, destroying per-share value and signaling management's inability to fund growth from operations. Free cash flow remains negative at -$73.8M [2]. The beverage pivot (SweetWater, Montauk, Breckenridge) has yet to prove synergistic with cannabis operations, and management governance is a persistent concern—shareholder lawsuits allege misleading proxy statements about share limit votes and merger-related fraud [4]. US cannabis legalization remains speculative, and without it, the total addressable market is constrained by fragmented international regulation.
Key Catalysts
  1. German and European cannabis market expansion driving international cannabis revenue beyond the current 73% YoY growth trajectory, with Germany's recreational market still in early stages
  2. US federal cannabis rescheduling or legalization enabling Tilray to deploy its existing CPG infrastructure into the world's largest cannabis market
  3. Achievement of consistent positive adjusted EBITDA toward the $62-72M FY2026 guidance, proving the path from revenue growth to sustainable profitability
Key Risks
  1. Chronic shareholder dilution (26% YoY share increase to 119M shares) destroys per-share value and signals management's structural inability to fund growth from operations
  2. $1.34B TTM net losses driven by goodwill impairments indicate systematic overpayment for acquisitions (SweetWater, Montauk, Breckenridge) with questionable strategic fit and integration challenges
  3. Shareholder lawsuits alleging proxy statement fraud and merger-related fraud signal persistent corporate governance weaknesses under CEO Irwin Simon's leadership
