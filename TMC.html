Company Profile
Basic Materials ·Other Industrial Metals & Mining ·NASDAQ ·Canada
TMC the metals company is a pre-revenue deep-sea mining explorer seeking to collect polymetallic nodules containing nickel, cobalt, copper, and manganese from the Clarion-Clipperton Zone in the Pacific Ocean. The company holds exploration contracts through its NORI, TOML, and Marawa subsidiaries and is pursuing U.S. regulatory approval for commercial production targeted for Q4 2027.

Investment Thesis
TMC represents a binary outcome bet on deep-sea mineral extraction that could unlock a $23.6B NPV resource base. NOAA's March 2026 compliance determination moves the company closer to the first-ever commercial deep-sea mining license under U.S. law, with production targeted for Q4 2027 [1]. The 65,000 sq km CCZ exploration area contains polymetallic nodules rich in nickel, cobalt, copper, and manganese — metals essential for EV batteries and energy infrastructure that currently face Chinese supply chain dominance. Strategic partners including Korea Zinc and the Hess family provide credibility and capital access [1]. At a $2.3B market cap with $162M liquidity, TMC offers asymmetric upside if commercial production materializes at the projected 43% EBITDA margins and $600/dry ton economics [1]. Wedbush maintains a Buy rating.
The bear case is severe and multi-dimensional. TMC is pre-revenue with cumulative losses exceeding $500M, and CEO Barron's prior deep-sea mining venture (Nautilus Minerals) went bankrupt, leaving Papua New Guinea with $100M+ in losses [2]. The unilateral U.S. permitting approach bypasses UNCLOS and faces opposition from 32 nations — France's ocean minister called it 'piracy' — creating existential legal risk if a future administration reverses course [3]. Sixty major corporations including BMW, Panasonic, and Renault have pledged to avoid deep-sea mined minerals, potentially limiting TMC's addressable market [3]. Environmental organizations have filed SEC complaints alleging material misrepresentation of risks [3]. Battery chemistry evolution toward LFP and sodium-ion formulations that eliminate cobalt, nickel, and manganese could erode demand for the very minerals TMC plans to extract. The $4.27 stock price is down 33% from its 50-day SMA of $6.38, reflecting the market's growing skepticism [4].
Key Catalysts

Key Risks

  2. Battery chemistry disruption — rapid adoption of LFP and sodium-ion batteries that eliminate cobalt, nickel, and manganese could structurally reduce demand for TMC's target minerals [2]
  3. Environmental and reputational risk — SEC complaints, Greenpeace campaigns, corporate boycotts from 60+ companies, and investor divestments (Storebrand) could limit financing access and customer base [3]
