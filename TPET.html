Company Profile
Energy ·Oil & Gas E&P ·NYSEAMERICAN ·United States
Trio Petroleum Corp. is a micro-cap oil and gas exploration and development company. Its flagship property is the South Salinas project, an 82.75% working interest in approximately 9,300 acres in Monterey, California. The company is transitioning to Canadian heavy-oil assets in Saskatchewan. Incorporated in 2021 and headquartered in Malibu, California.

Investment Thesis
Trio Petroleum is a speculative micro-cap oil and gas company with a $14.7M market cap, one full-time employee, and ~$400K in trailing twelve-month revenue [1][2]. The company is pivoting from California properties to Canadian heavy-oil assets in Saskatchewan, funded by an $18.6M ATM offering that eased going-concern pressures but significantly diluted shareholders. Revenue grew over 1,000% YoY, but this is from a near-zero base and does not yet represent meaningful scale.
The investment case for TPET is purely speculative — if Canadian heavy-oil production ramps successfully and oil prices remain supportive, there is upside from a very low base [1]. However, the company lacks virtually every quality IOWN looks for: no competitive moat, no technology differentiation, one employee, negative margins, leadership instability (terminated President and COO in 2025), and a history of dilutive financing [1][3]. The sub-$0.50 stock price and high share volume make this a penny stock with lottery-ticket characteristics rather than an investable company for a quality-focused portfolio.
Key Catalysts
  1. Canadian heavy-oil asset transition in Saskatchewan could provide production growth from a low base if execution succeeds [1]
  2. $18.6M ATM raise provides operating runway and removes near-term going-concern risk [1]
  3. Rising oil prices or a commodities supercycle would disproportionately benefit a leveraged micro-cap E&P [2]
Key Risks
  1. Sub-$15M market cap with one employee, -$7.3M annual net loss, and 0.50 current ratio create existential risk — further dilutive financing is highly likely [1][2]
  2. No analyst coverage, no forward earnings estimates, and no competitive moat in a commodity business dominated by companies 10,000x larger [1]
  3. Leadership instability — CEO change in 2024, terminated President and COO in January 2025 — raises governance concerns for an already fragile operation [3]
