Company Profile
Technology ·Semiconductors ·NYSE ·Taiwan
Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) is the world's largest dedicated semiconductor foundry, manufacturing over 90% of the world's most advanced chips (3nm and below). The company provides wafer fabrication for customers including NVIDIA, Apple, AMD, Broadcom, and Qualcomm across high-performance computing, smartphones, IoT, automotive, and consumer electronics applications.

Existential Flexibility

Existential Flexibility (1/2): TSMC's founding itself was an act of existential flexibility — Chang created an entirely new business model (pure-play foundry) that didn't exist before [2]. More recently, TSMC's geographic diversification with the $65B Arizona fab, Japan fab, and European partnerships represents meaningful strategic adaptation to geopolitical realities [3]. However, TSMC's core business model hasn't fundamentally changed since 1987 — it remains a foundry, albeit one that has evolved with technology. The evolution has been continuous improvement rather than transformative reinvention.

Investment Thesis
TSMC is the most critical company in the global technology supply chain, manufacturing over 90% of the world's advanced semiconductors and serving as the irreplaceable manufacturing backbone for the AI revolution [2]. Q4 2025 revenue of $33.7B (up 25.5% YoY in USD) with 62.3% gross margin and 48.3% net margin demonstrates extraordinary pricing power and execution [1]. Management guides ~30% USD revenue growth for 2026, with AI accelerator revenue growing at a 54-56% CAGR through 2029 — the company is the largest direct beneficiary of AI infrastructure buildout. At 19x forward P/E with 30%+ revenue growth and a forward PEG of ~0.54, TSMC is compellingly valued relative to its growth trajectory. The $3.07T TWD cash position, 35.1% ROE, and multiple insider purchases in March 2026 confirm financial strength and executive conviction [4][5].
The bear case centers on geopolitical concentration risk and cyclicality. Over 90% of TSMC's advanced chip production remains in Taiwan, creating existential exposure to cross-strait tensions — a Chinese blockade or invasion would disrupt the global technology supply chain catastrophically [3]. The $65B Arizona fab and Japan expansion mitigate this but cannot replicate Taiwan's manufacturing ecosystem in the near term. The $52-56B capex plan for 2026 creates execution risk if AI demand disappoints or customers delay orders due to macro uncertainty. TSMC's effective tax rate is rising to 17-18% in 2026 from lower historical levels, compressing earnings growth relative to revenue growth [1]. Trailing P/E of 33x prices in significant growth expectations, and any deceleration in AI spending could trigger a meaningful multiple compression from current levels. Additionally, U.S.-China export restrictions on advanced chips could limit TSMC's ability to serve Chinese customers, removing a meaningful revenue pool [3].
Key Catalysts
  1. AI accelerator revenue CAGR of 54-56% through 2029, with HPC/AI already representing 60% of quarterly revenue and growing as hyperscaler AI capex continues expanding [1]
  2. 2nm node ramp in 2025-2026 with A16 backside power rail technology and System-on-Wafer (SoW) for AI data centers, extending TSMC's 2-3 year technology lead over Samsung and Intel [2]
  3. Geographic diversification with $65B Arizona fab and Japan facility, reducing geopolitical concentration risk while capturing U.S. CHIPS Act subsidies and tariff advantages [3]
Key Risks
  1. Taiwan geopolitical risk — over 90% of advanced chip production on a small island in the South China Sea, with Chinese military exercises and cross-strait tensions creating tail risk of catastrophic supply disruption [3]
  2. AI demand cyclicality — if hyperscaler AI capex decelerates or customers pull back on orders, TSMC's $52-56B capex commitment could result in overcapacity and margin compression [1]
  3. U.S.-China technology decoupling — export restrictions on advanced chips to China could eliminate a meaningful revenue stream, while tariffs on Taiwan imports could pressure customer economics and order volumes [3]
