Company Profile
Technology ·Semiconductors ·NYSE ·Taiwan
Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) is the world's largest dedicated semiconductor foundry, manufacturing chips for fabless design companies including NVIDIA, Apple, AMD, Qualcomm, and Google. TSMC invented the pure-play foundry model in 1987 and holds approximately 60% of global foundry market share and 90%+ share of advanced nodes (sub-7nm). Led by Chairman and CEO C.C. Wei, the company operates fabs in Taiwan, the US (Arizona), and Japan. Market cap approximately $2.0 trillion.

Investment Thesis
TSMC is the most important company in the AI supply chain, manufacturing virtually every cutting-edge AI chip and benefiting from insatiable demand. Q1 2026 delivered $35.9B revenue (+40.6% YoY in USD) with gross margin expanding to 66.2% and operating margin to 58.1%, both exceeding guidance [1]. Management raised full-year 2026 USD revenue growth guidance to >30% and guided Q2 at $39.0-40.2B, confirming sustained AI-driven momentum. The balance sheet is impregnable—$105.8B cash against $34.2B debt (D/E 18%)—supporting $38-42B annual capex investment in next-generation manufacturing [5]. ROE of 36.21%, net profit margin of 46.5%, and 90%+ advanced node market share create a near-monopolistic position in the most critical technology supply chain on Earth. At 23.14x forward P/E with 35% revenue growth, the valuation is reasonable for a company with TSMC's growth and quality profile.
The dominant risk is geopolitical—TSMC's concentration in Taiwan creates an existential vulnerability to cross-strait tensions with China [4]. A military conflict, blockade, or even sustained tension could disrupt the global chip supply chain and TSMC's operations. The $40B+ Arizona fab investment partially mitigates this but represents only a fraction of TSMC's Taiwan capacity and operates at higher costs, compressing margins. At $2.0T market cap and 33.6x trailing P/E, TSMC is priced for continued hypergrowth—any deceleration in AI infrastructure spending (customer capex fatigue, model efficiency improvements reducing compute needs, or a macro recession) could trigger significant multiple compression. The cyclical history of semiconductors warns against extrapolating peak growth rates. Taiwan's energy infrastructure constraints (dependence on LNG imports, renewable energy targets lagging schedule) could limit fab expansion capacity [4].
Key Catalysts
  1. Full-year 2026 USD revenue growth target of >30% driven by AI/HPC demand, with Q2 guidance of $39.0-40.2B implying sequential acceleration toward $160B+ annual revenue
  2. 2nm (N2) process volume production ramp with customer engagement from Apple, NVIDIA, and hyperscalers, enabling next-generation AI chip performance at higher margins
  3. CoWoS advanced packaging capacity expansion to meet surging AI chip demand, with TSMC as the sole supplier of critical packaging technology for large AI accelerators
Key Risks
  1. Geopolitical risk remains existential—Taiwan Strait tensions, potential Chinese military action, or a selective quarantine could disrupt TSMC's core manufacturing operations, which represent 80%+ of global advanced chip capacity
  2. AI infrastructure spending cyclicality—customer capex fatigue among hyperscalers, advances in model efficiency reducing compute requirements, or a macro recession could decelerate the >30% revenue growth that justifies the $2.0T valuation
  3. Arizona fab cost premium (estimated 30-50% higher than Taiwan) and Taiwan energy infrastructure constraints (LNG import dependency, lagging renewable targets) could compress margins and limit capacity expansion over time
