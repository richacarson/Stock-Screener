Company Profile
Communication Services ·Telecom Services ·NYSE ·Mexico
Grupo Televisa, S.A.B. owns and operates cable companies and provides direct-to-home satellite pay television systems in Mexico and internationally. Operating through Cable and Sky segments, the company offers television subscription, internet, telephone, mobile, and advertising services, as well as data and long-distance solutions through its fiber-optic network. It also holds a significant stake in TelevisaUnivision, the world's leading Spanish-language media company.

Finite vs Infinite Game — Sinek (25%)

Grupo Televisa has the heritage of an infinite game player — a 57-year history as Mexico's dominant media conglomerate, with the Azcarraga family providing generational continuity and co-CEOs serving for 27 years. However, the company's failure to adapt to cord-cutting, its massive subscriber losses, and the junk credit downgrade suggest a company that clung to its legacy business model too long and is now scrambling to pivot to fiber broadband, which is a reactive transformation rather than a proactive one driven by a Just Cause.
Just Cause

Televisa's historical purpose — connecting Mexican families through entertainment and telecommunications — was genuine and served the nation for decades as the country's dominant media company [2]. However, this cause has not been validated through consistent strategic decisions in the modern era. The company held onto its declining satellite business too long while competitors invested in fiber and streaming, suggesting the cause was subordinated to protecting legacy revenue streams. The TelevisaUnivision merger preserved the content legacy, but the retained telecom business lacks a compelling purpose beyond infrastructure operation. Score: 1 weighted point.

Investment Thesis
Grupo Televisa offers a deep value opportunity for investors willing to bet on the fiber-to-the-home transformation in Mexico's telecom market. At $2.92 per share (75% below 5-year highs), the stock is priced for near-terminal decline, but the aggressive FTTH CapEx plan targeting 15-16 million homes (75% footprint coverage) by year-end 2026 could stabilize and eventually grow broadband revenue [1]. Forward P/E of 12.8x is cheap if the company achieves profitability through fiber subscriber growth and cost rationalization. Adjusted EBITDA of $1.6B grew 2% YoY despite the revenue decline, and excluding political advertising and FX, EBITDA grew 7% YoY, demonstrating that cost cuts and DTC streaming scale are generating margin improvement [1]. The TelevisaUnivision stake provides optionality on the world's largest Spanish-language streaming platform (ViX).
The bear case is severe and multifaceted. Televisa is a company in structural decline — revenue has fallen for four consecutive years from MXN 73.9B (2021) to MXN 62.3B (2024), net losses of MXN -8.3B in FY2024 reflect deep unprofitability, and the stock has lost 75% of its value in five years [3]. Moody's downgraded Televisa to junk in July 2025 and Fitch to BB+ in 2025, citing higher-than-anticipated subscriber losses and weaker-than-expected financial performance [3]. The Sky satellite business is in terminal decline (346,600 disconnections in Q2 2025 alone, 16.3% YoY revenue drop) and management has explicitly stated they expect it to continue shrinking [3]. The FTTH pivot requires investing 25% of sales in CapEx — a massive commitment for a junk-rated company with negative ROE of -8.3% and -15.6% profit margins. The Mexican peso's volatility adds FX risk for USD-denominated investors, and competition from América Móvil's dominant fiber network and global streaming platforms creates execution risk for the transformation strategy. The D/E of 89.2% limits financial flexibility, and the company's ability to fund the fiber buildout without further credit deterioration is uncertain [1].
