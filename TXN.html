Company Profile
Technology ·Semiconductors ·NASDAQ ·United States
Texas Instruments Incorporated designs, manufactures, and sells semiconductors to electronics designers and manufacturers globally. The company operates through Analog and Embedded Processing segments. Analog products include power management, signal chain, and high-volume analog & logic chips. Embedded Processing encompasses microcontrollers, processors, and connectivity solutions. Founded in 1951, headquartered in Dallas, Texas.

Finite vs Infinite Game — Sinek (25%)
Mindset: INFINITE Overall: 8 /10
Texas Instruments is one of the purest infinite game players in the semiconductor industry. The company's counter-cyclical investment of $14B+ in 300mm fab capacity during the 2023-2024 semiconductor downturn — when revenue fell 25% peak-to-trough from $20B to $15.6B — exemplifies 'thinking in decades, not quarters.' The 70+ year history spanning the invention of the integrated circuit through multiple business model transformations demonstrates remarkable institutional longevity and strategic clarity, though the cost-cutting culture noted in employee reviews creates some tension with the infinite game ideal.

Existential Flexibility
10
Existential Flexibility (2/2): TI has fundamentally reinvented itself multiple times over seven decades — from defense electronics to consumer calculators to digital signal processors to analog/embedded semiconductors, each time divesting large revenue streams to focus on higher-return opportunities [3]. The exit from the mobile processor market (ceding the smartphone chip race to Qualcomm despite billions in sunk investment) and the divestiture of defense electronics demonstrate willingness to abandon established positions when they no longer serve the long-term mission. The current $14B+ bet on 300mm fabs — when most analog competitors remain on 200mm — is another transformative move that will fundamentally reshape the company's cost structure and competitive position [3].
Courage to Lead
10
Courage to Lead (2/2): TI's decision to invest $14B+ in new 300mm manufacturing capacity during the 2023-2024 semiconductor downturn — when revenue fell from $20B to $15.6B and free cash flow compressed dramatically — is a textbook example of counter-cyclical courage [1][3]. Management maintained aggressive CapEx despite Wall Street pressure to cut spending and return capital, betting on long-term analog demand growth when the short-term cost was real and immediate. The earlier decision to exit the mobile processor market, walking away from billions in smartphone chip revenue to focus on higher-return analog markets, required similar conviction to sacrifice near-term revenue for long-term strategic clarity.

Investment Thesis
Texas Instruments delivered Q1 2026 revenue of $4.83B, up 19% YoY, with Analog revenue surging 22% to $3.92B at a 41.7% operating margin and Embedded Processing growing 12% to $723M, as the semiconductor cycle recovery accelerates across industrial and data center end markets [1]. ROE of 32.35%, profit margin of 29.11%, and the world's largest analog semiconductor market position (~19% share) create a durable competitive moat across 80,000+ products, while the $14B+ investment in 300mm wafer fabs is now delivering structural cost-per-transistor advantages that will widen as utilization ramps through 2030 [3][5]. Q2 2026 guidance of $5.00-5.40B implies continued acceleration, D/E of 84% reflects a manageable balance sheet, and 23 consecutive years of dividend growth demonstrate capital return discipline even through severe cyclical downturns.
The bear case centers on premium valuation and cyclical risk. At 34.98x forward earnings and a PEG of 1.67, TXN is priced for sustained recovery that leaves limited margin of safety — any demand deceleration or inventory correction could trigger meaningful multiple compression from the current valuation [5]. The 5-year revenue CAGR of just 4.1% reveals the cyclicality risk: revenue swung from $20B (FY2022) to $15.6B (FY2024) and is only now approaching prior peaks at $17.68B [5]. The payout ratio of 94.13% on dividends is stretched, leaving virtually no buffer during the next downturn, and net insider selling of $55M with CFO Lizardi reducing his position by 35.8% signals that management may view the stock as fully valued [6]. The Inspiration score of 4/10 reflects a cost-cutting culture with declining headcount and an uninspiring corporate mission, while geopolitical risk from U.S.-China semiconductor tensions could impact TI's significant China revenue exposure.
