Company Profile
Consumer Cyclical ·Apparel Manufacturing ·NYSE ·United States
Under Armour, Inc. develops, markets, and distributes branded performance apparel, footwear, and accessories for men, women, and youth. The company operates through North America, EMEA, Asia-Pacific, and Latin America segments, selling through wholesale channels and direct-to-consumer (DTC) via owned retail stores and e-commerce. Under the leadership of founder Kevin Plank (who returned as CEO), the company is executing a multi-year restructuring plan targeting $305M in total program costs. Founded in 1996 and headquartered in Baltimore, Maryland.

Investment Thesis
Under Armour is a turnaround story trading at $5.10 per share with a $2.2B market cap, down dramatically from its peak. The company's FY2026 revenue declined 4% to $5.0B with adjusted EPS of only $0.12, but the $305M restructuring plan is designed to stabilize the brand and restore profitability by FY2027 [1]. International revenue grew 4% (EMEA up 9%), suggesting the brand retains value outside the struggling North American market. At a forward P/E of 20.8x on depressed earnings, significant upside exists if the restructuring succeeds and margins normalize — the company generated $5.9B in revenue just three years ago with positive margins.
The bear case is substantial. Under Armour's revenue has declined for three consecutive fiscal years from $5.9B to $5.0B, and FY2027 guidance calls for continued slight declines including a 1-point headwind from the Curry brand exit [1]. Profit margins are deeply negative (-10%) with negative free cash flow of -$521M, and D/E of 137% with only $309M in cash raises balance sheet concerns. The founder-CEO's return after two failed successors creates execution risk — Plank's micromanagement philosophy and the organizational instability of three CEO changes in four years undermine turnaround credibility. Competitively, Under Armour occupies an uncomfortable middle ground: too expensive for value consumers, insufficiently aspirational for premium athletes, squeezed by Nike, Adidas, New Balance, On Running, and Hoka from multiple directions [2].
Key Catalysts
  1. $305M restructuring plan expected to be substantially complete by December 2026, potentially restoring positive operating margins and enabling reinvestment in brand-building initiatives
  2. International revenue growth (EMEA +9%, Latin America +9% in FY2026) provides a pathway to geographic diversification away from the declining North American market
  3. Forward P/E of 20.8x on trough earnings implies significant multiple expansion opportunity if turnaround succeeds and margins normalize to historical 5-8% levels
Key Risks
  1. Three consecutive years of revenue decline with FY2027 guided down again — turnaround may fail to stabilize revenue as competitive pressure from Nike, Adidas, On Running, and Hoka intensifies
  2. Negative profit margin (-10%), negative free cash flow (-$521M), and D/E of 137% create financial fragility during a restructuring that needs investment to succeed
  3. Founder-CEO Kevin Plank's micromanagement philosophy and three CEO changes in four years create organizational instability that undermines the trust and empowerment needed for brand turnaround

AI Resilience (25%)
AI Resilience MODERATE
6
AI Risk Protection (5/9): Under Armour's core business — designing, manufacturing, and selling physical athletic apparel and footwear — has meaningful protection from AI disruption because consumers need physical products, and brand equity, athlete endorsements, and retail relationships provide moats AI cannot directly replicate. However, AI tools are increasingly disrupting apparel design cycles (generative design), marketing (AI-generated content replacing expensive ad campaigns), and retail distribution (AI-powered personalization). Smaller competitors using AI-first design and marketing approaches could compress UA's already-thin margins further. The DTC e-commerce channel faces AI-powered shopping assistants that could erode brand loyalty [2]. AI Opportunity (1/1): UA hired a dedicated head of AI and advanced analytics and is deploying generative AI across operations including supply chain optimization, inventory management, and consumer personalization — early-stage but with measurable operational investment [2].
