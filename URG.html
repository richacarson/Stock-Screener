Company Profile
Energy ·Uranium ·NYSE ·United States
Ur-Energy is a U.S. uranium mining company operating the Lost Creek in-situ recovery (ISR) facility in Wyoming and developing the Shirley Basin ISR project. The company holds 31.4 million pounds of combined uranium resources and has a delivery schedule of 1.3 million pounds for 2026 across eight sales agreements.

Investment Thesis
Ur-Energy offers leveraged exposure to the uranium bull thesis at $1.42/share, with domestic U.S. production increasingly valued as data center demand drives nuclear renaissance [1]. The Shirley Basin project starting production in Q1 2026 doubles the company's operational capacity, and 1.3M-pound delivery schedule at $61+/lb average prices provides revenue visibility. Combined uranium resources of 31.4 million pounds at cash costs of $42-50/lb create significant option value if uranium prices sustain above $80/lb, and the $123.9M cash position funds development without near-term equity dilution [1].
The bear case is fundamental — Ur-Energy has never generated consistent profits, revenue declined 19% in 2025 despite higher uranium prices, and the net loss deepened [1]. D/E of 109.6% from the convertible note creates potential dilution overhang, and the 20.3x forward P/E on speculative earnings estimates prices in execution that has not been demonstrated. Uranium prices are volatile and subject to geopolitical supply disruption (Kazakhstan, Canada), enrichment bottlenecks, and policy changes that could compress spot pricing below $60/lb [2]. The ISR production method depends on favorable hydrogeology, and Shirley Basin startup risk could delay revenue contribution beyond Q1 2026.
Key Catalysts
  1. Shirley Basin production startup in Q1 2026 doubling operational capacity and demonstrating the company's ability to develop and operate multiple ISR facilities simultaneously [1]
  2. Uranium price appreciation above $100/lb driven by AI data center nuclear demand, global reactor restarts, and constrained mine supply [1]
  3. U.S. government policy support for domestic uranium production through potential import restrictions, strategic reserve purchases, or HALEU development incentives [2]
Key Risks
  1. Shirley Basin startup delays or cost overruns that could exhaust cash reserves and force dilutive financing before the facility achieves commercial production rates [1]
  2. Uranium price decline below $60/lb would make both Lost Creek and Shirley Basin marginally economic at best, given all-in costs of $43-50/lb [1]
  3. Convertible note conversion dilution — the $120M convertible note could significantly expand the share count at higher stock prices, capping per-share upside [2]
