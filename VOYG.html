Company Profile
Industrials ·Aerospace & Defense ·NYSE ·United States
Voyager Technologies is a defense technology and space solutions company operating through three segments: Defense & National Security (solid propulsion, signal intelligence, radiation-hardened communications, AI-powered edge computing), Space Solutions (in-space propulsion, Bishop Airlock on ISS, space science services), and Starlab Space Stations (commercial space station development). Founded in 2019 and headquartered in Denver, Colorado.

Investment Thesis
Voyager Technologies is a high-growth defense and space technology company with record backlog of $265.6M and raised 2026 revenue guidance of $225-$255M (35-53% growth) fueled by five strategic acquisitions in 2025 and surging defense demand [1]. The company's AI-powered edge computing (Latent AI partnership) and Space Domain Awareness (Palantir partnership) position it at the intersection of two secular tailwinds: increased defense spending and AI deployment in national security [2]. The Starlab commercial space station represents a multi-billion-dollar long-term optionality as the ISS approaches retirement. At $1.78B market cap with $166M revenue growing 24% YoY, VOYG trades as a high-multiple growth story with significant upside if execution continues and the path to profitability materializes [1].
The bear case is severe: Voyager is deeply unprofitable with widening losses — net income declined from -$25.4M (2023) to -$62.1M (2024) to -$104.8M (2025), and free cash flow of -$177.1M indicates the acquisition roll-up is consuming cash at an accelerating rate [5]. Profit margin of -63.0% and ROE of -56.7% show no near-term path to profitability. The five acquisitions in 2025 alone create enormous integration risk, and Glassdoor ratings (2.3/5 culture) suggest the organizational fabric is fraying under the pace [2]. Revenue concentration on government contracts (NASA, DoD) exposes the company to political shifts in defense and space funding priorities. The $466.8M debt load combined with negative operating cash flow means Voyager may need additional capital raises, diluting shareholders. Starlab's success depends on NASA funding commitments that remain uncertain. The negative forward P/E of -32x provides no traditional valuation anchor [5].
