Company Profile
Industrials ·Rental & Leasing Services ·NYSE ·United States
Vestis Corporation provides uniform rentals and workplace supplies in the United States and Canada. Spun off from Aramark in October 2023, the company offers uniform rental programs, managed restroom supply services, first-aid supplies, floor mats, towels, and linens to manufacturing, hospitality, retail, food service, automotive, and healthcare industries. Vestis is currently undergoing a business transformation under new CEO Jim Barber.
18,150 Employees ·www.vestis.com

Investment Thesis
Vestis is a turnaround story with significant risk and reward asymmetry. The stock has fallen ~75% from its post-spinoff highs, trading at $9.89 with a $1.3B market cap for a company generating $2.7B in revenue — approximately 0.5x revenue for a recurring-service business [5]. New CEO Jim Barber's transformation plan is showing early results: 7% plant productivity improvement, 12% customer complaint reduction, and 3% on-time delivery improvement in Q1 FY2026 [1]. FY2026 adjusted EBITDA guidance of $285-315M implies a 10.5-11.5% margin recovery trajectory. Activist investor Corvex Management (Keith Meister) holds 10%+ and provides external governance pressure for value creation. The uniform rental industry has favorable characteristics — recurring revenue, high switching costs, and essential service — that could reward patient investors if execution improves [4].
The bear case is severe. Vestis is losing customers at 5× the rate it acquires them, the Q1 FY2026 net loss of $(6.4M) marks continuing unprofitability, and the securities fraud lawsuit alleging leadership concealed 'systemic customer service failures costing millions weekly' creates ongoing legal liability [1][3]. D/E of 163% on a money-losing business creates financial fragility — free cash flow guidance of $50-60M barely covers the $18.5M annual dividend, leaving minimal room for deleveraging or investment. The company is on its third CEO in under two years (Scott → Holloman interim → Barber), signaling deep organizational dysfunction. Elis SA reportedly walked away from acquisition discussions, removing a potential floor on the stock price [3]. The transformation plan's $75M annual savings target must overcome a business that is structurally shrinking (revenue guided flat to down 2%), and margin recovery depends on operational improvements in an 18,000-employee service organization with damaged morale.
Key Catalysts
  1. Business transformation plan delivering $75M+ annual cost savings by end of FY2026, potentially restoring profitability and demonstrating operational discipline under CEO Jim Barber [1]
  2. Activist investor Corvex Management (10%+ ownership by Keith Meister) driving governance improvements and potential strategic alternatives including M&A [4]
  3. Adjusted EBITDA margin recovery toward 12%+ as plant productivity improvements (7% YoY) and customer complaint reduction (12% YoY) translate to stabilized revenue and lower operational costs [1]
Key Risks
  1. Customer attrition exceeding new business acquisition at a 5:1 ratio — if the company cannot stabilize its customer base, no amount of cost-cutting will offset structural revenue decline [2]
  2. Securities fraud litigation — the pending class action with judge findings that leadership 'hid the truth from investors' creates material legal liability and management distraction [3]
  3. Financial fragility — negative ROE, 163% D/E, and $50-60M free cash flow guidance leave almost no margin for error on dividend maintenance, debt service, and transformation investment [1][5]
