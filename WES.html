Company Profile
Energy ·Oil & Gas Midstream ·NYSE ·United States
Western Midstream Partners is a master limited partnership that develops and operates midstream energy assets primarily in the United States. The partnership gathers, compresses, treats, processes, and transports natural gas; gathers, stabilizes, and transports condensate, NGLs, and crude oil; and gathers and disposes of produced water. Operations are concentrated in the Delaware and DJ Basins with fee-based contracts that provide commodity price protection.

Investment Thesis
Western Midstream Partners offers a compelling 9.1% distribution yield backed by fee-based midstream infrastructure with improving fundamentals. The partnership guides 2026 adjusted EBITDA of $2.5-$2.7B (5% growth at midpoint) with DCF of $4.59-$5.08/unit, providing ~75% coverage of the $3.72 annualized distribution at the low end of guidance [1][2]. Revenue of $3.84B grew 11.1% YoY with ROE of 32.2% and profit margin of 30.0%, and the forward P/E of 11.0x offers significant value in a sector benefiting from AI-driven data center power demand [5]. The 18.3% 5-year distribution growth CAGR and recent increase to $0.93/quarter demonstrate management's commitment to returning capital. Fee-based contracts protect against commodity price volatility, and essential physical infrastructure creates a durable competitive moat [1].
The bear case centers on throughput headwinds, leverage, and structural MLP limitations. WES expects low-to-mid single-digit declines in crude oil and NGL throughput in 2026, and DJ Basin volumes could fall mid-to-high single digits — a concerning trend for a gathering-focused MLP whose revenue depends on production volumes [2]. D/E of 211.8% ($8.8B debt) creates significant financial risk if volumes decline faster than expected or if interest rates remain elevated [5]. Q4 2025 net income fell sharply to $187M despite revenue growth, with EPS declining 45.2% YoY — a potential signal of margin compression [1]. Dependency on Occidental Petroleum for a significant portion of volumes creates concentration risk tied to Oxy's production decisions. The MLP structure generates K-1 tax complexity and limits the investor base. The GAAP payout ratio of 121% (though covered by DCF) signals that reported earnings do not support the distribution level [5].
Key Catalysts
  1. Distribution increase to $3.72 annualized ($0.93/quarter) with 2026 DCF guidance of $4.59-$5.08/unit providing sustainable coverage and potential for further increases [2]
  2. AI-driven data center buildout increasing demand for natural gas-fired power generation, driving production growth in WES's Delaware and DJ Basin operating areas [1]
  3. Carbon capture pilots and compressor station electrification positioning WES for the energy transition while reducing methane emissions and operating costs [4]
Key Risks
  1. Throughput decline guidance — low-to-mid single-digit crude/NGL decline and mid-to-high single-digit DJ Basin volume decline in 2026 threaten the revenue base of a volume-dependent midstream operator [2]
  2. Leverage of 211.8% D/E ($8.8B debt) constrains financial flexibility and amplifies risk during production downturns or sustained high interest rate environments [5]
  3. Occidental Petroleum concentration — a significant portion of WES volumes depend on Oxy's production decisions, and any Oxy divestiture of its WES ownership stake could create unit price overhang [3]
