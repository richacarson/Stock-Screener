Company Profile
Financial Services ·Banks—Diversified ·NYSE ·United States
Wells Fargo & Company is one of the largest U.S. banks with approximately $1.9 trillion in assets, offering retail banking, commercial banking, corporate and investment banking, and wealth management services. Under CEO Charlie Scharf since 2019, the company has resolved the Federal Reserve's asset cap (removed in 2025) and multiple consent orders, positioning it to compete on a 'more level playing field' with JPMorgan and Bank of America for the first time in nearly a decade.

Investment Thesis
Wells Fargo is the most compelling major U.S. bank story for the next 3-5 years because it is starting from a structurally disadvantaged position—the asset cap suppressed loan growth and investment banking for 7 years—and now has permission to compete. With the Fed asset cap removed in 2025 and multiple consent orders resolved, management is guiding to 17-18% ROTCE vs. today's 14.5%[1]. At 11.5x forward earnings with $50B in guided NII, the stock trades at a 30%+ discount to JPMorgan despite similar underlying franchise quality.
The bear case is that Wells Fargo's 7-year suppression created structural customer losses—large corporate clients and wealth management clients who left during the consent order period may not return regardless of regulatory cleanup. The 3 consecutive years of dividend growth (post-2020 reset) is a thin foundation for income investors compared to JPM's 14-year streak. Consumer banking faces fintech competition in the exact segments (personal loans, payments, savings) where Wells Fargo's brand was most damaged. If the Fed initiates more rate cuts than the guided 2-3 in 2026, NII compression could prevent the ROTCE improvement that justifies the current valuation.
Key Catalysts
  1. ROTCE improvement from 14.5% toward the 17-18% medium-term target as asset cap removal enables loan growth and consent order resolutions reduce compliance costs
  2. Corporate and investment banking wallet share gains with institutional clients previously forced to primary bank with JPM during asset cap period
  3. AI efficiency initiatives delivering $2.4B in gross expense reductions in 2026, supporting operating leverage as revenue grows
Key Risks
  1. Remaining consent orders (still active) creating compliance culture that limits innovation agility and management bandwidth for growth initiatives
  2. Credit quality deterioration if mid-single-digit loan growth extends into riskier commercial real estate or consumer segments during economic softening
  3. Federal Reserve rate cuts exceeding the 2-3 assumed in NII guidance, compressing the ~$50B NII projection materially
